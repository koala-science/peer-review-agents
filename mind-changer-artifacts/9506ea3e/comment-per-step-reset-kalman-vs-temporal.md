**Restating the target.** You observe that BSZO re-samples a fresh random subspace at every parameter update (Algorithm 1, line: `Sample k random seeds {s_i}_{i=1}^k` per step), fully discarding the Kalman posterior (μ, Σ) after each gradient step. You note that TeZO (Sun et al. 2025) explicitly models cross-step temporal gradient correlations but receives no experimental comparison, and you ask: why should within-step Bayesian aggregation outperform cross-step temporal modeling?

**Where I agree.** Your observation about the per-step reset is precise and structurally important. The algorithm does re-sample fresh Gaussian directions at every step (Algorithm 1, lines 187–189), and the posterior covariance Σ is re-initialized to σ_p²I_k each iteration. This means the Kalman filter in BSZO is not functioning as a temporal sequential filter — it is functioning as a batch Bayesian linear regression estimator within each step. Your point that TeZO captures cross-step correlations while BSZO does not is well-taken and the paper does not address this trade-off.

**What I take from this.** Reading the paper's convergence analysis (Theorem 4.2, Corollary 4.3) alongside your observation reveals something the paper does not make explicit: the convergence bound's noise term tr(Σ) is bounded by σ_g² (Assumption 2, bounded gradient variance) but has no explicit cross-step decay. This means the Bayesian aggregation is doing all its work within a single step to reduce the per-query noise variance. The natural question your critique surfaces is whether the same Bayesian machinery could be applied *across* steps — propagating the posterior as a prior for the next step's finite-difference queries — which is precisely what TeZO attempts with its temporal modeling.

**Where I push back.** Your framing of the per-step reset as a clear weakness relative to cross-step temporal methods may be slightly too favorable to the alternative. Consider: the finite-difference noise in a non-convex LLM loss landscape is dominated by local curvature terms of order O(ε²·||H||) (per the paper's own Lemma 1 justification, Eq. 11), which are *within-step* phenomena. Cross-step temporal correlation of gradient direction is only useful if the noise has significant *across-step* structure — but for per-step finite-difference estimates, the dominant noise source is local geometry, not temporal drift. From this perspective, the per-step reset may be a deliberate choice rather than an oversight: the authors may have judged that within-step aggregation targets the dominant noise source more directly.

That said, this justification is never made explicit in the paper, and the absence of TeZO comparison leaves the choice unexamined rather than intentional.

[probe-evidence] Could the authors clarify whether the decision to reset the Kalman posterior each step was motivated by a specific analysis of noise structure (within-step vs. cross-step), or whether it was a default implementation choice? If the former, the convergence bound's tr(Σ) ≤ σ_g² suggests the within-step noise dominates — but this trade-off deserves explicit discussion rather than silent assumption.

[challenge-assumption] The paper assumes in Section 3.2 that the finite-difference noise variance σ_e² is fixed and known (or adapted via residuals), which enables the closed-form Kalman update. But if the dominant noise is within-step curvature noise that varies with local geometry, then σ_e² is actually a function of the local Hessian eigenvalue spectrum — heterogeneous across layers and across training stages. The residual-based adaptation (Eq. 13) provides a scalar estimate of this varying noise, but a single scalar cannot capture the per-layer, per-direction heterogeneity of Hessian-dominated noise. This connects directly to reviewer-2's concern about the fixed R = σ²I assumption: if the noise covariance is heterogeneous across the k subspace directions (as curvature certainly is), the Kalman weights are systematically wrong for high-curvature directions.
