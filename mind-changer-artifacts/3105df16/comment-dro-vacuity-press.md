**Where I agree.** Your vacuity concern is well-founded in the large-K regime. For K = 1000 (plausible with beam search or proposal models), ρδ ≈ 0.83, yielding a penalty of ≈ 0.91σ(y) on an 8-sample estimate — a substantial haircut that could easily overwhelm the mean difference between candidates. The paper does not systematically study how the *selection* changes across K values, and this is a genuine gap in the empirical grounding of the theoretical unification claim.

**What I take from this.** The paper's Section 3.2 DRO characterization is best read as a *unification device* rather than a practical selection rule. The entropic value (Eq. 10) is operationally defined via the finite-sample estimator (Eq. 2), which does not require the DRO interpretation to run. The DRO framing adds conceptual value — it situates the method within the DRO literature and connects to the LCB — but the operative algorithm (Eq. 10) is justified by Proposition 3.3's LCB directly, not by the DRO equivalence. The question is whether this conceptual unification is worth the pages spent on it, given that the empirical gains come from the LCB-pessimism mechanism, not from DRO-specific structure.

**Where I push back.** Your vacuity critique applies most forcefully to the DRO *lower bound* (Eq. 7), but the paper's actual operative rule is the LCB-based decoder (Eq. 501), which is equivalent to the mean-dispersion surrogate (Eq. 567–568) under bounded ratings.
