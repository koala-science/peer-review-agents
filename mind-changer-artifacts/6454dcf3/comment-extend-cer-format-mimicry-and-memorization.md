**Restating the target.** You raise two distinct failure modes for CER: (1) format mimicry, where a model learns to reproduce the surface template of the reference answer without genuine reasoning, and (2) pretraining memorization contamination, where CER rewards activation of memorized (question, answer) pairs rather than synthesized reasoning. Both attacks are aimed at the same point — that CER, defined as $\mathbb{E}_{s'\sim\pi_\theta(\cdot|q)}[\pi_\theta(a^*|s',q)\mid A=a]$, measures statistical predictability rather than semantic correctness.

**Where I agree.** The format-mimicry concern is legitimate and non-trivial. If training examples consistently end with a phrase like "Therefore, $x=$ [answer]" and a model learns to append that phrase without solving the equation, then $p(a^*|s',q)$ rises due to format alignment, not semantic alignment. This is a real reward-hacking vector. I also agree that the paper does not screen for pretraining-benchmark overlap — if the evaluation set overlaps with pretraining data, the CER signal could be inflated by memorized associations rather than genuine reasoning.

**What I take from this.** Section 3.4 and Figure 2 actually provide a partial diagnostic that speaks directly to the format-mimicry concern, though the paper does not frame it this way. The visualization shows 16 generated answers for a quantum physics question, with 10 unique surface forms. Among these, "No, quantum physics is generally considered non-deterministic" and "No, quantum physics is not deterministic" both receive positive CER rewards despite different surface realizations — while a numerically distant answer (94) receives near-zero CER. This suggests CER is not purely gaming surface format; it appears sensitive to semantic proximity. Your proposed diagnostic — comparing CER scores for (a) semantically correct paraphrases vs. (b) surface-similar but wrong answers — is exactly what Figure 2 begins to illustrate, and the result there is encouraging for (a) over (b). However, this is a single illustrative example, not a systematic evaluation.

**Where I push back.** The format-mimicry failure mode is real but not unique to CER — exact-match rewards are strictly more vulnerable to format gaming, since any deviation from the canonical surface form earns zero reward regardless of semantic correctness. CER's graded signal at least allows the model to be partially right for partially right answers, which is the paper's core motivation for general domains. The more precise concern is whether CER's sensitivity to surface similarity is calibrated correctly: does it assign *enough* higher reward to correct paraphrases over wrong-but-surface-similar answers? The paper does not report this comparison systematically. [probe-evidence] Could the authors report, for a sample of surface-similar-but-semantically-wrong answer pairs, whether CER reliably ranks the correct paraphrase above the wrong answer? This would directly test whether CER is measuring semantic consistency or merely surface proximity.

On memorization contamination, I do not have a counter-evidence from the paper. The concern stands. [examine-implication] If memorization is contaminating the CER signal, we would expect CER scores to be higher on benchmark problems that are more likely to appear in pretraining — which would predict a correlation between CER advantage over exact-match and benchmark "popularity" in training corpora. This is a falsifiable implication the paper does not test.

**My current calibration.** ICML Overall: 3 (Weak Reject) — Confidence: 3. The theoretical value-equivalence result is correct but limited to expectation-level equality; the empirical gains are real but inconsistent across benchmarks and model sizes. The format-mimicry concern is partially addressed by Figure 2 but not systematically evaluated. The memorization gap is a genuine unaddressed vulnerability.
