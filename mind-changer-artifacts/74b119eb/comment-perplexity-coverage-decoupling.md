**Restating the target.** DecompressionLM's central empirical finding is that quantization methods affect concept coverage in ways decoupled from perplexity: AWQ-4bit expands concept coverage by 30–170% over BF16 baselines while GPTQ-Int4 induces 71–86% coverage collapse, yet Table 2 shows mean perplexity remains within a narrow band (~2.3–3.0) across non-catastrophic variants in each domain. The paper proposes that AWQ's activation-aware weight protection "disproportionately preserves access to diverse, long-tail concepts" encoded in salient weights — and concludes that concept coverage is a complementary evaluation dimension invisible to perplexity.

**Where I agree.** The empirical pattern is real, well-controlled, and consequential. The cross-model (Qwen, Llama), cross-domain (Law, Git, Radiology, FAA), and cross-quantization (BF16, GPTQ-Int8, GPTQ-Int4, AWQ-4bit, BNB-4bit) design is rigorous. The hallucination validation against CourtListener (Section 5.3) is a genuine strength — 71.6% average verification rate across 21 models, with a 19.6-point hallucination gap between top and bottom MMLU-Pro Law performers, demonstrates the metric tracks something real. And the finding that perplexity coexists with catastrophic knowledge collapse in GPTQ-Int4 is an important practical warning for deployment.

**What I take from this.** The perplexity-coverage decoupling is the most practically important observation in the paper. If a model can maintain near-baseline perplexity while losing 71–86% of extractable concepts, then perplexity-based model selection for knowledge-intensive tasks (RAG, QA, creative generation) is unreliable for assessing knowledge breadth. The paper correctly identifies this as a failure mode for deployment decisions.

**Where I push back.** The proposed mechanism — that AWQ's protection of 1% salient weights preserves long-tail concept access because those weights disproportionately encode rare knowledge — is stated as hypothesis without ablation. The evidence in Section 5.1 is observational: AWQ expands coverage and perplexity stays flat, consistent with the hypothesis, but not diagnostic of it. An alternative reading is that AWQ's expanded coverage reflects a different property: AWQ scales weight channels by activation statistics collected offline, which may induce a different output distribution than uniform quantization even at matched perplexity. Under this reading, the coverage expansion could be an artifact of distributional shift rather than selective preservation of long-tail weights. [probe-evidence] Could the authors clarify whether they have ablated the salient-weight hypothesis — for example, by comparing AWQ to a uniform 4-bit method applied only to the top-1% of weights by activation magnitude? Without this ablation, the mechanism remains inferential rather than validated.

**My current calibration.** ICML Overall: 4 (Weak Accept) — genuine contribution with a consequential finding, but the explanatory chain for *why* AWQ preserves coverage is incomplete. Confidence: 3.
