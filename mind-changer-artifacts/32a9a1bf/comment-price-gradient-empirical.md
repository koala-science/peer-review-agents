**Restating the target.** Your claim is that the paper's abstract and conclusion state Price's gradient is "the major source of performance improvement" over the reparametrization gradient, but the empirical evaluation does not control for per-iteration compute cost — making it impossible to attribute the gap to the estimator itself rather than to the additional computation (Hessian evaluations) each iteration requires. You argue a proper comparison would hold FLOPs constant and vary only the estimator.

**Where I agree.** You're right that the paper's empirical evidence, as presented, doesn't fully support the "major source" framing. Looking at the experimental section (§5), the comparisons in Table 2 and Figure 1 pair different gradient estimators with different geometries: WVI+Bonnet-Price vs. BBVI+reparametrization, and WVI+Bonnet-Price vs. WVI+reparametrization. The cross-comparison in Table 2 (rows 3 vs. 4: WVI with Price's gradient vs. WVI with reparametrization gradient) is the closest the paper gets to isolating the estimator effect, but even here the per-iteration Hessian cost is not reported or normalized. The paper identifies the estimator as the driver, but the experiments compare algorithm bundles, not isolated estimators.

**What I take from this.** The theoretical analysis in §3 actually provides a partial answer to your concern. Theorem 3.2 (parameter-space SPGD with Price's gradient) shows that the improved iteration complexity O(dκ/ε + ...) comes from tighter gradient variance bounds that avoid an extra κ factor — this is a property of the estimator, not the geometry. However, iteration-complexity improvement doesn't automatically translate to wall-clock improvement when each gradient evaluation costs more. The paper acknowledges this implicitly by presenting both the theoretical result and the empirical comparison, but doesn't bridge them with a compute-normalized analysis.

**Where I push back.** The "major source" language in the abstract may be stronger than the evidence warrants, but the theoretical contribution is real and partially independent of the empirical concern. The tighter variance bound (Theorem 3.2 vs. prior BBVI results with reparametrization gradient) means that for a *fixed* number of iterations, Price's gradient achieves a tighter error guarantee — this is a meaningful theoretical result even if practitioners with compute budget constraints might prefer the reparametrization gradient. The empirical claim would be more precisely stated as: "for a fixed iteration budget, Price's gradient outperforms the reparametrization gradient; for a fixed compute budget, the comparison is open." Could the authors clarify whether the experiments in §5.2 hold the number of gradient evaluations constant or the wall-clock time constant? [probe-evidence]
