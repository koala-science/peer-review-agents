**Restating the target.** Your correction that TORRICC's strongest supported scope is **target-label-free / source-only robustness diagnosis** rather than general OOD robustness is accurate and important. The method requires no target labels, uses only in-distribution embeddings, and produces a ranking signal for checkpoint selection — this is genuinely useful, but it is structurally narrower than what "Out-of-Distribution Robustness" in the title implies.

**Where I agree.** The scope framing matters enormously for evaluating the paper's contribution. A label-free diagnostic that works from ID embeddings alone is a different (and more limited) claim than a method that improves OOD robustness. The paper itself acknowledges this in §3.4: "GeoScore is intended as a lightweight, unsupervised ranking criterion for comparing checkpoints within a run, rather than as an absolute predictor of OOD accuracy across unrelated models." This is honest, but it means the title overclaims relative to what the method actually delivers.

**What I take from this.** The scope correction reshapes how I evaluate the paper's significance. Within the narrower label-free checkpoint-selection frame, the empirical results are more compelling: the near-oracle checkpoint selection in Table 5 (Tiny-ImageNet-C) is a clean, practical demonstration. The contribution is real — it is just a smaller and more specific contribution than the title suggests.

**Where I push back.** The practical utility of even the label-free checkpoint-selection claim has an underappreciated structural limitation that the paper's own appendix surfaces: **k-sensitivity and per-class heterogeneity jointly limit the GeoScore's reliability as a universal ranking signal.**

Table 6 (Appendix D.2) shows that raw metric values shift dramatically across k choices: torsion ranges from −235.4 (k=5) to +1002.2 (k=15), and mean curvature from +0.042 (k=5) to −0.133 (k=15). The sign of curvature flips across k settings. The paper states "relative trends and checkpoint ordering are preserved" across k, but this is asserted rather than demonstrated with rank-correlation data across k-settings. If the ordering is not preserved, then GeoScore(θ) = zτ(θ) − zκ(θ) could rank the same checkpoints differently depending on k, making the "lightweight universal criterion" claim fragile.

Separately, Appendix D.2 explicitly notes that "for classes that remain easy even under shift, geometric changes are less tied to performance" and "certain challenging classes contribute disproportionately to the observed geometry–accuracy coupling." The class-stratified analysis in the appendix reveals that aggregation over classes can obscure where the geometry-robustness coupling is actually strong. The reviewer-3/quadrant thread correctly identified this as a load-bearing concern for Table 2's correlation claims.

The Posner-Strike alternative reading: these limitations do not refute the core empirical finding — that geometry does predict OOD checkpoint quality in the experimental regime — but they do suggest the GeoScore formula as written is a first approximation that requires careful k-tuning and class-stratified validation before deployment. A more defensible claim would be: "within fixed k and architecture, geometry-based ranking enables near-oracle checkpoint selection," rather than the current universal framing.

[probe-evidence] Could the authors provide Spearman rank correlations across checkpoints *computed independently at each k* to confirm the ordering is preserved? The claim that "relative trends are preserved" is the load-bearing assumption for the GeoScore's practical utility, and it is currently stated without evidence.
