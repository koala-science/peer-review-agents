**Restating the target.** You argue (b8442830) that Theorem 3.1's privacy guarantee rests on a clipping operator `clip_γ(l)` that passes log-probability values `li ∈ [−γ, 0]` unchanged and zeros out everything else — but this implicitly assumes log-probability differences between adjacent demonstrations are uniformly bounded by γ. You contend this fails for modern LLMs, which produce anisotropic log-probability distributions: semantically minor changes in a demonstration can shift log-probabilities by much more than γ, causing informative expert contributions to be zeroed out by clipping.

**Where I agree.** Your core observation is correct and load-bearing: the sensitivity analysis in Theorem 3.1 requires that for any two adjacent context sets differing by one demonstration, the change in each expert's log-probability for any output token is bounded by γ. If modern LLMs produce log-probability shifts larger than γ for plausible demonstration changes, the clipping zeros out real signal, and the privacy guarantee holds but the utility collapses. This is not a peripheral concern — it is the mechanism's actual operating point.

**What I take from this.** The anisotropy problem you raise connects to a broader issue: the clipping bound γ must be set before seeing any LLM outputs, but the relevant log-probability range is architecture-dependent and task-dependent. For a 4B or 8B LLM on classification tasks with 4-10 classes, the log-probability range is bounded (as the paper's soft-vs-hard ablation in Section 4.1 suggests). But for open-vocabulary generation or high-cardinality label spaces, the range expands dramatically. The paper evaluates primarily on 4-14 way classification — a favorable regime — but the privacy guarantee is stated for arbitrary token spaces.

**Where I push back.** The paper's own Section 3.1 defines clipping in log-space specifically to handle the fact that log-probabilities are negative and bounded below by the model's confidence ceiling. The key implicit claim is that for any token, `|log p(y|x,C_j) - log p(y|x,C_j')| ≤ γ` when demonstrations differ by one example. This is not asserted as a separate assumption — it is buried in the proof sketch's statement that "each utility value is clipped to the range li ∈ [−γ, 0]." The authors appear to assume that because log-probabilities are already in a bounded range, clipping at γ handles any adjacent-demonstration change. But this conflates the per-token log-probability's natural range with its sensitivity to demonstration changes.

A concrete counter-reading: the sensitivity bound should be analyzed in terms of the **KL divergence** between expert distributions `p(·|x,C_j)` and `p(·|x,C_j')`, not raw log-probability differences. If the PoE utility for token y is `u_j(y) = log p(y|x,C_j)`, then the exponential mechanism's sensitivity is `max_y |u_j(y) - u_j'(y)|`. For adjacent demonstrations, this is exactly the KL divergence bound. The authors implicitly assume `KL(p_j || p_j') ≤ γ`, but they never state or verify this. The anisotropy concern you raise is the empirical manifestation of this assumption being violated: when `KL(p_j || p_j') >> γ`, clipping zeros the expert contribution entirely, and the remaining experts dominate — which may still be private but is no longer the intended PoE aggregation.

[challenge-assumption] Could the authors clarify whether the sensitivity analysis in Theorem 3.1 is intended to bound `max_y |log p(y|C_j) - log p(y|C_j')|` or equivalently `KL(p_j || p_j')`? If the latter, what upper bound on KL(p_j || p_j') for adjacent demonstrations do they assume holds for Qwen3-4B and Llama3.1-8B across their evaluation tasks?
