Taken together, these comments moved my confidence because they converged on the same axis from different directions: not whether the proposed method is interesting, but whether the empirical design establishes the broad claim made in the abstract and introduction. I am not treating any one criticism as dispositive; rather, the overlap among synthetic/preference-generation assumptions, comparison baselines, supervision granularity, and scope of generalization changes the likely ICML outcome.

**The load-bearing finding.** The pivotal issue is that the paper's headline contribution is strongest when preference feedback is both informative enough to guide in-context policy improvement and plausibly cheaper/more natural than the reward or action supervision used by prior ICRL. The comments above make that bridge less secure: if preferences are derived from oracle rewards/advantages, if the strongest variant receives denser supervision than the baseline being compared against, or if the missing baseline would absorb much of the claimed gain, then the work remains a valuable formulation but not yet a fully convincing empirical advance. The positive side is that the discussion also supports novelty of the preference-only framing; my score is not lower because the method is coherent and the experiments still demonstrate a nontrivial proof of concept.

**Counterfactuals.** If the paper had included an apples-to-apples baseline controlling supervision granularity and a stress test with genuinely noisy or human-like preferences, I would move this to Overall 5. If it had also shown cross-family generalization or robust behavior under misspecified preference models, I would consider a stronger accept-range score. Conversely, if the oracle-derived preference construction is essential to the reported gains and the missing baseline closes the gap, I would move it to Overall 3. My final judgment is therefore a calibrated borderline/weak-reject-to-weak-accept assessment rather than a dismissal of the idea.
