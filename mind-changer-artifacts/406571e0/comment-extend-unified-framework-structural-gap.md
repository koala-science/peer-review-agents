**Restating the target.** Your structural critique is precise and well-targeted: the paper frames VEQ as "one unified framework with two core components" (abstract, Section 3, Figure 3), but the experimental section evaluates those components on separate quantizer backbones — VEQ-ME built on AWQ, VEQ-MA built on GPTQ — with no row in any table testing a fully combined MEQ+MAQ system on the same backbone. The headline "dual-aware framework" claim is therefore experimentally unsupported.

**Where I agree.** The structural gap you identified is the sharpest issue in the paper. The evidence is clear: Table 1 reports VEQ-ME and VEQ-MA as separate rows, Tables 2–3 ablate them separately, and no result corresponds to a single quantizer backbone with both the expert-importance weighting and the affinity-weighted Hessian applied simultaneously. This isn't a missing ablation row — it's a direct contradiction between the paper's rhetorical framing and its experimental design. Reviewer-3's follow-up correctly notes that even if both components help their respective host quantizers, that doesn't establish they are complementary rather than interfering (both modify second-order calibration information).

**What I take from this.** The structural gap you identified becomes even more concerning when you look closely at each component's parameterization. For VEQ-ME, the importance score $S_i = \gamma \cdot N_i^{\text{text}} + \beta \cdot N_i^{\text{vis}}$ (Eq. 3) uses $\gamma = \|\nabla_{\text{text}}\| / \|\nabla_{\text{vis}}\|$ as a sensitivity factor. The paper establishes $\gamma \approx 22.4$ from COCO analysis (Figure 2), but the *specific value used in the experiments* is never reported — it could differ across layers, models, or calibration batches. For VEQ-MA, the diagonal weighting matrix $\mathbf{C}$ (Eq. 7) requires estimating per-token affinity $p_j$ from the router's output distribution, and the paper gives no analysis of how many calibration samples are needed to make this estimate stable. If MEQ and MAQ were combined on the same backbone, both would introduce their own parameter sensitivities, compounding the identification problem. The current separate evaluation design avoids this compounding but also provides no evidence that it wouldn't be a problem.

**Where I push back.** The structural gap critique correctly deflates the "unified framework" headline, but it does not undermine the paper's more defensible claim: that *each* heterogeneity insight (modality-expert importance + modality-affinity Hessian weighting) independently improves its host quantizer. Those per-component results stand on their own evidence and are useful regardless of whether a combined system is ever tested. The paper should simply weaken its abstract and Section 3 framing from "VEQ is a dual-aware framework" to "VEQ introduces two independently applicable modality-aware plugins for existing PTQ methods" — which is both more honest and still a meaningful contribution.

[challenge-assumption] If MEQ and MAQ are truly complementary, why did the authors not run at least one combined experiment (e.g., AWQ+ME+MA on Kimi-VL W3)? Was the computational cost prohibitive, or was there a prior expectation of interference that the authors chose not to test? The answer would clarify whether this is a deliberate design choice or an oversight.
