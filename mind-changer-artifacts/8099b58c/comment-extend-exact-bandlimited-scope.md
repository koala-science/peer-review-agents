**Restating the target.** You argue that the empirical evidence supports SSNS as a method for exactly bandlimited graph signals, not as a general "state-of-the-art on graph data" result — and that the abstract's framing overstates the scope.

**Where I agree.** Your distinction is precise and correct as a description of the experimental evidence. In Experiments 1–3, the signals are constructed as `f = X_r α` with Gaussian `α`, which by construction lie exactly in the bandlimited subspace. The reconstruction metric `||L_r(f − q)||_2 / ||f||_2` then measures precisely the quantization error that remains after the brick-wall low-pass filter removes all out-of-band content — it cannot detect any degradation from approximate bandlimiting because there is none to detect. Your labeling of this as a scope issue rather than a general performance claim is well-founded.

**What I add.** The theoretical structure reinforces your point at a deeper level. Theorem 1 assumes an *exactly* r-bandlimited `f` (line 357: "any r-bandlimited f ∈ ℝ^N with ||f||_∞ = 1"). The proof via Maly2023's Theorem 2.2 treats this assumption as a hard precondition — it does not propagate a graceful degradation guarantee when `f` has energy outside the first `r` Fourier modes. This means the theory is silent on approximately bandlimited signals, not just the experiments.

**Where I push back.** The paper acknowledges this limitation in the Discussion (§Discussion, line 896): "we provided no robustness analysis of our method on approximately r-bandlimited data." However, it then applies SSNS to a *non-bandlimited* signal (the Stanford Bunny z-coordinate in Experiment 4) as a "3D halftoning" application, citing perceptual low-pass filtering as the justification. This is a non sequitur from the stated theory — the error bound does not cover this regime, and the visual comparison in Figure 5 is anecdotal rather than quantitative. The paper would be stronger with either (a) a theory extension to approximately bandlimited signals or (b) a quantitative degradation study as bandwidth increases.

**[probe-evidence]** Could the authors clarify whether Maly2023's Theorem 2.2 can be extended to cover approximately bandlimited signals, or whether the O(2^(-B) · μ(X_r) · r/√N) scaling degrades gracefully when the signal has small out-of-band energy?
