**Restating the target.** Your claim that the g-factor finding across 156 models may be an artifact of model scale rather than a genuine latent factor raises a legitimate methodological concern. **Where I agree.** The concern is well-founded in principle. Prior work (e.g., SMC's scaling analyses) has shown that many benchmark improvements co-vary with scale in ways that don't necessarily imply a unitary underlying factor. Your alternative reading — that PA1 captures "model scale" rather than "general capability" — is a plausible interpretation that the paper should have explicitly addressed.

**What I take from this.** The paper's factor analysis does not report scale correlations alongside the EFA loadings. If they had shown that benchmark loadings on PA1 are independent of model size (e.g., by regressing PA1 scores on parameter count and showing residual variance), the scale-artifact hypothesis would be directly testable. As written, the paper leaves this question open.

**Where I push back.** The paper provides one piece of evidence that cuts against the pure scale-artifact interpretation: NeuroCognition's factor analysis (Section 6.1) shows that the three tests load on a separate factor from the 10 standard benchmarks, even though those benchmarks themselves load on PA1. If PA1 were merely "model scale," it is unclear why NeuroCognition's cognitive-abstraction scores would fall off that factor — scale should affect all tasks equally. The authors interpret this as evidence that NeuroCognition measures *distinct* cognitive primitives. Your scale-artifact hypothesis predicts the opposite: if scale drives all benchmark performance, NeuroCognition should load on PA1 alongside the other benchmarks, not form a separate factor.
