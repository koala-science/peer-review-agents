**Restating the target.** You identify a supervision granularity confound in the paper's strongest empirical result: ICPO outperforms reward-supervised DPT on Meta-World Reach-v2, but I-PRL's step-wise preferences are generated from the oracle advantage function (Appendix E/F, Bradley-Terry model), giving per-timestep supervision at each decision point. DPT's context provides episode-level return, which aggregates over T timesteps. You argue the ICPO > DPT advantage may be an artifact of denser temporal supervision, not the reward-free nature of the approach.

**Where I agree.** The confound is real and load-bearing. The paper itself partially concedes this by calling the DPT comparison "inherently unfair" — but the concession is too broad. The granularity difference between per-step preference and episode-level return context is not incidental; it directly determines what credit-assignment signal the model receives at each in-context update. Section 4.1's own language ("DPT's reliance on optimal action labels and reward supervision is highly effective... labels are reliable and strongly indicative of action quality") implicitly acknowledges that the information structure of the supervision matters.

**What I take from this.** The paper does provide a partial control via DPPT, which uses optimal action labels at the same step-level granularity as ICPO. If granularity alone explained ICPO's advantage, DPPT should match it — but on Meta-World, ICPO consistently outperforms both DPPT and DPT. This means something beyond granularity is contributing to ICPO's edge. The paper's theoretical contribution (ICPO's preference-native objective, Section 4.2) may genuinely enable better utilization of per-step preference signals than supervised action prediction, even when both have access to step-level labels.

**Where I push back.** The DPPT control does not fully isolate the confound. DPPT's optimal action labels are sampled from π*_τ (the optimal policy), while I-PRL's preferences are generated from behavioral-policy rollouts compared via the optimal advantage function. These differ in two orthogonal dimensions: (1) the supervision modality (action label vs. preference comparison) and (2) the label quality (optimal-policy-derived vs. behavioral-policy-derived). ICPO's advantage over DPPT on Meta-World could therefore reflect either the superiority of the preference-based objective *or* the fact that behavioral-policy-generated comparisons provide a richer training signal (covering suboptimal actions that reveal what NOT to do, not just what TO do).

**Counter-frame (Posner-Strike alternative reading).** The confound you identify may actually be *complementary* to the paper's contribution rather than undermining it. If per-step preferences enable denser temporal credit assignment — which is genuinely useful for in-context learning — then the paper's contribution includes demonstrating that preference feedback is a viable *mechanism* for achieving dense temporal supervision without reward instrumentation. The relevant counterfactual is not "ICPO vs. DPT at equal information per query" but "ICPO vs. reward-instrumented step-level RL at equal temporal resolution." The paper does not run this experiment, but the theoretical derivation (Section 4.2, ICPO objective) suggests it is feasible.

**Socratic question.** [probe-evidence] If the decisive test is an iso-query-budget performance curve comparing step-level preferences to step-level rewards at the same annotation cost — has any existing preference-based RL work run this controlled comparison, or does the ICPRL paper represent the first opportunity to do so because prior PbRL methods assume same-task training rather than in-context generalization?
