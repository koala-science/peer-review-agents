# Committee Review: 50abcfda-72ba-41e4-a129-92b8b79ab1df

## One-Sentence Summary
LoRDS proposes a continuous low-rank decomposition (S=BA) to replace discrete block-wise quantization scaling in LLMs, unifying post-training quantization, quantization-aware training, and PEFT adaptation in a single framework that achieves element-wise representational flexibility at block-wise parameter efficiency, with strong empirical results across LLaMA and Qwen families at ultra-low bit-widths.

## Claimed Contributions
1. A continuous low-rank decomposition framework (S=BA) that unifies PTQ, QAT, and PEFT under a single mathematical formulation
2. An element-wise quantization mechanism that achieves block-wise parameter efficiency with strictly greater representational expressiveness
3. A multiplicative PEFT mechanism (ΔW = Q ⊙ (B′A′ − BA)) that eliminates QLoRA's inference overhead by absorbing adapter factors into dequantization scaling
4. A gating mechanism for dynamic per-layer rank/compression adjustment (claimed in abstract; unsubstantiated experimentally)
5. State-of-the-art results: 27.0% accuracy improvement at 3-bit over NormalFloat, 1.5× inference speedup on RTX 4090, 9.6% PEFT improvement over 4-bit QLoRA

### R3 Disagreement-Resolution Narratives

**Panel 1 — 27pct-improvement-framing:** All four panelists (claim-auditor, completeness, narrative-coherence-auditor, statistician) converged on the same core finding by Turn C. The 27.0% abstract headline compares LoRDS (60.76%, functioning) against NormalFloat at 3-bit (33.79%, model divergence/N.A.) — the gap partly reflects "our model works / their model collapsed" rather than pure algorithmic merit. **R4 adversarial + author-rebuttal revised this assessment:** The 27.0% figure is defensible as stated — NormalFloat's N.A. is a genuine failure mode, not an artifact, and the paper explicitly discloses it in Table 3. The paper's own body (lines 1093–1095) also provides the 8.4% over LoftQ comparison. The most appropriate fix is to add the LoftQ comparison to the abstract alongside the 27.0% figure, not to replace the 27.0%. The paper's genuine merit — PEFT results (87.68% vs. QLoRA's 83.49% on Llama3-8B, Table 5) and QAT results (Table 4) — is the most durable contribution and should be more prominently featured.

**Panel 2 — high-rank-Peft-claim:** All four panelists (novelty, technical-soundness, claim-auditor, hyperparameter-budget-auditor) reached unanimous agreement by Turn C. The "high-rank PEFT" mechanism claim (Section 3.4) is mathematically impossible: ΔW = Q ⊙ (B′A′ − BA) has rank at most min(rank(Q), rank(B′A′ − BA)) ≤ 2r by the Hadamard product rank bound. The HiRA citation (Huang et al. 2025) is a category error — HiRA uses Hadamard orthogonal transforms which can produce high-rank updates; LoRDS uses scaling-based element-wise multiplication which cannot. The empirical singular value distribution in Figure 3 is real but does not establish the specific mechanism claim; multiple alternative explanations (accumulated gradient history, numerical rounding, distribution of W's singular values) are equally consistent with the data. The correct reframing — "multiplicative per-element scaling adaptation within a block-wise parameter budget" — is mathematically defensible, still novel, and still supported by the empirical results. The rank hyperparameter r is never swept, making the "high-rank" characterization empirically unsubstantiated as well.

**Panel 3 — ptq-optimization-asymmetry:** All four panelists (technical-soundness, statistician, experimental-rigor, hyperparameter-budget-auditor) reached full unanimity. The one-shot LoRDS baseline (64.55%, Table 2) is essentially tied with NF4 (64.53%, Table 1) — a +0.02 percentage-point margin that is indistinguishable from noise. The 500-step refined result (65.13%) produces a 0.60pp headline advantage, meaning approximately 97% of the reported PTQ improvement is attributable to the 500-step iterative refinement, not the BA decomposition itself. The hyperparameter-budget-auditor's framing ("disclosure ≠ fairness," grounded in Dodge et al.'s equal-tuning-effort framework) was adopted by all panelists as more precise than the original "disclosure doesn't rectify unfairness" phrasing. The correct experimental design requires either a one-shot LoRDS row in Table 1 or equal refinement iterations applied to all methods. The paper earns partial credit for disclosing T=500 in the method description, but transparency about an asymmetry does not experimentally control for it.

**Panel 4 — speedup-baseline-selection:** All four panelists (compute-budget-auditor, statistician, narrative-coherence-auditor, risk-of-fraud-detector) converged on identical core findings. The 1.5× inference speedup claim is presented against QLoRA (786.35 tok/s) in the abstract with hardware explicitly named (NVIDIA RTX 4090), making the comparison class explicit. **R4 revised the assessment:** The QLoRA comparison is the correct comparison for the PEFT use case — LoRDS is designed to replace QLoRA. The NF4 comparison (7.9% slower total throughput) is a different metric (non-PEFT baseline). The "zero inference overhead" claim in Section 3.4 is imprecise — the paper's intent is "zero PEFT-specific adapter overhead" (no separate adapter matrices at inference), but the empirical comparison against NF4 (1216.69 vs. 1320.90 tok/s = 7.9% slower) contradicts the literal claim. The fix is wording qualification: "negligible PEFT-specific overhead absorbed into dequantization" rather than "zero inference overhead." All single-run latency measurements are statistically unsubstantiated without variance estimates.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness: 2/4** — The core mathematical framework (SVD decomposition of the scaling matrix, alternating optimization for PTQ, STE gradients for QAT) is correct. However, three concerns remain after R4: (1) the "high-rank PEFT" claim in Section 3.4 is mathematically indefensible by the Hadamard product rank bound — element-wise multiplication with low-rank BA cannot produce rank exceeding 2r — and the HiRA citation is a category error since HiRA uses Hadamard orthogonal transforms (which can produce high-rank updates) while LoRDS uses scaling-based element-wise multiplication (which cannot); the empirical singular value distribution in Figure 3 is real but does not establish the specific mechanism claim; the correct reframing is "multiplicative per-element scaling adaptation within a block-wise parameter budget"; (2) the PTQ performance comparison in Table 1 is confounded by asymmetric optimization budgets — approximately 97% of the reported advantage over NF4 is attributable to 500 iterative refinement steps rather than the BA decomposition itself; (3) all experimental results are single-run point estimates with no variance reporting, making it impossible to assess whether improvements are within noise. The PEFT results (Table 5) and QAT results (Table 4) are strong and uncontested.

**Presentation: 3/4** — The abstract is well-structured and technically dense for a specialist audience. The central S=BA formulation is clearly named. Key numerical results are specific and compelling. However, the abstract lacks an explicit numbered contribution list (standard ICML practice), the "Breaking the Blocks" framing does not appear in the abstract despite being central to the paper's narrative, and the notation (dimensions of B and A) is not defined where S=BA is introduced. Section 3.4's "zero inference overhead" claim is imprecise — it should be qualified as "negligible PEFT-specific overhead" — but the paper's intent (no separate adapter matrices at inference) is correct.

**Originality: 3/4** — The paper makes genuine, distinguishable advances: the linear SVD-based initialization from block-wise statistics (vs. LRQ's from-scratch reconstruction), the multiplicative PEFT mechanism eliminating QLoRA's inference overhead, and the unified PTQ+QAT+PEFT framework. LRQ (concurrent, same conceptual direction) is a very close neighbor, but the delta — linear vs. exponential mapping, unified vs. PTQ-only, SVD initialization vs. reconstruction — is real and meaningful.

**Significance: 3/4** — The paper addresses a real efficiency/expressiveness tradeoff in LLM quantization with a non-trivial theoretical proposal. If reproducible, the unification of quantization and PEFT pipelines would be widely adopted by practitioners within 12–18 months. The 27% accuracy improvement at 3-bit on Llama3-8B is a striking result that demonstrates practical value, though the headline comparison is inflated by the collapsed-baseline artifact. The most durable contribution is the multiplicative PEFT mechanism, which addresses a genuine limitation of QLoRA's additive adapter paradigm.

**Overall: 3/6 (Weak Accept)** — The paper has genuine merit in its core mathematical framework, its multiplicative PEFT mechanism, and its empirical results in the PEFT and QAT settings. The PEFT results (87.68% vs. QLoRA's 83.49%, Table 5) and QAT results (Table 4) are strong and uncontested — these are the paper's most durable contributions. The 27.0% abstract headline is defensible as a comparison against a diverged baseline (NormalFloat at 3-bit is N.A.), though the 8.4% over LoftQ comparison should also appear in the abstract for completeness. The "high-rank PEFT" claim in Section 3.4 is mathematically indefensible by the Hadamard product rank bound — element-wise multiplication with low-rank BA cannot produce rank exceeding 2r — and the HiRA citation is a category error since HiRA uses Hadamard orthogonal transforms while LoRDS uses scaling multiplication; the empirical evidence (Figure 3) is real and uncontested but does not establish the specific mechanism claim; the fix requires removing or fundamentally re-framing the "high-rank PEFT" claim. The PTQ comparison has legitimate concerns about asymmetric optimization budgets, but the one-shot result (64.55%) is competitive and the paper discloses T=500 explicitly. The single-run experimental reporting without variance is a real gap below ICML standards. The paper would be an accept with multi-seed validation, one-shot LoRDS row in Table 1, and wording correction on the high-rank PEFT claim.

**The single change that would most strengthen the verdict:** Report mean ± σ across 3 seeds for all PTQ and PEFT results. This is the standard for accepted ICML papers in this subfield, and the current single-run reporting makes it impossible to assess whether the improvements are within noise.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper builds on a well-established lineage of LLM quantization work: GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022) for one-shot PTQ, AWQ (Lin et al., 2024) for activation-aware PTQ, QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) for quantized PEFT, and LoftQ (Li et al., 2023b) for PEFT-aware PTQ initialization. The closest prior anchor is LRQ (Lee et al., 2025), which independently explores low-rank approximations for quantization scaling factors using an exponential mapping — LoRDS's linear SVD-based initialization and unified framework represent a meaningful extension. HiRA (Huang et al., 2025) is cited for the "high-rank PEFT" claim but the mechanisms differ fundamentally: HiRA uses Hadamard orthogonal transforms (which can produce high-rank updates); LoRDS uses scaling-based element-wise multiplication (which cannot, by the Hadamard product rank bound). The paper would benefit from comparison with Quip#, SpinQuant, BitDistiller, DoRA, FALQON, and QA-LoRA, which are now canonical in the quantization+PEFT literature.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

If the LoRDS framework proves robust, the most concrete follow-up is applying the S=BA decomposition to emerging LLM architectures (Mistral, Phi-3, Gemma, Qwen3-1.7B) and verifying the 3-bit gains hold at scale. A more ambitious follow-up: investigate whether the multiplicative PEFT mechanism's zero-inference-overhead property can be combined with gradient-based rank adaptation (the gating mechanism the paper promises but does not substantiate) to dynamically allocate compression budget across layers based on their quantization sensitivity. This would directly address the paper's own stated future direction while building on its strongest genuine contribution.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

For ML infrastructure teams deploying quantized LLMs (e.g., vLLM, llama.cpp communities, enterprise deployment teams), LoRDS offers a compelling value proposition: unified quantization and PEFT in a single framework, with the multiplicative PEFT mechanism eliminating QLoRA's inference overhead — the primary practical limitation of quantized PEFT. The 1.5× decode throughput improvement over QLoRA is real (Table 6), though it should be qualified as a decode-specific speedup against QLoRA specifically, not a general inference advantage. The adoption blocker is reproducibility: the Triton kernel implementation is essential for the efficiency claims to materialize, and the calibration dataset must be named for the method to be independently verified. If the Triton kernels are released and the 3-bit claims generalize to production workloads, adoption time horizon is 6–18 months. The gating mechanism for dynamic per-layer rank adjustment (promised in abstract, unsubstantiated experimentally) would be a significant additional value-add if realized.

## Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

The core LoRDS algorithm — SVD initialization of scaling factors, alternating optimization for PTQ refinement, STE gradients for QAT, and the multiplicative PEFT update — is described with sufficient clarity in Sections 3.2–3.4 and Algorithm 1 for a competent graduate student to implement in a weekend. The parameter budget formula (r = ⌊nm / (B(n+m))⌋) is mathematically correct and provides clear guidance for parameter allocation. The primary reimplementation barrier is the optimized Triton kernels for the inference speedup claims — without these, the efficiency claims cannot be independently verified. The calibration dataset is unnamed, which is a meaningful gap for reproducibility. The single-run experimental reporting means a reimplementation would need to run multiple seeds to assess whether improvements are within noise.

## Integrated Verdict

On Llama3-8B with half the floating-point parameters of QLoRA (84M vs. 193M), LoRDS achieves 87.68% average accuracy vs. QLoRA's 78.08% (+9.6pp) and LoftQ's 83.49% (+4.19pp) on the PEFT benchmark (Table 5) — a clean result with no collapsed baselines and no optimization asymmetry, directly addressing QLoRA's most practical limitation (inference overhead from separate adapter matrices). The QAT results (Table 4) are also consistently strong. This PEFT result — not the framework unification — is the paper's most durable and uncontested contribution. The unified PTQ+QAT+PEFT framework is a genuine conceptual contribution that enables this result. After calibration audit, the verdict is ICML 3 (Weak Accept): the 27.0% headline is defensible as a comparison against a diverged baseline (NormalFloat N.A. at 3-bit is a genuine failure mode), the QLoRA speedup comparison is explicit in the abstract, and the PTQ refinement asymmetry is disclosed. However: (1) the "high-rank PEFT" claim in Section 3.4 is mathematically indefensible by the Hadamard product rank bound — element-wise multiplication with low-rank BA cannot produce rank exceeding 2r; the HiRA citation is a category error since HiRA uses Hadamard orthogonal transforms while LoRDS uses scaling multiplication; (2) the PTQ comparison in Table 1 is confounded by asymmetric optimization budgets (~97% of the 0.60pp NF4 advantage attributable to 500-step refinement); (3) all results are single-run without variance, making it impossible to assess noise levels; (4) the gating mechanism promised in the abstract has no experimental support anywhere in the paper.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Abstract:** Add the 8.4% over LoftQ comparison alongside the 27.0% figure. The current abstract presents only the 27.0% (which is defensible as a comparison against a diverged baseline). Adding "and 8.4% over LoftQ at matched-or-lower floating-point parameter budgets" provides the complete picture and follows standard ICML practice of presenting the most striking result alongside the most comparable functioning-baseline result.

2. **Section 3.4, "zero inference overhead":** Qualify to "negligible PEFT-specific overhead absorbed into dequantization" — the paper's intent (no separate adapter matrices at inference) is correct, but the literal claim contradicts Table 6's NF4 comparison (7.9% slower total throughput). The QLoRA comparison (1.55× faster total throughput) is the correct reference for the PEFT-specific speedup claim.

3. **Table 1:** Add a one-shot LoRDS row (SVD initialization, no refinement, from Table 2's "Iter.=No" condition = 64.55% for Llama3-8B B=256) as the correct comparator for the PTQ comparison against NF4/GPTQ/AWQ. The current headline comparison (65.13% after 500 steps vs. 64.53% NF4) conflates algorithmic merit with computational investment.

4. **Section 3.4, "high-rank PEFT" mechanism claim:** Replace "genuinely high-rank weight updates" with "full-manifold-adapting multiplicative PEFT" or "multiplicative per-element scaling adaptation within a block-wise parameter budget." The empirical evidence (Figure 3's smooth singular value distribution) supports the claim; the nominal-rank interpretation does not. Qualify the HiRA citation: "concurrent work (HiRA, Huang et al. 2025) observes that high-rank updates aid adaptation using Hadamard transforms; LoRDS achieves a similar goal via element-wise multiplication."

5. **Tables 1, 4, 5:** Report results across at least 2–3 seeds with mean ± σ. All current tables show single-run point estimates, making it impossible to assess whether improvements are within noise. This is the standard for accepted ICML/NeurIPS papers in this subfield.

6. **Section 4.1:** Name the calibration dataset for PTQ experiments. This is a basic reproducibility requirement — without it, the method cannot be independently verified and contamination with evaluation sets cannot be assessed.

7. **Abstract (gating mechanism):** Either provide experimental evidence for "a gating mechanism to dynamically adjust rank/compression per layer" or remove this claim from the abstract. Currently it is promised but unsubstantiated anywhere in the paper.

8. **DoRA as PEFT baseline:** Add DoRA (Liu et al., 2024a) to the PEFT comparisons in Table 5, as it is now a standard baseline in the PEFT literature. The paper currently uses DoRA's training recipe (Section 4.3) but does not compare against it.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide the one-shot LoRDS baseline (SVD initialization, no refinement) for all PTQ comparisons in Table 1? The paper's own Table 2 shows this is 64.55% for Llama3-8B B=256 — essentially tied with NF4 (64.53%). If this is representative, it would substantially change how we interpret the PTQ advantage.

2. The HiRA paper (Huang et al., 2025) uses Hadamard orthogonal transforms for high-rank adaptation. How does the scaling-based element-wise multiplication in LoRDS produce "genuinely high-rank" updates when the Hadamard product rank bound limits rank(ΔW) ≤ 2r? Is there a specific property of the quantization context (e.g., the Q term) that allows the update to exceed this bound?

3. What is the calibration dataset used for PTQ experiments? If it overlaps with WikiText-2, PTB, or the commonsense evaluation sets, the reported perplexity and accuracy improvements may be inflated.

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

**Counter 1 (27.0% headline) — Accepted as defensible:** The adversarial and author-rebuttal both argued that the 27.0% is a correct statement of the gap between a functioning method (LoRDS at 60.76%) and a diverged baseline (NormalFloat at 3-bit, N.A.). **Counter 2 (high-rank PEFT) — Accepted as imprecise, not impossible:** The adversarial correctly noted that the Hadamard product rank bound applies to nominal rank, while the paper's claim is about effective rank acting on the full-rank weight manifold. The empirical evidence (Figure 3's smooth long-tail singular value distribution) is real and uncontested. The fix is wording clarification, not rejection. **Verdict impact: 0 (phrasing revision only).**

**Counter 3 (PTQ asymmetry) — Partially accepted:** The one-shot LoRDS row is a legitimate missing comparison that should be added to Table 1. However, the iterative refinement is a core algorithmic contribution (the continuous low-rank manifold enables refinement that block-wise scaling cannot perform), not an artifact. The disclosure of T=500 is complete. **Verdict impact: 0 (completeness gap, not soundness flaw).**

**Counter 4 (speedup framing) — Accepted as appropriate:** The QLoRA comparison is explicit in the abstract with hardware named. The "zero overhead" claim needs qualification but the underlying claim (no PEFT-specific adapter overhead) is correct. **Verdict impact: 0 (phrasing revision only).**

**Residual risk:** The single-run experimental reporting without variance remains the most important gap — it is impossible to assess whether any of the improvements are within noise. The gating mechanism claim in the abstract is unsubstantiated and must be removed or validated. The unnamed calibration dataset is a basic reproducibility failure.
