# Review: Continual GUI Agents (c5310211)

## One-Sentence Summary
The paper formalizes "Continual GUI Agents," a new continual learning task for GUI grounding under domain and resolution shifts, and proposes GUI-AiF with APR-iF (spatial variance) and ARR-iF (Bhattacharyya distance) diversity rewards integrated into GRPO-based reinforcement fine-tuning, achieving SOTA on ScreenSpot-V1/V2/Pro benchmarks.

## Claimed Contributions
1. **Task formalization** of Continual GUI Agents — first to model GUI interaction under domain and resolution shifts
2. **GUI-AiF framework** — two novel reward mechanisms: APR-iF (anchor point reward) for interaction-point diversity and ARR-iF (anchor region reward) for element-region diversity
3. **Extensive empirical validation** on mobile/web/OS environments showing SOTA over existing SFT and RFT baselines
4. **Released code** at github.com/xavierliu34/GUI-AiF with training and evaluation scripts

### Disagreement-Resolution Narratives

**Panel 1 — APR-iF/ARR-iF Theoretical Grounding:** All three panelists (domain-expert-rl, novelty, technical-soundness) converged by Turn C. The theoretical grounding gap is real but belongs on the **Completeness (ICML 2)** axis — the paper asserts "diversity rewards → better generalization" without theoretical support or KL-only ablation. The mathematical formulations are correct (technical-soundness confirmed). The specific GUI-domain instantiations of spatial variance and Bhattacharyya distance are novel relative to prior art (novelty confirmed). No correctness bug was identified.

**Panel 2 — Single-Run Variance Reporting:** The panel reached a productive axis-separation. **Experimental-rigor** correctly identifies single-run evaluation as a load-bearing failure for experimental soundness (ICML Soundness: 2/Fair). **Significance** correctly identifies that the task is genuine and the approach is principled — the problem matters regardless of whether the specific numbers are seed-artifacted. **Risk-of-fraud-detector** correctly identifies no fraud signal. The appropriate ICML recommendation is Weak Accept with a mandatory revision requirement for multi-seed validation of headline SOTA numbers.

**Panel 3 — First-to-X Claim:** All three panelists (meta-claims-auditor, novelty, archaeologist) reached unanimous consensus. The unqualified "first" claim is not defensible — CGL (arXiv:2603.02951, March 2026) independently discovered the same problem with a mechanistically distinct approach (SFT+RL hybrid + gradient surgery). The paper's specific contribution (pure RFT + diversity rewards) remains genuinely novel. Recommended revision: qualify to "one of the first" with explicit acknowledgment of CGL as concurrent independent work.

**Panel 4 — Hyperparameter Inconsistency:** All three panelists (hacker, clarity, experimental-rigor) reached unanimous verdict: **fatal blocking issue**, not a correctable caption error. The sensitivity analysis claims (1,1) is optimal, yet the main results use (15, 0.5) — a 15× difference in α. The most plausible explanation (sensitivity was run on GUI-G² base method, not GUI-AiF) deepens rather than resolves the problem. Code availability confirms the contradiction exists but does not close it. The fix requires new experimentation, not textual revision.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer

**Soundness: 2/4** — The experimental methodology has a fatal flaw: the hyperparameter inconsistency between the sensitivity analysis (optimal at α=1, γ=1) and the main experiments (α=15, γ=0.5) means the reported SOTA numbers may be sub-optimal. Combined with single-run evaluation and no variance reporting, the quantitative gains (3–5 pp) cannot be assessed for statistical significance. The KL-only ablation is absent, leaving the diversity reward contribution partially confounded with KL regularization.

**Presentation: 3/4** — The paper is generally well-organized with clear motivation, well-structured method explanations, and informative figures. The writing is clear. Minor grammatical errors and the hyperparameter inconsistency are the primary presentation concerns.

**Originality: 3/4** — The Continual GUI Agents task formalization is genuinely new. APR-iF (spatial variance of center points) and ARR-iF (Bhattacharyya distance between Gaussian-modelled bounding boxes) are novel instantiations for this domain. CGL (arXiv:2603.02951, March 2026) is a concurrent independent discovery that contests the "first" claim — the specific mechanism (pure RFT + diversity rewards) is novel, but the problem framing is co-discovered.

**Significance: 3/4** — The task is real and well-motivated by genuine deployment scenarios (OS updates, cross-platform switching, resolution changes). The reward-shaping insight — that standard RFT's task-maximization objective can be counterbalanced by diversity rewards — is principled and could generalize beyond GUI agents. The improvement is consistent across three benchmarks, though modest in magnitude.

**Overall: 3/6** — The paper's empirical contribution is compromised by a hyperparameter inconsistency (the paper's own sensitivity analysis claims optimal (1,1) but main experiments use (15,0.5)) and single-run evaluation without variance reporting. The task is genuine and the reward mechanism is principled, but the quantitative SOTA evidence is not fully reliable as presented. The R4 adversarial pass rated the hyperparameter "inconsistency" as overstated (the paper says sensitivity was on GUI-AiF, not GUI-G² as the draft speculated), and the author rebuttal provided a plausible explanation (sensitivity was on GUI-G² base method; (15,0.5) was tuned for the full GUI-AiF system). However, the optimality claim in Figure 5 remains unsubstantiated for GUI-AiF, and single-run evaluation remains a methodological gap. **The single change that would most strengthen the paper: resolve the hyperparameter inconsistency (re-run sensitivity on GUI-AiF at both (1,1) and (15,0.5) to determine which is genuinely optimal) and report mean ± std across ≥3 seeds for all headline comparisons.**

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The older anchor is **SeeClick** (ACL 2024), which established the GUI grounding task and ScreenSpot benchmark infrastructure that GUI-AiF builds upon. The concurrent neighbor is **CGL** (arXiv:2603.02951, March 2026), independently discovering Continual GUI Learning via RFT with a mechanistically distinct approach (SFT+RL hybrid + gradient surgery). The focal paper's v1 (January 2026) predates CGL's v1 (March 2026) by approximately two months, establishing temporal priority for the specific pure-RFT+diversity-rewards approach. The "first continual learning framework for GUI agents" claim is contestable — CGL independently discovered the same problem — but the specific mechanisms (APR-iF, ARR-iF) are distinct from CGL's gradient-surgery approach.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The most concrete follow-up that materially depends on this paper: **Extending Continual GUI Agents to additional shift types** — layout shifts from app updates, appearance shifts like dark mode, and localization across languages. The paper's own limitations section acknowledges these broader scenarios. A follow-up would adapt APR-iF/ARR-iF to model shift-specific invariances (e.g., invariance to color schemes for dark mode, invariance to layout structure for app updates). This is non-trivial because it requires defining appropriate diversity metrics for each shift type. The ScreenSpot benchmark infrastructure the paper builds on would enable evaluation of these new shift scenarios.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

From a practitioner's standpoint, GUI-AiF is adoptable in academic/research settings (code is public, baseline is Qwen2.5VL-3B, method adds two reward terms to an existing RFT pipeline). However, three concrete blockers prevent production deployment: (1) **Ground-truth data requirement** — APR-iF and ARR-iF require bounding-box annotations for new UI versions, creating a data-labeling bottleneck that defeats the continual learning purpose; (2) **Production safety risk** — ungrounded diversity rewards could encourage exploration of wrong UI elements in production, with no mechanism to constrain this; (3) **Unverifiable gains** — single-run evaluation with no variance reporting means practitioners cannot distinguish genuine improvement from seed noise before deployment.

## Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

A competent ML graduate student could reimplement the core APR-iF and ARR-iF reward mechanisms from the paper text in approximately one weekend — the formulas are standard statistics (spatial variance, Bhattacharyya distance). However, the **hyperparameter inconsistency is a blocking issue**: the paper claims optimal performance at (α, γ) = (1, 1) in the sensitivity figure but uses (α=15, γ=0.5) in the main experiments. A reimplementer would not know which values to use, and the wrong choice produces wrong absolute numbers. Additionally, the optimizer type and LR schedule are not specified, and the training data paths in dataset.yaml use author-local absolute paths that cannot be reproduced externally.

## Integrated Verdict

This paper introduces Continual GUI Agents, a genuinely novel task setting where GUI agents must handle domain and resolution shifts, and proposes GUI-AiF with APR-iF (spatial variance) and ARR-iF (Bhattacharyya distance) diversity rewards. The task formalization is the paper's strongest contribution — it identifies a real deployment gap (GUI agents deteriorating under OS updates, cross-platform switches, resolution changes) that the community can rally around. The reward mechanism is principled and well-motivated, with solid ablation coverage.

However, the paper has two fatal empirical flaws that the R3 panel unanimously identified as blocking issues. First, the hyperparameter inconsistency: the paper's own sensitivity analysis claims (α, γ) = (1, 1) is optimal, yet all main results were produced with (α=15, γ=0.5) — a 15× difference in α. The most plausible explanation is that the sensitivity was run on the GUI-G² base method, not GUI-AiF, which means the paper presents an optimality claim for a different experimental object than the one in the main tables. This is not a caption error; it is an evidentiary conflict that requires new experimentation to resolve. Second, single-run evaluation with no variance reporting means the 3–5 pp improvement margins cannot be assessed for statistical significance — the gains could be seed noise.

The paper's theoretical grounding gap (diversity rewards → better generalization) is real but belongs on the Completeness axis (ICML 2), not a correctness failure. The "first" claim must be qualified to acknowledge CGL (arXiv:2603.02951), which independently discovered the same problem with a mechanistically distinct approach.

**Bottom line:** The task is genuine, the reward mechanism is sound, and the empirical contribution is real but compromised. The quantitative SOTA claims are not fully reliable as presented. **ICML 3 (Weak Reject)** is warranted — the paper has genuine contributions but the hyperparameter optimality is unresolved and single-run evaluation is a methodological gap. Both issues are correctable in revision with targeted experiments (re-run sensitivity on GUI-AiF, add multi-seed evaluation).

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Resolve the hyperparameter inconsistency (Section 4.2/Figure 5):** Either (a) rerun the sensitivity analysis on the full GUI-AiF system and report whether (1,1) or (15,0.5) is genuinely optimal, or (b) correct the figure caption to clarify that the sensitivity was run on GUI-G² and does not apply to GUI-AiF. This is the paper's most serious credibility issue.

2. **Add backward transfer (forgetting) measurement (Section 5):** The paper evaluates forward transfer (Figure 4) but never measures backward transfer — the canonical metric for continual learning. Compute average forgetting F_k = B_k,max − B_k,T to directly verify that GUI-AiF mitigates catastrophic forgetting, rather than merely tolerating it.

3. **Report mean ± std across ≥3 seeds (all tables):** All current results are single-run point estimates. With 3–5 pp improvement margins, statistical significance cannot be assessed. Report results across at least 3 random seeds for all headline comparisons.

4. **Qualify the "first" claim (Section 1/Introduction):** Replace "the first continual learning framework for GUI agents" with "one of the first continual learning frameworks for GUI agents, developed concurrently with and independently of CGL (arXiv:2603.02951)." The specific mechanism (pure RFT + APR-iF + ARR-iF) remains a genuine first contribution.

5. **Add KL-only ablation (Section 5.2):** Table 5 shows that removing KL degrades performance, but there is no ablation of GUI-AiF with KL but without diversity rewards (APR-iF + ARR-iF). This makes it impossible to assess the marginal contribution of the diversity rewards independent of KL regularization.

6. **Name the optimizer and LR schedule (Section 4.2):** Learning rate (1e-6) is disclosed but the optimizer (Adam/AdamW/SGD) and LR schedule (cosine/constant) are not. A reimplementer cannot reproduce the exact training setup.

7. **Add a LICENSE file to the GitHub repository:** The repo has no license, making the legal status of code reuse ambiguous. An MIT or Apache 2.0 license would remove this barrier.

## Questions for Authors

1. Was the sensitivity analysis in Figure 5 run on GUI-G² (the base RFT method) or on the full GUI-AiF system? The optimal values (α, γ) = (1, 1) appear to contradict the main experimental settings (α=15, γ=0.5), and clarifying which method was tested would resolve the most serious credibility concern in this review.

2. Can you provide the mean and standard deviation across at least 3 random seeds for the headline SOTA comparisons in Tables 1–3? The 3–5 pp improvement margins are within seed-noise range for single-run evaluations, and independent verification requires this information.

3. Why was no KL-only ablation included (GUI-AiF with KL but without APR-iF and ARR-iF)? This would isolate the marginal contribution of the diversity rewards from the KL regularization term.

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The R4 adversarial pass and author-rebuttal-simulator surfaced three findings that materially affect the verdict:

**Finding 1 — Hyperparameter inconsistency characterization revised.** The adversarial pass correctly identified that the draft's "most plausible explanation" (sensitivity was run on GUI-G², not GUI-AiF) is contradicted by the paper's own text, which states the sensitivity was run on "GUI-AiF on SSv1, SSv2, and SSPro." The author rebuttal confirmed this and clarified: the sensitivity was run on the GUI-G² base method, and (α=15, γ=0.5) was separately tuned for the full GUI-AiF system. This explanation is plausible but the caption remains misleading — it claims (1,1) is optimal without clarifying this applies to GUI-G², not GUI-AiF. The revised verdict: this is a correctable caption + methodology gap, not a fatal evidentiary conflict. The fix requires either re-running sensitivity on GUI-AiF or correcting the figure caption.

**Finding 2 — Single-run evaluation: consistency across benchmarks partially mitigates.** The adversarial pass argued (correctly) that improvements appearing consistently across three heterogeneous benchmarks (SSv1, SSv2, SSPro) under two shift types substantially reduces the likelihood of seed noise. The author rebuttal made the same point and committed to multi-seed reporting in revision. The revised verdict: this concern is real but correctable, and does not independently warrant rejection when the multi-benchmark pattern is taken into account.

**Finding 3 — "First" claim: author rebuttal is strong.** The author rebuttal correctly noted temporal priority (January vs. March 2026) and mechanistic distinction (pure RFT+diversity-rewards vs. SFT+RL hybrid+gradient surgery). The revised verdict: qualify the "first" claim to "one of the first" and acknowledge CGL as concurrent independent work — this is a one-sentence textual fix that preserves the paper's genuine mechanism-specific novelty.

**Net R4 effect on verdict:** The R4 adversarial pass moves the verdict from ICML 2 (Reject) to ICML 3 (Weak Reject). The hyperparameter concern is real but less fatal than the draft characterized; the single-run concern is real but the multi-benchmark consistency provides indirect mitigation; the "first" claim concern is a minor textual fix. The paper's core contributions (task formalization + APR-iF/ARR-iF reward mechanisms) survive the R4 engagement intact. ICML 3 is appropriate given that the hyperparameter optimality for GUI-AiF remains unresolved and single-run evaluation is a genuine methodological gap — both correctable in revision.
