## One-Sentence Summary
Mosaic Learning proposes a model-fragmentation-based decentralized learning framework with per-fragment independent gossip matrices and fragment-wise averaging, proving worst-case convergence matching Epidemic Learning (EL) and an eigenvalue-reduction mechanism in convex settings, but the primary eigenvalue mechanism is never measured in the non-convex experimental regime where the empirical gains are demonstrated.

## Claimed Contributions
1. A model-fragmentation-based decentralized learning framework (Mosaic Learning) that disseminates model fragments independently across the network, using per-fragment distinct gossip matrices and fragment-wise averaging.
2. Theoretical analysis proving (i) state-of-the-art worst-case convergence rate matching EL, and (ii) improved contraction via parameter-correlation-aware eigenvalue reduction as the number of fragments K increases.
3. Empirical evaluation on 4 learning tasks showing up to 12 percentage points higher node-level test accuracy vs. EL under non-IID settings.

### Disagreement-Resolution Narratives

**Panel 1 — theory-empirics-mismatch:** All three panelists (completeness, experimental-rigor, narrative-coherence-auditor) converged from independent angles by Turn C. The paper's Section 4.2 proves eigenvalue-reduction of the consensus contraction matrix only for quadratic, identical, convex loss landscapes. Section 5 operates on non-convex CNNs/LSTMs where consensus distance *increases* with K — the opposite of what the theory predicts. The paper offers a disclosed limitation statement ("consensus distance is unreliable in non-convex settings," §5.1 line 820–822) that is not post-hoc. However, the conclusion then introduces "information diversity" as a second unmeasured rescue mechanism. The panel found the theory-empirics gap is more accurately characterized as a **presentation scope problem** than a structural attribution failure — the paper explicitly scopes the eigenvalue analysis to convex settings, but the abstract's "simplified system" qualifier is insufficiently prominent, and the conclusion introduces a new unmeasured mechanism without empirical support. Resolution: completeness = 2, experimental-rigor = 2, narrative-coherence = 2. Required remediation: add explicit abstract scope markers for the convex-only eigenvalue claim; remove or empirically validate the "information diversity" framing.

**Panel 2 — first-to-capture-falsified:** All three panelists (meta-claims-auditor, archaeologist, novelty) reached full unanimous resolution. The conclusion's "first to capture the evolution of the entire global model within a model-fragmented network architecture" is definitively falsified by Shatter (biswas2024noiseless, PoPETs 2025), which shares full co-authorship with this submission and is correctly cited in the paper's own related work section (§sec:related, line 915). The paper's durable contribution — per-fragment independent gossip matrices (W_t^(k)) with fragment-wise averaging and the convergence theory connecting fragmentation factor K to eigenvalue reduction — is real and novel, but it cannot salvage the empirical "first to capture" framing. Resolution: the conclusion must be revised to remove or substantially qualify the meta-claim. Recommended revised language: "first to provide a convergence theory connecting fragmentation factor K to eigenvalue reduction in model-fragmented networks."

**Panel 3 — proof-gap-severity:** All three panelists (proof-checker, technical-soundness, assumption-stress-tester) converged by Turn C on a three-part resolution: (1) Mixing Lemma step (c) is a fixable presentation omission whose underlying mathematics (RMS-AM inequality chained with Lemma 1) is standard and correct — not a critical proof error; (2) the telescoping argument for Theorem 1's ε-bound — entirely delegated to de Vos et al. with no independent verification — is the paper's actual load-bearing soundness concern; (3) the assumption-specification failures (unstated eigenvalue heterogeneity requirement in Theorem 2; convexity mismatch between theory and empirical validation) constitute compound fragility that narrows the effective domain of the headline claim below what the paper advertises.

**Panel 4 — 12pp-cherrypick:** All three panelists (experimental-rigor, claim-auditor, figure-and-table-auditor) reached unanimous agreement by Turn C. The "up to 12 percentage points" headline in the abstract constitutes an **undisclosed cherry-pick** of the maximum across a 48-condition grid (4 datasets × 3 non-IID levels × 4+ K-values), traceable to Figure 10 (CIFAR-100 α=0.1, K=16). The paper does not label the specific condition producing 12pp in the main text. The no-seed-count problem — rendering every "mean ± 95% CI" in the paper statistically void — is the load-bearing flaw. The 12pp cannot be validated as a statistically meaningful improvement without reported seed count.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's integrated reading)

**Soundness: 2 / 4** — The paper's core theoretical claims are plausible but presentation is incomplete. Lemma 2 (consensus error evolution) is correctly derived with valid linear algebra. However, Theorem 1's convergence proof is delegated to de Vos et al. with no independent verification, and the primary eigenvalue-reduction mechanism is proven only in convex/quadratic settings that do not match the non-convex experimental substrate. The adversarial pass correctly noted that the paper explicitly scopes the eigenvalue analysis to "a simplified quadratic setting" (line 648) and explicitly states consensus distance is unreliable in non-convex settings (line 820–822) — these are disclosed limitations, not hidden failures. However, the abstract's "simplified system" qualifier is insufficiently prominent, and the conclusion introduces "information diversity" as an unmeasured rescue mechanism.

**Presentation: 3 / 4** — The paper is commendably well-organized: Algorithm 1 is clear, the two theoretical contributions are cleanly separated, and the four research questions in Section 5 provide excellent signposting. The abstract is self-contained and under 250 words. However, the abstract's "simplified system" eigenvalue claim is undefined, the paper lacks a dedicated Limitations section (now standard at top venues), and all experimental learning curves lack error bars or seed counts.

**Originality: 3 / 4** — The per-fragment independent gossip matrices (W_t^(k)) with fragment-wise averaging is genuinely novel over SHATTER/DIVSHARE/YOGA. The eigenvalue analysis connecting K to consensus contraction in convex landscapes is a real theoretical contribution. The "first to capture" meta-claim in the conclusion is definitively falsified by Shatter (PoPETs 2025). The "state-of-the-art worst-case convergence" is more accurately characterized as a wording issue — the paper matches EL's rate, which is the state-of-the-art rate, while adding eigenvalue-reduction benefits EL lacks.

**Significance: 3 / 4** — The paper addresses a real gap in decentralized learning with a 12pp empirical improvement that is substantial if reproducible. The theoretical convergence results advance the sub-field. However, comparison is limited to one baseline (EL), the eigenvalue mechanism is unmeasured in the experimental regime, and the "new DL standard" framing is not supported by the evidence.

**Overall recommendation: 3 / 6 (Weak Reject)** — The paper has genuine theoretical and empirical merit: the fragmentation mechanism is novel, the eigenvalue analysis is a real contribution, and the empirical gains in non-IID settings are potentially significant. However, two unrebutted compound failures prevent acceptance: (1) no seed count is reported anywhere in the paper, making all "mean ± 95% CI" figures statistically void and the 12pp improvement unverifiable as anything other than a single-draw observation; (2) the "first to capture" meta-claim in the conclusion is materially false (falsified by the authors' own prior work Shatter, PoPETs 2025). The theory-empirics gap — while real — is more accurately a presentation scope problem than a structural failure, because the paper explicitly discloses the convex-only scope of the eigenvalue analysis. The paper requires major revision on the statistical reporting and meta-claim issues before it can be accepted.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Report random seed count (n≥3, ideally n≥5) for all experiments. This single addition would validate the empirical claims and transform the statistical reporting from below-bar to acceptable.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper correctly cites SHATTER (biswas2024noiseless, PoPETs 2025) as prior work studying model-fragmented network evolution — but the paper's conclusion then claims to be "the first to capture" this same phenomenon. This is self-contradictory within the paper itself. SHATTER and Mosaic Learning share co-authorship, meaning the "first to capture" claim is not merely missing prior art but is contradicted by the authors' own prior published work. The paper's actual novel contribution — the convergence theory connecting fragmentation factor K to eigenvalue reduction — is genuinely new over SHATTER/DIVSHARE/YOGA, but the empirical phenomenon of model evolution in fragmented networks was already captured by Shatter.

The "state-of-the-art worst-case convergence" claim is more defensible than the draft initially stated: the paper matches EL's rate (which IS the state-of-the-art convergence rate for this problem family), while adding eigenvalue-reduction benefits that EL lacks. The issue is wording precision, not mathematical inaccuracy — the abstract should say "matches the worst-case convergence rate of EL" rather than "state-of-the-art worst-case convergence rate."

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The most concrete follow-up project this paper enables is applying Mosaic Learning's fragmentation principle to large language model fine-tuning across geographically distributed institutions (e.g., hospitals sharing patient data, financial institutions sharing transaction data). The fragmentation approach could enable parameter-efficient communication where only relevant model fragments are shared based on local data characteristics, reducing communication by an order of magnitude compared to full-model gossip protocols. This extension is non-trivial and materially depends on the fragmentation framework and eigenvalue-based contraction analysis from this paper.

The paper also opens theoretical questions: what is the precise regime boundary between convex and non-convex settings where the eigenvalue-reduction mechanism transitions from beneficial to counterproductive? Characterizing this boundary formally would be a significant follow-up contribution.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

For a practitioner considering Mosaic Learning for a real decentralized learning deployment, the most important gap is the absence of an open-source implementation — the paper promises code "upon acceptance," which is not useful for current decision-making. Beyond this, the evaluation on 3 machines × 2 nodes is at the small end of practical scale; more nodes and wider topologies would better demonstrate practical relevance. The paper also does not measure communication cost directly — the claim of "no increase in communication cost" is theoretical, not empirical, and a practitioner would need actual bandwidth measurements to plan deployment.

The most actionable positive finding for practitioners: the IID condition serves as a useful control — Mosaic Learning matches EL in IID settings, meaning the fragmentation mechanism's benefit is concentrated in the high-heterogeneity settings where decentralized learning is most needed. This makes Mosaic Learning most attractive for settings with highly heterogeneous data distributions.

The fragmentation operator C is defined only informally ("a mapping C: [1,d] → [1,K]") without sensitivity analysis. A weekend reimplementation of the K=1 case (EL equivalence) is feasible; a full K=16 evaluation matching the paper's claims is not independently verifiable without the labeled data from the paper's own experiments.

## Integrated Verdict

Mosaic Learning proposes a model-fragmentation framework for decentralized learning with per-fragment independent gossip matrices and fragment-wise averaging. The core idea is genuine and potentially useful: fragmenting models and propagating different fragments along independent gossip topologies can exploit parameter correlation structure to improve learning in heterogeneous settings. The eigenvalue analysis connecting K to consensus contraction in convex landscapes is a real theoretical contribution. The empirical gains in non-IID settings — up to ~10pp on CIFAR-10/100 — are substantial if reproducible.

However, the R4 adversarial pass and author-rebuttal-simulator clarified that the paper's theory-empirics gap is more accurately characterized as a **presentation scope problem** than a "structural attribution failure." The paper explicitly scopes the eigenvalue analysis to "a simplified quadratic setting" (line 648) and explicitly states that "consensus distance does not constitute a reliable metric to reflect performance in non-convex settings" (§5.1 line 820–822). These are disclosed limitations, not hidden failures. The R3 panel's "structural attribution failure" characterization was too harsh — the paper never claims the Section 4.2 eigenvalue mechanism is validated in the non-convex regime.

Nevertheless, two unrebutted concerns remain as the basis for a Weak Reject verdict. First, **no seed count is reported anywhere** in the paper, making all "mean ± 95% CI" figures statistically void. The paper explicitly reports "mean ± 95% CI" on every figure — without knowing the number of independent runs (n_seeds), these intervals are uninterpretable. If n_seeds = 1, there is no variance estimate at all. If n_seeds = 3, the intervals are wide and the 12pp improvement cannot be distinguished from noise. This is a below-bar statistical reporting failure for an empirical paper. Second, **the "first to capture" meta-claim in the conclusion is materially false** — it is falsified by Shatter (biswas2024noiseless, PoPETs 2025), co-authored by the same authors and correctly cited in the paper's own related work.

The "state-of-the-art worst-case convergence" claim — initially characterized as "inaccurate" — is more fairly characterized as a **wording issue**. The paper matches EL's rate, which IS the state-of-the-art rate for this problem family, while adding eigenvalue-reduction benefits that EL lacks. "Matches the worst-case convergence rate of EL" is more precise than "state-of-the-art worst-case convergence rate," but the underlying mathematical claim (equality with EL's rate) is accurate.

The paper has real merit and could be strengthened significantly through revision. The most important single improvement would be reporting random seed count for all experiments, transforming the statistical reporting from below-bar to acceptable.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Conclusion — Remove or qualify the "first to capture" meta-claim.** Shatter (biswas2024noiseless, PoPETs 2025) empirically captured model-fragmented network evolution and shares co-authorship with this submission. The conclusion must be revised to remove this claim or replace it with the precise scope: "first to provide a convergence theory connecting fragmentation factor K to eigenvalue reduction in model-fragmented networks."

2. **Abstract — Soften the "state-of-the-art" convergence claim.** Change "state-of-the-art worst-case convergence rate" to "matches the worst-case convergence rate of EL." The underlying mathematical claim (equality with EL's rate, proven in Remark 1) is accurate; the phrasing "state-of-the-art" implies superiority and should be corrected.

3. **Abstract — Add the IID-parity qualifier to the 12pp claim.** The abstract claims "up to 12 percentage points higher node-level test accuracy compared to EL" without noting this only holds in non-IID settings. The introduction (bullet 4) correctly states "in settings with high label heterogeneity, while maintaining parity with EL in homogeneous (IID) settings" — this qualifier must appear in the abstract.

4. **Abstract — Make the "simplified system" scope unmistakable.** The phrase "improving contraction by reducing the highest eigenvalue of a simplified system" (abstract) should be expanded: "(specifically, in quadratic/convex loss landscapes, where the eigenvalue-reduction mechanism can be formally analyzed)." This is not a hidden failure — the paper discloses it in §5.1 — but the abstract should make it explicit.

5. **Conclusion — Remove or empirically validate "information diversity."** The conclusion introduces "information diversity" as an explanatory mechanism for the empirical gains in non-convex settings. This term is never operationalized or directly measured. If it is to be retained, it requires its own dedicated experimental validation.

6. **§5.1 and all experimental figures — Report random seed count.** No seed count appears anywhere in the paper. All "mean ± 95% CI" figures are statistically void without this. Report at minimum n≥3 seeds for each condition, ideally n≥5.

7. **§5 — Add significance testing.** Report p-values or confidence intervals for the Mosaic vs. EL comparison on each dataset. The ~10pp observed for CIFAR-10 non-IID (not the claimed 12pp) cannot be verified as statistically significant without this.

8. **Add a dedicated Limitations section.** Standard at ICML/NeurIPS/ICLR. Should explicitly state: (a) the eigenvalue/contraction analysis holds only for convex/quadratic losses; (b) the experimental evaluation uses 3 machines × 2 nodes at small scale; (c) no sensitivity analysis on the fragmentation operator C is provided; (d) communication cost is not empirically measured.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide the number of random seeds used for each experiment? All figures report "mean ± 95% CI" but the sample size is never disclosed. Without n≥3 seeds, these intervals are statistically void and the 12pp improvement cannot be verified as anything other than a single-draw observation.

2. The paper claims "state-of-the-art worst-case convergence rate" but Theorem 1 shows equality with EL. Is the intended claim that matching EL's rate while also providing eigenvalue-reduction benefits (in convex settings) constitutes "state-of-the-art"? If so, please clarify this in the abstract — "matches the worst-case convergence rate of EL" is more precise and equally compelling.

3. The conclusion claims to be "the first to capture the evolution of the entire global model within a model-fragmented network architecture." Given that Shatter (biswas2024noiseless, PoPETs 2025) — co-authored by the same authors — already studied model-fragmented network evolution empirically, what is the precise distinction between Shatter's empirical capture and this paper's contribution that justifies the "first" claim?
