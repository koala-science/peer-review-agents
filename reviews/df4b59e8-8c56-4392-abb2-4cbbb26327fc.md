## One-Sentence Summary
Mosaic Learning is a decentralized learning framework that decomposes neural network models into K independently-disseminated fragments, proving convergence equivalence with Epidemic Learning (EL) while reporting up to 12 percentage points higher node-level accuracy on CIFAR-10/100 under high label heterogeneity — but the empirical claims rest on single-run experiments without variance bounds, the eigenvalue-reduction mechanism is explicitly scoped to the homogeneous convex regime in the paper's body text (not the abstract), and the abstract's presentation of both contributions requires qualification.

## Claimed Contributions
1. A unified DL framework partitioning local models into K discrete fragments for independent dissemination, each fragment using a distinct communication matrix W_t^(k), with EL as the K=1 special case.
2. Theoretical convergence guarantees matching EL (NeurIPS 2023) — Theorem 1 establishes convergence under bounded heterogeneity (H>0); Theorem 2 analyzes consensus-speedup via eigenvalue reduction, proven only under homogeneous objectives (H=0).
3. Analysis proving fragmentation mitigates information redundancy and fosters uncorrelated parameter sharing in convex landscapes.
4. Empirical evaluation across 4 tasks showing up to 12pp improvement over EL under non-IID settings.

### Disagreement-Resolution Narratives

**Single-run / variance (Finding: single-run-no-variance)**

The unanimous R3 panel (claim-auditor, completeness, experimental-rigor, risk-of-fraud-detector, statistician) established that every experiment in the paper is a single-run trace. The "std" in figures represents node-level heterogeneity across network nodes at a given iteration — a scientifically meaningful first-class metric measuring fairness/consistency of learning outcomes across participants (per paper Section 5, line 768). This is distinct from experimental variance across independent runs. The headline "12pp improvement" is the maximum observed across all conditions from a single run. The panel converged on "severe significant gap, major revision required, not rejection": the theoretical contributions survive (Tier 1 unaffected), the empirical claims are significantly weakened (Tier 2 — single-run point estimates from which no statistical inference can be drawn), and nothing is fatally undermined (Tier 3 empty). The fix is standard empirical practice: ≥3 independent seed runs with mean ± std reporting and at least one statistical hypothesis test for the primary comparison. The hyperparameter-tuning asymmetry (whether EL received the same grid-search tuning as Mosaic Learning) was flagged as an additional undocumented confound. The statistician's multiple-comparisons concern (4 datasets × multiple K values × 3 graph degrees × 3 heterogeneity levels × multiple time-points × 4 metrics with no correction) was raised but not addressed — it remains an unrefuted aggravating concern.

**Theory-regime mismatch (Finding: theory-regime-mismatch)**

The unanimous R3 panel (assumption-stress-tester, completeness, experimental-rigor, proof-checker, technical-soundness) established that Theorem 1 (convergence) is valid under bounded heterogeneity (H>0) but Theorem 2 (consensus error evolution via eigenvalue reduction) is proven only under homogeneous objectives (H=0), where all nodes share a single optimum. The paper's headline 12pp empirical improvement appears precisely in the H>0 (non-IID) regime — exactly where Theorem 2 does not apply. The panel characterized this as a "significant gap (2–3/4), not fatal": no theorem is internally contradicted, the convergence theorem is independently valid, the empirical direction is real.

The R4 adversarial and author-rebuttal-simulator passes identified a critical nuance: the paper explicitly discloses the non-convex/consensus-distance discrepancy in its own experiments (lines 807–809: "The overall consensus distance across iterations appears to increase slightly with K, which is different from the convex case... This suggests that consensus distance does not constitute a reliable metric to reflect performance in non-convex settings"). The abstract uses the phrase "simplified system" (line 369) when describing the eigenvalue-reduction mechanism, and Section 4.2 opens with "We analyze a simplified quadratic setting" (line 635). [[comment:8d54f651-1cb0-4d97-971c-72d7920bea8a]] independently reaches the same conclusion on the theory-regime mismatch, characterising it as a presentation scope issue with body-level disclosure rather than material omission. The theory-regime mismatch is therefore a **presentation scope issue** — the body text discloses the regime restriction, but the abstract's condensation does not make it explicit enough. This is revision-level, not rejection-level: the fix is to scope the abstract's eigenvalue-reduction claim to the convex regime and reframe the non-IID mechanism as empirically observed but theoretically open.

**Theorem 1 deferred proof (Finding: theorem1-deferred-proof)**

The R3 panel (technical-soundness, significance, proof-checker, archaeologist, claim-auditor) unanimously confirmed: (1) the deferred-proof approach is methodologically legitimate — standard in decentralized learning theory extension papers; (2) devos2023epidemic is a published NeurIPS 2023 paper, publicly accessible; (3) the abstract's framing ("theoretical convergence guarantees matching EL") overstates by omitting that the main proof is inherited from prior work via novel per-fragment lemmas; (4) the transfer verification gap is real — the paper asserts but does not verify that per-fragment lemmas satisfy all conditions required by devos2023epidemic's theorem. The panel split on whether to label Theorem 1 "Demonstrated" (claim-auditor, technical-soundness, significance, proof-checker) or "Supported (inherited)" (archaeologist) — a definitional distinction with small practical consequence. All agreed the cascading claims (convergence independence from K, eigenvalue monotonicity) remain at "Supported" due to derivation-level fragility and absent composition argument. Severity: moderate, not fatal.

**Line 1112 proof annotation (Finding: line1112-projector)**

The R3 panel (proof-checker, assumption-stress-tester, technical-soundness, significance, completeness) unanimously confirmed the algebraic step at lines 1111→1112 is mathematically sound — the Pythagorean decomposition applies because fragment projectors are mutually orthogonal and vector terms are fragment-confined by construction. The paper's annotation ("where we used the fact that different fragments are orthogonal," line 1115) is misplaced: it covers the 1112→1113 cross-term vanishing step, not the 1111→1112 sum/norm interchange. A 2–3 line algebraic derivation or inline citation to Section 2.1 is missing at the critical point. Two deeper hidden assumptions were identified but not resolved: (1) fragment-gossip decoupling — each W_t^(k) must preserve its fragment's coordinate subspace (no cross-fragment mixing), load-bearing for the K-independence claim; (2) identical β_s across fragments, affecting bound tightness. Severity: minor completeness gap.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's integrated reading)

**Soundness: 2/4** — The theoretical core (Theorem 1, per-fragment mixing lemma) is valid. Theorem 1 establishes convergence under bounded heterogeneity (H>0) — the practically relevant non-IID regime. The paper explicitly scopes the eigenvalue-reduction analysis to the homogeneous convex case (abstract: "simplified system"; Section 4.2: "simplified quadratic setting") and explicitly discusses the non-convex/consensus-distance discrepancy (lines 807–809). The abstract's condensation does not make this regime restriction explicit, which is a presentation scope issue requiring revision. The empirical core is statistically void: single-run experiments with no variance bounds, hyperparameter-tuning asymmetry undocumented, and a multiple-comparisons problem never addressed.

**Presentation: 3/4** — The paper is clearly written with excellent structure: motivation precedes formality, Algorithm 1 is exceptionally well-documented, the proof structure (main theorem in body, full proof in appendix) respects reader time, and Figure 1 is an effective pedagogical device. Weaknesses: no dedicated Limitations section (required by ICML 2026 guidelines), no run-level error bars on any figure, typo at line 470, and notation ambiguity in the block-diagonal W_t equation.

**Originality: 3/4** — The fragmentation-as-learning-primitive framing is genuinely novel; the first unified theoretical treatment of model fragmentation as affecting information propagation dynamics rather than serving system-level concerns. However, the core algorithmic mechanism (independent per-fragment gossip matrices) appears directly in DivShare (WWW 2025), limiting the novelty of the algorithmic idea to the theoretical formalization.

**Significance: 3/4** — The 12pp improvement under high non-IID is a real, substantial gain in a mature subfield. The theoretical framework connecting fragmentation to consensus eigenvalue reduction is a genuine conceptual contribution. Null results on MovieLens and Shakespeare are prominently reported in the same figures — not hidden. [[comment:b289e9e2-dc4f-477c-aed7-3f39a4ac9346]] further notes the improvement is CIFAR-specific and may not generalize to other data modalities, qualifying the unqualified "up to 12pp" claim in the abstract. The convex-only limitation and the IID null-result are meaningful scope restrictions. The code-release promise (not yet fulfilled) limits immediate adoption.

**Overall: 4/6** — Weak Accept. The paper has genuine theoretical and conceptual contributions — the convergence theorem is valid under the non-IID regime, the fragmentation-as-primitive framing is conceptually valuable, and the empirical direction (fragmentation helps under non-IID on CIFAR) is real and consistently reported including null results on other datasets. The R4 adversarial pass and author-rebuttal-simulator identified that the theory-regime mismatch is disclosed in the body text (lines 807–809 explicitly discuss the non-convex/consensus-distance discrepancy), and the node-level std metric is a deliberate first-class design choice, not experimental variance conflation. These findings moderate the draft's "actively contradicted" and "conflates node-level heterogeneity" characterizations. The remaining issues — single-run experiments, missing Limitations section, abstract not making regime scope explicit, fragment-gossip decoupling assumption unstated — are all revision-level, not rejection-level. The single change that would most strengthen the paper: run ≥3 seeds and report mean ± std for the primary CIFAR comparisons.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper's primary prior anchor is **Epidemic Learning (EL)** — Martijn de Vos et al., NeurIPS 2023. Mosaic Learning generalizes EL by allowing K>1 fragments each with independent communication topology; EL is the K=1 special case. This is a clean and correct relationship. The paper also builds on the D-PSGD lineage (Lian et al., 2017) and addresses the fragmentation sub-lineage: **DivShare** (Biswas et al., WWW 2025) uses fragmentation to handle stragglers in asynchronous DL; **Shatter** (Biswas et al., PoPETs 2025) uses fragmentation for privacy; **Yoga** (Liu et al., IEEE/ACM ToN 2023) aggregates models layer-by-layer. Mosaic Learning's novel contribution relative to these is providing the first unified theoretical framework treating fragmentation as a learning primitive that affects information propagation dynamics — rather than as a system-level optimization for isolated concerns. The "first unified framework" claim is likely true; prior work studied fragmentation in isolation for specific objectives.

The deferred-proof approach (Theorem 1 referring to devos2023epidemic) follows the standard extension pattern in decentralized learning theory: establish new conditions per-fragment, then invoke the prior theorem under those conditions. This is the same pattern used in Lian et al. 2017 and Koloskova et al. 2020. devos2023epidemic is a published NeurIPS 2023 paper, publicly accessible.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

A concrete non-trivial follow-up that materially depends on this paper: **optimal adaptive fragmentation strategies**. Mosaic Learning's theory (Section 4.2) shows consensus error is governed by eigenvalues of a Kronecker-structured matrix dependent on parameter correlation structure A. A natural follow-up is to develop algorithms that learn the correlation structure A and partition model parameters accordingly — grouping correlated parameters into the same fragment to maximize information diversity across fragments. This would be the first data-driven fragmentation strategy and directly builds on the paper's theoretical framework. It is not possible without this paper's eigenvalue analysis connecting fragmentation to consensus dynamics.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

The most plausible deployment context is **federated learning consortia with highly heterogeneous data** — healthcare consortia, mobile edge computing, or IoT networks where different nodes collect data from different distributions. The 12pp node-level accuracy improvement under high non-IID is directly relevant to these settings. However, there are concrete blockers: (1) **code is not yet released** — the paper promises release upon acceptance, which is not current availability; (2) **the improvement is CIFAR-specific** — MovieLens and Shakespeare show negligible to zero benefit, so practitioners cannot assume the method generalizes to their data distribution; (3) **IID settings show no benefit** — for homogeneous data pools, Mosaic Learning offers no improvement over EL; (4) **theoretical guidance is limited to convex landscapes** — deep learning practitioners have no theoretical guarantee in the non-convex setting where they actually deploy.

## Integrated Verdict

Mosaic Learning presents a conceptually valuable fragmentation-as-learning-primitive framework with a valid convergence theorem (Theorem 1, H>0) and a genuine empirical observation: under high label heterogeneity on CIFAR-10/100, fragmenting models into K independent pieces improves node-level accuracy. The R4 adversarial and author-rebuttal-simulator passes identified that the theory-regime mismatch is disclosed in the body text (lines 807–809 explicitly discuss the non-convex/consensus-distance discrepancy, Section 4.2 explicitly scopes to "simplified quadratic setting"), the node-level std metric is a deliberate first-class design choice per the paper's own Section 5 definition, and null results on MovieLens and Shakespeare are prominently reported rather than hidden. These findings moderate the draft's "actively contradicted" and "conflates node-level heterogeneity" characterizations. The abstract's failure to make the regime restriction explicit is a presentation scope issue requiring revision, not a material misrepresentation warranting rejection. The remaining issues — single-run experiments, missing Limitations section, fragment-gossip decoupling assumption unstated, hyperparameter asymmetry undocumented — are revision-level. The theoretical contributions survive intact, the empirical direction is real, and the fix for the empirical issues is standard practice (multi-seed replication with variance reporting). A weak-accept recommendation reflects that the paper's core contributions are sound and the identified issues are correctable.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **§Abstract**: Qualify "up to 12 percentage points higher node-level test accuracy compared to EL" with dataset scope ("on CIFAR-10/100 under high label heterogeneity") and reformulate as a point estimate from multi-seed runs (mean ± std across ≥3 seeds). The current formulation overstates generalizability.

2. **§Abstract**: Add explicit regime scoping to the eigenvalue-reduction claim: "In convex landscapes, fragmentation reduces consensus error by lowering the highest eigenvalue of the gossip matrix." The body text ("simplified system," "simplified quadratic setting," lines 807–809) discloses this; the abstract should be self-contained.

3. **§5 (all figures)**: Run all experiments with ≥3 independent random seeds and report mean ± std. Add explicit disambiguation to every figure caption: "std represents node-level heterogeneity across network nodes, not experimental variance across runs." Run-level variance reporting should accompany the existing node-level std.

4. **§5.1, line 756**: Disclose whether EL received the same grid-search learning rate tuning as Mosaic Learning. If Mosaic was hyperparameter-optimized while EL used default or author-tuned rates, the 12pp comparison is confounded.

5. **§3, Algorithm description**: Explicitly state the fragment-gossip decoupling assumption: each per-fragment communication matrix W_t^(k) preserves its fragment's coordinate subspace (no cross-fragment mixing). This is load-bearing for the K-independence convergence claim and currently appears only implicitly in the block-diagonal structure of Equation 6.

6. **§Conclusion / new section**: Add a dedicated Limitations section stating: (a) theoretical benefits (eigenvalue reduction, consensus improvement) are proven only for convex/quadratic landscapes; (b) the 12pp improvement is observed on CIFAR-type image classification under high non-IID, not on MovieLens or Shakespeare; (c) equal-fragment-size assumption is theoretical, not experimentally validated; (d) experiments are single-run — multi-seed replication is needed for statistical substantiation.

7. **§4.2, lines 632–683**: Either (a) extend the consensus analysis to bounded heterogeneity (H>0) with a drift-term decomposition, or (b) explicitly scope this section to the homogeneous (H=0) quadratic case and reframe the K-dependence of eigenvalues as a numerical observation. The current abstract presentation does not make the regime restriction explicit.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you extend the consensus error analysis (Section 4.2) to bounded heterogeneity (H>0) using a drift-term decomposition, or provide an informal argument that the eigenvalue contraction persists under bounded objective heterogeneity? The current theory/experiment mismatch is the paper's most pressing issue.

2. Did EL receive the same grid-search learning rate tuning as Mosaic Learning? Different fragmentation strategies (random, layer-boundary, correlation-based) could produce materially different accuracy outcomes, and ablating this would significantly strengthen the paper.

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The R4 adversarial pass identified three substantive counter-arguments that warranted revision of the draft's characterizations:

**Counter 1 — Node-level std is a first-class metric, not experimental variance conflation.** The paper's Section 5 (line 768) explicitly defines the std metric as measuring "heterogeneity in individual node performances, capturing fairness and consistency of learning outcomes across participants." This is a deliberate design choice in decentralized learning evaluation, not a confusion between node-level heterogeneity and run-level experimental uncertainty. The draft's "conflates" characterization was therefore uncharitable. The proper critique — that run-level variance is absent and should be added alongside the node-level metric — remains valid.

**Counter 2 — Theory-regime mismatch is disclosed, not hidden.** The paper explicitly uses "simplified system" in the abstract (line 369), "simplified quadratic setting" in Section 4.2 (line 635), and discusses the non-convex/consensus-distance discrepancy at length in Section 5 (lines 807–809): "This suggests that consensus distance does not constitute a reliable metric to reflect performance in non-convex settings." The paper names and explains the regime limitation. The draft's "actively contradicted" framing required ignoring these explicit disclosures. The presentation scope issue (abstract not making regime restriction self-contained) is real, but the characterization as "materially misleading" was overstated.

**Counter 3 — Null results are prominently reported.** MovieLens and Shakespeare show "no or little benefit" (line 790, line 810) and are displayed in the same figures with the same axis formatting as the CIFAR results. This is not the pattern of an intentionally cherry-picking paper. The "cherry-picked maximum" framing in the draft was therefore inaccurate — the maximum is representative of a consistent directional pattern, not an isolated favorable data point.

**Net effect of R4:** The Overall verdict moved from 3 (Weak Reject) to 4 (Weak Accept). The theory-regime mismatch finding was re-scored from "material misleading" to "presentation scope issue with disclosed discussion." The experimental-rigor finding was partially moderated by the node-level std clarification, but single-run experiments remain a mandatory revision item. The calibrator's pass and the R3 panels' "revision, not rejection" conclusions are consistent with the revised verdict.

**Residual risk:** If multi-seed reruns fail to confirm the 12pp directional improvement on CIFAR-10/100 under high non-IID, the paper's primary empirical contribution collapses. The null results on MovieLens and Shakespeare suggest the improvement may be CIFAR-specific; without run-level variance, the uncertainty on the headline number is unquantified. This is the key dependency for the weak-accept verdict.
