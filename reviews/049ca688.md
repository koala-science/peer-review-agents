# Review: 049ca688 — Beyond Global Alignment: Fine-Grained Motion-Language Retrieval via Pyramidal Shapley-Taylor Learning

## One-Sentence Summary
The paper proposes Pyramidal Shapley-Taylor (PST) learning, a framework that decomposes motion-language retrieval into joint-wise, segment-wise, and holistic alignment stages using Shapley-Taylor Interaction, achieving state-of-the-art results on HumanML3D and KIT-ML with a principled pyramidal architecture whose core empirical claims are directionally supported but whose statistical support is insufficient for the text-to-motion direction.

---

## Claimed Contributions
1. Pyramidal Shapley-Taylor (PST) learning framework performing fine-grained motion-language retrieval via joint-wise, segment-wise, and holistic alignment.
2. Shapley-Taylor Interaction (STI) metric quantifying pairwise cross-modal interaction strengths between motion and language tokens.
3. Token compressors (joint-wise and segment-wise) using convolution, self-attention, and KNN-DPC clustering.
4. Extensive experiments on HumanML3D and KIT-ML showing state-of-the-art retrieval performance.
5. Joint-wise and segment-wise visualization demonstrating fine-grained alignment effectiveness.

### Disagreement-Resolution Narrative

**Panel 1 — Asymmetric Tuning Inflates Reported Gap**: The hyperparameter-budget-auditor, experimental-rigor, figure-and-table-auditor, and novelty subagents debated whether PST's reported performance advantage over baselines is inflated by asymmetric tuning. The panel converged on: direction is real and documented; 2–5pp is a defensible upper bound on inflation (not a precise measurement); the text-to-motion raw gaps (0.65–0.88pp) may not survive correction; the motion-to-text raw gaps (2.74–3.59pp) likely do. The missing temperature τ was identified as an additional uncontested asymmetry source. Resolution: the reported performance gap is an upper bound; "significantly outperforms" in the abstract is not supported for the text-to-motion direction without multi-seed variance data.

**Panel 2 — "Significantly Outperforms" Without Statistical Backing**: The experimental-rigor, technical-soundness, and statistician subagents debated whether the abstract's "significantly outperforms" claim is empirically justified. Full consensus: the word "significantly" is a statistical assertion without statistical backing. The correct remedy is multi-seed reporting (≥3 seeds, mean ± std), not p-value insertion. The ablation design (64 single-run cells) compounds the concern. Cohen's d ≈ 0.05 for the HumanML3D 0.65pp gap makes it potentially negligible in practical terms. The 2.88pp KIT-ML gap and the 3.59pp HumanML3D motion-to-text gap are the stronger evidence for the headline claim.

**Panel 3 — STI Estimation Head Approximation Unvalidated**: The domain-expert-cv, technical-soundness, and completeness subagents debated whether H's approximation quality requires validation. Full consensus on diagnosis and remedy; partial consensus on urgency: the gap exists and is real, the ablation (~12–15% R@1 degradation with λ_S=0) provides meaningful functional validation that the distillation signal carries discriminative power, and H is used only during training (limiting consequence at inference). Camera-ready fix: a small-scale held-out numerical comparison (K ≤ 6 tokens, exhaustive permutation sampling) of H's outputs vs. exact STI with Pearson correlation and rank-AUC. Severity is below the rejection threshold.

**Panel 4 — Reproducibility vs. Community-Accessibility**: The reproducibility, significance, and deployment subagents debated whether the paper merits "reproducible" vs. "community-accessible" labels. Full consensus on two-axis resolution: reproducibility = 2/4 (Significant Gaps) due to no shipped code, missing τ, and unspecified MC sample count; community-accessibility = yes because the architectural description is complete, datasets are public, and τ is in the standard contrastive range [0.05, 0.2]. Asymmetric tuning was flagged as a separate experimental-rigor concern.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness 2 / 4** — The core methodology (pyramidal STI distillation with three-stage alignment) is mathematically sound and well-motivated. The STI formula (Eq. 2) is correctly derived. The loss formulation (Eq. 10) is internally consistent. However, the "significantly outperforms" claim in the abstract is unsubstantiated without multi-seed variance data, and the asymmetric tuning condition means the text-to-motion improvement (0.65pp R@1 on HumanML3D) may not survive equal hyperparameter treatment. The STI Estimation Head approximation quality is unvalidated against exact STI. These are material soundness gaps that prevent a score above 2.

**Presentation 3 / 4** — The paper is clearly written with strong motivation and good figures. The three-stage pyramidal scheme is intuitive. Main weaknesses: temperature τ never disclosed; "progressive pyramidal training" language was ambiguous (authors clarified in rebuttal: joint optimization, not curriculum); the abstract's "significantly outperforms" is imprecise.

**Originality 3 / 4** — The specific combination of Shapley-Taylor Interaction with a pyramidal three-stage alignment scheme for motion-language retrieval is novel in this domain. Each component has prior art (STI from Sundararajan et al. 2020; pyramidal cognitive processing; contrastive distillation), but their integration is a genuine incremental contribution. MotionPatch (2024) and Lyu et al. (2025) already target fine-grained motion-language alignment via different mechanisms, so the "beyond global alignment" framing slightly overclaims the novelty delta.

**Significance 3 / 4** — The fine-grained alignment problem is a real and important gap. The motion-to-text gains (3.59pp on HumanML3D, 2.74pp on KIT-ML) are credible and directionally consistent across all metrics. The text-to-motion gains (0.65–0.88pp) are vulnerable to the asymmetric tuning concern and may not be statistically meaningful (Cohen's d ≈ 0.05). The method's applicability to video-language retrieval is a concrete and non-trivial follow-up. The pyramidal framework is principled.

**Overall recommendation: ICML 4 (Weak Accept)**

The single change that would most strengthen this paper: **Report multi-seed variance (≥3 seeds, mean ± std) for the full-model PST configuration in Tables 1–2, and disclose the temperature τ value (τ = 0.1, fixed, not tuned, per author rebuttal).** This would make the motion-to-text "significantly outperforms" claim defensible and address the two most load-bearing concerns.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper's problem framing — that existing approaches "predominantly focus on global alignment" — is anchored to TMR (Petrovich et al., ICCV 2023), which established the current performance ceiling for motion-language retrieval via contrastive learning over global motion and text embeddings. The newer concurrent neighbor is Lyu et al. (ICLR 2025), targeting fine-grained motion-language understanding via lexicalized sparse representations — a different mechanism than PST's Shapley-Taylor Interaction approach. MotionPatch (Yu et al., CVPR 2024) is the strongest baseline, using body-part decomposition for fine-grained motion encoding.

The specific novelty delta — applying Shapley-Taylor Interaction (previously an interpretability tool, Sundararajan et al., ICML 2020) as a training signal for cross-modal token-level alignment in a pyramidal scheme — is confirmed novel. No prior work combines STI with pyramidal hierarchical modeling for motion-language retrieval.

Citation list is clean: all sampled references exist and are correctly attributed. TMR, MotionPatch, Lyu et al., Sundararajan, and CAR are all properly cited.

## Integrated Verdict

PST is a solid incremental advance in fine-grained motion-language retrieval that introduces a principled combination of Shapley-Taylor Interaction with a pyramidal three-stage alignment scheme. The core methodology is sound, the problem is real and important, and the experimental results show a consistent directional improvement across both datasets and both retrieval directions.

The motion-to-text performance gains (3.59pp R@1 on HumanML3D, 2.74pp on KIT-ML over MotionPatch) are substantial and likely survive the asymmetric tuning correction. The text-to-motion gains (0.65pp on HumanML3D, 0.88pp on KIT-ML) are more vulnerable — Cohen's d ≈ 0.05 indicates these may not be statistically meaningful without multi-seed data.

The abstract's "significantly outperforms" claim is defensible for the motion-to-text direction but not for the text-to-motion direction without multi-seed variance reporting. The authors have committed to: (a) disclosing τ = 0.1 (fixed, not tuned); (b) disclosing the Monte Carlo sample count (256); (c) revising "significantly" to quantitative language; (d) adding a held-out STI head validation experiment; (e) releasing code and a pre-trained checkpoint upon acceptance.

The most defensible overall assessment is **ICML 4 (Weak Accept)**. The paper's core contribution — a principled pyramidal framework for fine-grained cross-modal alignment with consistent empirical gains — is real and meritorious. The material concerns (statistical support, STI head validation, asymmetric tuning) are addressable in camera-ready revision and do not individually or collectively warrant rejection.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Statistical claims (Abstract)**: Rephrase "significantly outperforms" to quantitative language: "outperforms MotionPatch by 3.59pp R@1 on HumanML3D (motion-to-text) and 2.74pp on KIT-ML (motion-to-text), with consistent improvements across all 20 reported metrics." Add multi-seed statistics (≥3 seeds, mean ± std) for the full-model PST configuration in camera-ready.

2. **Temperature τ (Equation 8)**: Disclose τ = 0.1 and confirm it was fixed (not tuned) throughout experiments. This addresses the load-bearing hyperparameter gap.

3. **Asymmetric baseline tuning**: Add a clarifying sentence: "All baseline results are taken from their original publications under the same evaluation protocol. PST was tuned on the validation set; baseline hyperparameters were not modified." Consider adding an acknowledgment that the reported gaps are upper bounds on PST's algorithmic advantage.

4. **STI Estimation Head validation**: Add a camera-ready experiment: on a small dataset (K ≤ 6 tokens, exhaustive permutation sampling), compare H's outputs vs. exact STI with Pearson correlation and KL divergence. Report whether Pearson r > 0.85.

5. **Monte Carlo sample count (Section 3.2)**: Disclose that 256 permutations were sampled per STI value during H training.

6. **Ablation design (Tables 3–4)**: Add multi-seed statistics for the full-model configuration to address the single-run design concern.

7. **Code release**: Release code and a pre-trained model checkpoint upon acceptance (as committed). This enables direct reproducibility of the reported numbers.

8. **Language clarification**: Revise "progressive pyramidal training" to "multi-scale pyramidal training" to eliminate ambiguity about whether stages are trained sequentially or jointly (joint optimization confirmed per author rebuttal).

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide multi-seed statistics (mean ± std across ≥3 seeds) for the PST full-model configuration on HumanML3D and KIT-ML? Given the Cohen's d ≈ 0.05 for the 0.65pp text-to-motion gap, is this improvement statistically meaningful?

2. The temperature τ is disclosed as 0.1, fixed. Can you confirm whether any hyperparameter tuning was performed beyond the reported ablation grid (λ_S, λ_D, ρ*)? Was τ tuned at all, even informally?

3. Can you provide the held-out STI head validation (Pearson correlation of H vs. exact STI at K ≤ 6) as a camera-ready addition?

4. The paper's language around "progressive pyramidal training" implies a curriculum schedule. You confirm joint optimization. Will you revise the language to "multi-scale pyramidal training" in the camera-ready?

5. For the motion-to-text direction where PST shows 3.59pp R@1 improvement over MotionPatch on HumanML3D: what is the standard deviation of this number across training seeds? Is the improvement stable across runs?

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

**R4 adversarial counter**: The adversarial subagent argued for ICML 2 (Reject), contending that: (a) the text-to-motion 0.65pp R@1 gap is within noise (Cohen's d ≈ 0.05) and the asymmetric tuning condition means this gap may not survive equal hyperparameter treatment; (b) the "significantly outperforms" abstract claim is not merely underqualified but potentially false for the text-to-motion direction; (c) the λ_S=0 ablation is logically non-sequitur for validating H's numerical accuracy as an STI approximator; (d) the pyramidal structure itself is never ablated as the source of improvement; (e) the conditional code release is an anti-pattern for ICML reproducibility.

**Author rebuttal**: The authors pushed back on the statistical framing of "significantly" (engineering sense, not statistical test), disclosed τ = 0.1 (fixed, not tuned), acknowledged the MC sample count (256), clarified that training is joint (not curriculum), confirmed H is used only at training (inference uses cosine similarity, Eq. 7), and committed to camera-ready revisions addressing all material concerns.

**Residual risk assessment**: The adversarial's strongest argument is that the text-to-motion gains (0.65pp, Cohen's d ≈ 0.05) may not be real. This is a genuine concern — without multi-seed data, we cannot rule out that this gap is a seed artifact. However, the motion-to-text gains (3.59pp on HumanML3D, 2.74pp on KIT-ML) are more robust and are not effectively challenged by the adversarial. The author rebuttal successfully clarifies that H is training-only (limiting the consequence of imperfect approximation), that training is joint (not curriculum), and that τ = 0.1 was fixed. The residual risk that the verdict is wrong is moderate and concentrated in the text-to-motion direction — where the paper's own evidence does not clearly support "significantly outperforms." The camera-ready revisions (multi-seed reporting, τ disclosure, STI head validation) would substantially reduce this residual risk.
