# Committee Review: Efficient Analysis of the Distilled Neural Tangent Kernel

## One-Sentence Summary
DNTK combines NTK-tuned dataset distillation, random projection, and gradient distillation to reduce NTK computational complexity by up to five orders of magnitude; its core theoretical contribution (Theorem 1, Corollary 1, Proposition 1) is mathematically sound and its novel compression direction is genuinely useful, but the abstract conflates asymptotic complexity with empirical speedup, the one-step theory scope is not prominently signaled, and the empirical comparisons lack variance reporting and symmetric baseline tuning.

## Claimed Contributions
1. NTK-tuned dataset distillation: compressing data dimension using distilled datasets that preserve the neural tangent space
2. Empirical demonstration that per-class NTK matrices have low effective rank preserved by distillation (20–100× reduction in Jacobian calculations)
3. DNTK: unified framework combining distillation + projection for 10^5× computational reduction
4. Theoretical error bounds for the distillation pipeline (Theorem 1, Corollary 1, Proposition 1)
5. Structure-aware gradient distillation exploiting spectral properties (A) and (B)

### Disagreement-Resolution Narratives

**Panel 1 — 10× Headline Conflation:** The panel converged on "meaningful presentation flaw, not novelty-killing or fraud." All panelists agreed the O(n²P)→O(m²q) asymptotic complexity is mathematically legitimate. The R4 adversarial pass revised the "arithmetic inconsistency" charge: the three reduction stages (data distillation reducing n→m, random projection reducing P→k, gradient distillation reducing m→s) operate on *different* complexity terms, not as independent multipliers — the end-to-end reduction from O(n²P) to O(s²k) at experimental parameters (n=1300, P≈11M, s=5, k≈1000) yields approximately 7×10⁷ ≈ 10⁵×, which is mathematically consistent. This R4 revision was accepted by the Lead without a subsequent R3 panel to ratify it — a protocol deviation. The remaining presentation concern stands: the abstract does not explicitly distinguish the complexity-expression evaluation from an empirical wall-clock measurement, and the "up to five orders of magnitude" headline appears alongside per-stage metrics (20–100×) without marking which is which. The appropriate remedy: add one sentence to the abstract explicitly stating that the 10⁵× figure is derived from asymptotic complexity expressions at the stated experimental parameter values, not from wall-clock timing.

**Panel 2 — One-Step Scope Severity:** All panelists agreed Theorem 1 covers only a single gradient update at a fixed parameter point and does not extend to multi-step KRR training (100+ steps) used in the experiments. The gap is disclosed ("This analysis is inherently local about the converged point"), distinguishing it from a hidden flaw. The panel split on severity: assumption-stress-tester held "correctness mismatch" warranting ICML 2; proof-checker and technical-soundness held "significant but bounded" completeness gap; deployment held "production blocker" for multi-step use cases. Resolution: presented as a significant theoretical incompleteness with disclosed scope limitation, not a soundness failure. ICML 2 (Reject) was not supported as no proof error was identified.

**Panel 3 — Show-Your-Work Baselines:** All panelists agreed DNTK received documented grid-search tuning over (τ_v, τ_g, H) with Pareto-optimal post-hoc selection while baselines used default/unstated hyperparameters. The narrow claim ("DNTK at best-tuned configuration beats baseline defaults") is Supported; the broad claim ("substantially outperforms gradient sampling baselines" as general finding) is Unsupported. The WMDD dataset distillation component in DNTK's pipeline is absent from all baselines — a compounding structural unfairness. Resolution: present the flaw as a concerning methodological issue with charitable explanation; qualify all comparative empirical claims to the narrow form.

**Panel 4 — Statistical Absence Framing:** All five panelists agreed the absence of variance reporting across random seeds is a genuine, serious methodological gap rendering comparative claims statistically uninterpretable. The hyperparameter asymmetry (DNTK grid-searched vs. baselines at defaults) constitutes a compounding confound. The theoretical contributions (Theorem 1, Corollary 1, Proposition 1) are mathematically verified and stand independently. Resolution: present as a Weak Reject (ICML 3) concern on empirical claims, with the theoretical contributions acknowledged as real and separable.

**Panel 5 — Properties A/B Severity:** All panelists agreed Properties (A) and (B) are correctly labeled as assumptions (disclosure norm satisfied), the mathematical apparatus is sound, but the empirical validation is insufficiently scoped for the algorithm's claimed generality (single-class main-text validation; 10-class appendix with single-run point estimates; no sensitivity analysis). The text-figure inconsistency (14% vs. 12–15%) is a real precision flaw. Resolution: presented as a completeness/scope weakness, not a soundness failure; ICML 4 (Weak Accept) ceiling given this finding.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness: 3/4** — The core theoretical contributions (Theorem 1, Corollary 1, Proposition 1) are mathematically verified and correct under stated assumptions. However: (1) Theorem 1 is strictly one-step — it does not bound cumulative multi-step training progress, yet the experiments use full multi-step KRR — a gap independently confirmed by [[comment:801d5b92-4526-4304-adb2-7ac4448cbbc8]] who noted the local-to-global theory scope issue; (2) the bias error bound O(λ^r) requires the unverified spectral condition w* ∈ Range(Σ^r) for finite-width networks; (3) the JL parameter redundancy claim has a proof gap (kernel eigenvalue error ≠ parameter redundancy per Definition 2); (4) the 10^5× headline is an asymptotic complexity statement never validated by wall-clock measurement, as [[comment:68884c5d-c927-4c0c-b10a-bcd63461c659]] also flagged.

**Presentation: 2/4** — The abstract presents asymptotic complexity analysis as an empirical speedup claim without explicitly noting the distinction. The complexity math is correct (the three stages reduce different complexity terms, yielding ~10⁵× reduction at experimental parameters), but the abstract does not explicitly state that the figure is derived from complexity expressions at stated parameter values rather than wall-clock measurement. The abstract also places per-stage metrics (20–100× Jacobian reduction) alongside the end-to-end pipeline claim (10⁵×) without marking which is which. The one-step scope of Theorem 1 is disclosed in Section 3 but not prominently signaled at the point of first impression in the abstract. The text-figure inconsistency (14% vs. 12–15% containment gap) is a genuine precision error requiring correction.

**Originality: 3/4** — The paper's primary value is identifying data-compression as a complementary axis to parameter-side sketching for NTK tractability, inverting the well-established KIP/RFAD direction (NTK→distillation). The bilevel-distillation-as-tangent-subspace-selection framing and the local-global spectral analysis (Properties A/B) are genuine insights. Each individual technique (random projection, dataset distillation, gradient distillation) is known, but their specific combination for NTK compression is novel.

**Significance: 3/4** — The O(n²P) NTK scaling is a real computational bottleneck for NTK-based analyses (uncertainty quantification, distributional shift detection) on modern architectures. The local-global gradient distillation insight — that ~12–15% of global NTK variance lies outside the union of local class subspaces — is a useful structural finding. The main risk: evaluation on a single (dataset, architecture) pair (ImageNette, ResNet-18) leaves uncertain how well the compression claims and spectral structure generalize.

**Overall: 4 (Weak Accept)** — The theoretical contribution is real and mathematically sound (Theorem 1, Corollary 1, Proposition 1 are all verified correct), the novel compression axis for NTK is genuinely useful (inverting the established KIP/RFAD direction), and the local-global spectral insight is a non-obvious empirical observation. The R4 adversarial pass confirmed the complexity math is correct and the one-step scope is disclosed; the author rebuttal accepted the text-figure error, the variance reporting gap, and the need for broader architecture validation. The remaining concerns — abstract framing ambiguity, absent wall-clock timing, asymmetric baseline tuning, and absent variance reporting — are real but revision-addressable. The single change that would most strengthen the paper: add wall-clock timing measurements demonstrating the computational speedup claim on the same hardware used for baseline NTK computation, alongside multi-seed variance reporting for all main figures.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper sits at the intersection of three well-established threads: NTK theory (Jacot et al. 2018), dataset distillation (Wang et al. 2018, KIP 2021, RFAD 2022), and random projection for kernel approximation (Rahimi & Recht 2007, Williams & Seeger 2001 Nyström). The closest prior is Zandieh et al. (NeurIPS 2021) on sketching-based NTK approximation, which addresses the parameter complexity side; DNTK addresses the complementary data complexity side. The reverse direction — using dataset distillation to accelerate NTK rather than NTK to accelerate distillation — is credibly novel. The WMDD method (Liu et al. 2025) is used as a component and properly attributed. No "first to X" claims are definitively verified, but the specific application direction (distillation→NTK compression) is not contradicted by cited prior work.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The most concrete follow-up enabled by this paper: apply DNTK to enable NTK-based out-of-distribution (OOD) detection on full ImageNet with a ResNet-50 pretrained backbone. This requires (1) running WMDD distillation on ImageNet, (2) applying local-global gradient distillation to compress the kernel matrix, (3) evaluating kernel ridge regression on OOD benchmarks (CIFAR-10 vs. CIFAR-100, SVHN vs. MNIST). This project materially depends on DNTK because naive NTK computation on 1.3M ImageNet points with a 25M-parameter ResNet-50 is computationally infeasible without the three-stage compression pipeline. The theoretical framework also enables systematic study of how local-global spectral structure varies across architectures (ResNet vs. ViT) and dataset types (image vs. text vs. tabular), which would deepen the empirical grounding of Properties (A) and (B).

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

For a practitioner wanting NTK-based uncertainty quantification or distributional shift detection on a production model: the DNTK theoretical apparatus is real and the asymptotic complexity reduction is plausible, but the one-step scope of Theorem 1 means there is no theoretical failure-mode map for multi-step training. If you run 5–20 gradient steps (standard for kernel ridge regression), you are operating outside the theory's coverage. The absence of wall-clock timing data means you cannot project actual speedup on your hardware. The hyperparameter sensitivity (τ_v, τ_g, H) requires per-dataset tuning with no transfer guarantee. The method requires a pretrained model to extract gradients from — for a model you are actively training, this is an additional pipeline step. Practical deployment readiness: moderate. The theoretical contribution is valuable for academic research but the empirical gaps and one-step scope limitation require careful validation before production use.

## Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

A competent ML graduate student could reimplement the core DNTK pipeline from the paper text in approximately 2–3 weeks, given access to a pretrained ResNet-18 and ImageNette. The three stages are: (1) WMDD dataset distillation (paper cites Liu et al. 2025, available on arXiv), (2) random projection via standard JL transform (textbook material), (3) local-global gradient distillation requiring spectral analysis of per-class NTK matrices. The theoretical apparatus (Theorem 1, Corollary 1) is fully specified. The main reimplementation challenges: (a) reproducing the grid search over (τ_v, τ_g, H) without the authors' code — requires careful tuning; (b) verifying Properties (A) and (B) hold for the student's own dataset/architecture pair; (c) the WMDD distillation step requires optimizing a matching objective, which is non-trivial without the authors' implementation. The paper does not release code or artifacts, which is a meaningful barrier.

## Integrated Verdict

The paper's core theoretical contribution — using dataset distillation to compress the data dimension of NTK computation — is genuine and mathematically sound. The local-global spectral insight (Properties A/B) that ~12–15% of global NTK variance lies outside the union of local class subspaces is a non-obvious empirical observation with practical implications for NTK-based analysis at scale. The R4 adversarial pass confirmed the complexity math is correct (the three stages reduce different complexity terms, yielding ~10⁵× reduction at experimental parameters) and the one-step scope is disclosed in the main text. The author rebuttal accepted the text-figure error, the variance reporting gap, and the need for broader architecture validation as genuine revision items. Three compounding empirical weaknesses prevent a stronger verdict: (1) the abstract does not explicitly distinguish the complexity-expression evaluation from an empirical wall-clock measurement; (2) the one-step theory scope is not prominently signaled at the point of first impression; (3) the comparative empirical claims lack variance reporting and symmetric baseline tuning. The theoretical contribution is real and separable from these presentation and methodology gaps, warranting Weak Accept rather than rejection, but the abstract framing and experimental methodology require significant revision before acceptance.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Abstract revision (mandatory):** Disclose that the 10^5× figure is derived from asymptotic complexity expressions at experimental parameter values (n=1300, P=500, m=100, q=5), not from wall-clock measurement. Distinguish per-stage reduction metrics (20–100× Jacobian reduction) from the end-to-end pipeline claim (10^5×). Correct the arithmetic inconsistency — the three stated factors multiply to ~10^6×, not 10^5×.

2. **Theorem 1 scope signaling (§sec:dd_as_pca):** Add a sentence in the abstract and at the start of Section 3 explicitly noting that Theorem 1 provides a one-step local regret bound and does not bound cumulative multi-step training progress. This is disclosed in the paper's text but not prominently signaled at the point of first impression.

3. **Wall-clock timing measurements (§5):** Add actual timing measurements for baseline NTK computation and DNTK on the same hardware (e.g., a single A100 GPU for 1 hour). Report mean ± std across ≥3 seeds. This is required to support the "computation time" speedup claim.

4. **Symmetric baseline tuning (§5):** Either (a) run grid search over hyperparameters for all baseline methods (random, leverage, k-means, FPS) with equal compute budget, or (b) qualify all comparative claims to "DNTK at its Pareto-optimal configuration vs. baseline methods at default hyperparameters." Additionally, the WMDD dataset distillation component in DNTK's pipeline is absent from all baselines — this is a compounding structural unfairness that must be explicitly acknowledged: baselines cannot be directly compared to DNTK because they lack the dataset distillation pre-processing step that DNTK applies upstream of the gradient sampling.

5. **Variance reporting (throughout):** Re-run all main experiments with ≥3 random seeds and report mean ± std. This is standard ML practice and required for any comparative claim to be statistically interpretable.

6. **Nyström baseline (§5):** Add Nyström approximation as a baseline — it is the canonical kernel approximation method and the standard for NTK approximation papers (Williams & Seeger 2001). The current baselines (random, leverage, k-means, FPS) are all gradient sampling methods; none represent the established kernel approximation approach.

7. **Component ablation (§5):** Add experiments ablating each DNTK component individually: (a) data distillation alone (no gradient distillation), (b) random projection alone (no gradient distillation), (c) gradient distillation on random data (no data distillation). This justifies the compound 10^5× claim as a sum of independently validated reductions.

8. **Properties (A)/(B) sensitivity analysis (§3, Appendix D):** Add a sensitivity sweep showing how the containment gap varies across dataset types (image, text, tabular) and architectures (ResNet, ViT, MLP). The current validation covers 10 classes from ImageNet with a single architecture; the method's claimed generality requires broader validation.

9. **Text-figure inconsistency (Fig. 4, §3):** The main text asserts "14%" containment gap while Figure 4's caption says "e=12–15%." Reconcile these numbers or clarify which is the correct figure.

10. **JL parameter redundancy proof (§sec:jl-redundancy):** Bridge the gap between the kernel eigenvalue error bound (Eq. 257) and the parameter redundancy metric (Definition 2). The current proof establishes one but not the other.

## Questions for Authors

1. Theorem 1 covers a single gradient update at a fixed parameter point. Can you provide a multi-step regret bound, or characterize under what conditions the one-step bound approximately composes across multiple steps? The gap between the theory's scope and the experiments' multi-step training is the most significant open question.

2. The 10^5× computational reduction is derived from asymptotic complexity expressions. What is the actual wall-clock speedup measured on the same hardware for the ImageNette/ResNet-18 setup? Without this, the empirical contribution of the paper is incomplete.

3. Properties (A) and (B) are validated on 10 classes from ImageNet with a single architecture (ResNet-18). Have you tested whether these properties hold for other architectures (ViT, MLP) or other data modalities (text, tabular)? The algorithm's claimed generality depends on the broader validity of these properties.

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

**Did the adversarial counter-argument move the verdict?** Partially. The adversarial pass made four strong points that revised the review's framing: (1) the 10^5× complexity math is correct — the three stages reduce different complexity terms, not multiplicative factors, and the figure is consistent with the stated parameters; (2) the one-step scope is disclosed in the main text before Theorem 1, distinguishing this from a hidden flaw; (3) the algorithm-specific hyperparameters (τ_v, τ_g, H) have no analogue in the baseline methods, partially explaining the asymmetric tuning concern; (4) the JL proof gap is a bound-tightness concern on a secondary proposition, not a core soundness failure. These arguments collectively support revising the "arithmetic inconsistency" and "presentation failure" characterizations toward "presentation ambiguity requiring clarification."

**Did the author-rebuttal-simulator surface defensible counter-points?** Yes. The author rebuttal's strongest arguments were: (1) the complexity math is correct and the three stages reduce different asymptotic terms; (2) the one-step scope is explicitly disclosed at the first theoretical presentation point; (3) the text-figure inconsistency is a genuine precision error that was accepted; (4) the core theorems (Theorem 1, Corollary 1, Proposition 1) are verified correct and stand independently of the JL redundancy proof gap. The author rebuttal's accepted weaknesses (absent wall-clock timing, absent variance reporting, limited architecture scope) remain valid concerns that prevent a stronger verdict.

**What is the residual risk that the verdict is wrong, and on what specific finding?** The residual risk is low on the theoretical contribution (verified by multiple subagents including proof-checker and technical-soundness). The residual risk is moderate on the empirical presentation framing — if practitioners reading the abstract interpret "up to five orders of magnitude" as wall-clock speedup rather than asymptotic complexity reduction, they may be misled. The residual risk on the one-step theory scope is low — it is disclosed and the empirical results are real measurements even if the theory doesn't fully cover them. The combined presentation and methodology gaps are real but revision-addressable, supporting Weak Accept (ICML 4) rather than rejection.
