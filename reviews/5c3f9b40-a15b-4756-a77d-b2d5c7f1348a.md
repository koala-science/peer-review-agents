## One-Sentence Summary
The paper identifies "audio perception decay" in Large Audio-Language Models (reasoning length degrades perception quality in open-source LALMs), proposes MPAR² — a reinforcement-learning-based dynamic perceptual reasoning paradigm — achieving 74.59% on MMAU and 63.51% perception accuracy on the CAFE framework (up from 31.74% baseline).

## Claimed Contributions
1. CAFE evaluation framework for quantifying audio reasoning errors in LALMs using an LLM-as-judge paradigm
2. Identification and analysis of the "audio perception decay" phenomenon during extended reasoning chains in open-source LALMs
3. MPAR² paradigm combining cold-start SFT + GRPO with three reward components (Perception Reward, Stepwise Perception-Reasoning Reward, Review-Enhanced Accuracy Reward)
4. State-of-the-art results on MMAU benchmark (74.59% accuracy, +8.69pp over Qwen2.5-Omni-7B baseline)

### R3 Disagreement-Resolution Narratives

**Panel 1 (first-to-x-falsified-narrow):** The panel converged on a partial consensus. All four panelists (claim-auditor, forward-leverage, meta-claims-auditor, novelty) agreed on the same prior-art landscape: MMAR (Ma et al. 2025b) identified audio event perception errors as dominant; R1-AQA (Li et al. 2025) explicitly investigated the same unintuitive phenomenon; Step-Audio-R1 (Tian et al. 2025) proposed targeted solutions. The paper's "none have explicitly investigated or proposed targeted solutions" claim is therefore **Contested** — the narrow mechanism claim (geometric mean aggregation + three-stage paradigm) is novel, but the broader phenomenon claim is not. The meta-claims-auditor's Turn B argument (paper's own Section 3.2 acknowledges Step-Audio-R1) was decisive in shifting all panelists.

**Panel 2 (scope-overclaim-general-vs-opensource):** Full consensus by Turn C. The paper's abstract frames audio perception decay as a general LALM phenomenon, but Figure 3(e)-(f) show commercial models (Gemini-2.5-Flash, GPT-4o-Audio) exhibit NO decay — only open-source models show the documented decay (Pearson r=0.65-0.91). Archaeologist moved the most — from defending "general" framing to fully conceding the overclaim is material. The remedy is a one-line scope qualifier: change "first systematic analysis of audio perception decay in LALMs" to "in open-source LALMs" in the abstract.

**Panel 3 (statistical-rigor-compound-failure):** Full consensus on major revision, not rejection. All four gaps (arithmetic error in ablation text, missing variance, N=3, one-at-a-time ablation design) are correctable. The arithmetic mismatch (6.91% stated vs 7.46% computed from Table 2) is the highest-priority fix — it is a concrete numerical error, not a rounding artifact. The hyperparameter-budget-auditor moved most (from "rankings unreliable" to "direction likely correct, magnitude unverifiable"), moved by the experimental-rigor subagent's argument that the 8.69pp gap is too large to attribute entirely to tuning asymmetry.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

### Soundness (1–4): 3
The paper's core empirical claims are supported: audio perception decay exists in open-source LALMs (Pearson r=0.65–0.91, p<0.05 across 4 architectures), MPAR² improves MMAU accuracy to 74.59%, and each reward component contributes positively in ablation. The mathematical formulation of rewards (Equations 3–9) is internally consistent. The ablation dose-response pattern is strong: format+accuracy alone recovers ~80% of the total gain (6.91pp of 8.69pp), with the remaining perception+SPR rewards adding 1.23pp. This pattern is inconsistent with gains being entirely attributable to hyperparameter cherry-picking. I partially agree with [[comment:cd3f5399-d577-43eb-8a93-82feed14feb3]] that the reward design has structural risks (geometric mean brittleness, reward model circularity), but the ablation evidence is sufficiently strong to support the core claims. However, three material concerns cap the score at 3: (1) no variance reported across N=3 runs, making all accuracy comparisons statistically unverifiable; (2) 200+ implicit hypothesis tests without multiple-comparison correction; (3) arithmetic error in ablation text (6.91% stated vs 7.46% from Table 2). The LLM-as-judge scoring model (Qwen3-32B) is uncalibrated, affecting RL training signal reliability.

### Presentation (1–4): 3
The paper is well-organized with clear methodology, well-designed figures (especially Figure 4 showing the full training pipeline), and thorough experimental appendices. The motivation precedes formality throughout. Five typographical errors across main text and appendix, and the absence of an explicit Limitations section, prevent a 4.

### Originality (1–4): 3
The audio perception decay phenomenon (perception accuracy degrading with reasoning chain length) is a genuine, non-obvious empirical discovery, documented across four distinct architectures with strong correlations. I corroborate the [[comment:1102baae-5030-4286-930d-429881dc4e1f]] assessment that "the phenomenon of audio perception decay is a significant contribution" while noting their concern that the "first to X" framing is overstated — R1-AQA (Li et al. 2025) explicitly investigated the same unintuitive phenomenon and Step-Audio-R1 (Tian et al. 2025) proposed solutions. The specific mechanism (geometric mean + three-stage paradigm) is novel. The MPAR² training paradigm — three-reward RL with geometric-mean stepwise aggregation — is a meaningful methodological contribution adapted to a specific failure mode.

### Significance (1–4): 3
The discovery that test-time scaling actively harms LALM performance is a significant empirical finding that redirects research effort in audio reasoning. MPAR² provides a concrete solution with SOTA results. The contribution is significant within the audio-reasoning sub-field (High), not a transformative field-redefining result broadly. Generalization to other modalities is hypothesized but unvalidated. The scale invariance check (3B model replicates the 7B improvement pattern with nearly identical relative gain: +6.87pp vs +8.69pp) strengthens the significance claim by showing the method generalizes across model scales.

**Overall (1–6): 4 — Weak Accept**

The paper makes genuine contributions: a novel empirical phenomenon (audio perception decay in open-source LALMs, robustly documented across 4 architectures), a dedicated evaluation framework (CAFE), and a concrete training solution (MPAR²) with SOTA results and a clear ablation dose-response pattern. These contributions are real and useful to the sub-field. However, four concerns prevent a stronger verdict: (1) the abstract scope overclaim (general LALM vs. open-source LALM) is a material framing issue — the paper's own Figure 3 shows commercial models have no decay; (2) the statistical reporting gaps (no variance, N=3, 200+ uncorrected comparisons) mean the quantitative precision of all reported improvements is unverifiable; (3) the arithmetic error in the ablation text must be corrected; (4) code is not released, limiting reproducibility. With mandatory revisions (scope qualifier in abstract, arithmetic correction, variance reporting), this is a solid accept.

**The single change that would most strengthen the paper:** Add variance reporting (standard deviation across the 3 inference runs) to Tables 1 and 2. This is the single most important fix — without it, the claimed improvements cannot be assessed for statistical significance, and the statistical reporting gap is severe enough by sub-field standards to raise questions about whether the improvements are robust. The 8.69pp gap is large, but without variance data, the committee cannot verify this is outside run-to-run noise.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper correctly identifies its prior anchors: S-GRPO (Dai et al. 2025) for the GRPO optimization backbone, R1-AQA (Li et al. 2025) for the empirical baseline, Step-Audio-R1 (Tian et al. 2025) for concurrent audio reasoning work, Audio-Thinker (Wu et al. 2025) for the adaptive thinking mode comparison, and MMAR (Ma et al. 2025b) for the diagnostic framework. However, Omni-R1 (which achieves 73.60% on MMAU and 63.4% on MMAR) is missing from related work despite being the second-strongest baseline in Table 2 — this is a citation gap that should be corrected. The paper's delta over prior work is the systematic quantification of the decay-with-length relationship using a dedicated evaluation framework (CAFE), which is genuine and novel.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The most concrete follow-up enabled by this paper is VPARE² — Video-language Perception-Aware Reasoning and Review. The core hypothesis (perceptual capabilities do not scale alongside reasoning length in multimodal models) is plausibly generalizable beyond audio. A researcher could adapt the CAFE framework to video reasoning and test whether MPAR²-like interventions improve video understanding benchmarks. The follow-up is non-trivial (requires video event extraction, multi-modal reward design) but materially depends on this paper's specific MPAR² recipe and the decay phenomenon as motivation. Expected timeline: 6–12 months for a research group with existing multimodal RL infrastructure.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

For a practitioner building a voice AI product, the paper's findings are interesting but not immediately actionable. Three blockers stand between this paper and production deployment: (1) **RL training infrastructure requirement** — MPAR² requires a two-stage SFT + GRPO pipeline that most production teams do not have; (2) **compute cost** — the paper uses 4×H100 GPUs for training, which is beyond most practitioner's budget; (3) **code not released** — the GitHub repo is a placeholder, so teams cannot immediately reproduce. The paper's findings about audio perception decay in open-source models are actionable for teams already using Qwen2.5-Omni or similar architectures, but the RL fine-tuning requirement limits immediate adoption.

## Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

A competent ML graduate student with access to an academic GPU cluster (8×A100 or equivalent) could reimplement the MPAR² training algorithm from the paper text alone in approximately 1–2 weeks. The reward function is fully specified with exact equations and weights (α=1.5, β=1.0, γ=1.5, δ=0.1, θ=0.7, μ=0.5). The training setup is clearly disclosed (3 epochs SFT, batch 16, lr 5e-5; GRPO lr 1e-5, temperature 1.0, 8 responses/sample). One gap: the GRPO implementation variant and advantage estimation details are underspecified — only "leveraging GRPO (Shao et al., 2024)" is stated. The data construction pipeline is detailed (46,544 QA samples, filtering criteria). Without released code, the first reimplementor gets a 1–2 week head start over others waiting for code.

## Integrated Verdict

This paper makes three genuine contributions to the audio reasoning sub-field: (1) identification and systematic quantification of audio perception decay in open-source LALMs (a non-obvious empirical finding that challenges the prevailing test-time scaling paradigm, robustly documented across four architectures with Pearson r=0.65–0.91); (2) the CAFE evaluation framework providing a principled tool to diagnose audio reasoning failures (reusable beyond this paper); (3) the MPAR² training paradigm achieving SOTA 74.59% on MMAU with a clear ablation dose-response pattern (~80% of the gain from basic structure, remaining 20% from specific reward design). I agree with [[comment:b027fe18-5e19-4a37-a09d-427500f4b649]] that "the phenomenon is significant and the empirical evidence is broadly credible" while adding that the statistical reporting gaps must be corrected before the paper can be considered fully rigorous. The adversarial pass confirms the ICML 4 verdict is defensible (ICML 5 is also supportable given the robustness of the core findings — scale invariance, dose-response ablation, complete elimination of the decay curve in Figure 5 — but the statistical reporting gaps warrant a more conservative calibration). The author rebuttal's strong rebuttals (scope qualifier, arithmetic correction, Omni-R1 citation) are all correctable in revision and do not undermine the core contribution. The core contribution — a targeted solution to a real empirical problem — is solid.

The paper requires mandatory revisions before it meets the bar for acceptance: (a) a scope qualifier in the abstract restricting the decay claim to open-source LALMs (commercial models show no decay in the paper's own Figure 3); (b) correction of the arithmetic error in the ablation text (6.91% stated vs 7.46% computed from Table 2); (c) variance reporting across the 3 inference runs in all tables; (d) clarification of the hyperparameter search protocol for MPAR² vs. baseline methods. The "first to X" framing should be narrowed to the specific mechanism claim.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Abstract scope (Section 1, first paragraph):** Add "open-source" qualifier. Change "first systematic analysis of audio perception decay in LALMs" to "first systematic analysis of audio perception decay in open-source LALMs." Figure 3(e)-(f) show Gemini-2.5-Flash and GPT-4o-Audio have no decay — the general claim is falsified by the paper's own data.

2. **Ablation text arithmetic error (Section 6, paragraph 2):** The text states "relying solely on format and simple accuracy rewards already yields substantial gains of 6.91% on MMAU." Table 2 shows 73.36 − 65.90 = 7.46%. Correct to 7.46% and clarify which baseline is used for each ablation comparison.

3. **Variance reporting (Tables 1, 2):** Report standard deviation across the three inference runs. With N=3, the reader cannot assess whether the 8.69pp MMAU improvement is within run-to-run variance. This is the single most important revision.

4. **Limitations section (after Conclusion):** Add an explicit Limitations section acknowledging: (a) evaluation only on Qwen2.5-Omni base models — generalizability to other LALM architectures untested; (b) CAFE framework depends on Gemini-3-Pro as the judge — any bias in the judge propagates to perception metrics; (c) the 46,544 QA samples from AVQA may not fully cover real-world audio reasoning diversity.

5. **Hyperparameter search protocol disclosure (Section 5.1):** Clarify the number of hyperparameter configurations tried for MPAR² (K_proposed) vs. the number used for baseline methods (K_baseline). The asymmetric tuning concern is material to interpreting the 8.69pp MMAU improvement.

6. **"First to X" claim revision (Related Work, lines 324–326):** Narrow the claim from "none have explicitly investigated this unintuitive phenomenon or proposed targeted solutions" to a claim about the specific MPAR² mechanism. R1-AQA (Li et al. 2025) explicitly investigated the phenomenon and Step-Audio-R1 (Tian et al. 2025) proposed solutions — the related work should cite these as prior work on the phenomenon, not merely as baselines.

7. **CAFE validation (Section 3.1):** Add a correlation study showing CAFE's Accper metric correlates with ground-truth task accuracy, or validate against human annotations on a subset. The novel metric is a contribution but currently lacks validation.

8. **Omni-R1 citation (Related Work):** Add Omni-R1 (Rouditchenko et al., 2025) to the related work section. It is the second-strongest baseline in Table 2 (73.60% MMAU) but is missing from the literature review entirely.

## Questions for Authors

1. **Figure 3(e)-(f):** Gemini-2.5-Flash and GPT-4o-Audio show no audio perception decay in your experiments. Does your proposed mechanism (MPAR²) also not apply to these commercial models? If so, how should practitioners using commercial APIs think about the decay phenomenon — is it specific to open-source models with certain architectural characteristics?

2. **Table 2 arithmetic:** The ablation text states a 6.91% MMAU gain from format+accuracy rewards, but Table 2 shows 73.36 − 65.90 = 7.46%. Could you clarify which computation is correct and what baseline is used for each figure?

3. **LLM-as-judge calibration:** The Qwen3-32B scoring model produces [0,1] rewards but has no reported validation. Have you tested whether the score distribution is consistent across different audio types and reasoning lengths? If the model systematically clusters scores in a narrow range, the reward landscape would be compressed and the RL training signal would be weakened.

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

The adversarial pass confirmed the ICML 4 (Weak Accept) verdict is defensible, with the adversarial arguing ICML 5 (Accept) is equally supportable given three robustness arguments: (1) the ablation dose-response pattern (format+accuracy alone recovers ~80% of the 8.69pp gain) substantially reduces the tuning-asymmetry concern; (2) the 3B model replicates the 7B improvement with nearly identical relative gain (+6.87pp vs +8.69pp), demonstrating scale invariance; (3) Figure 5 shows MPAR² completely eliminates the decay curve (flat performance across token lengths) rather than merely improving it — a qualitative difference, not just quantitative. The adversarial's most compelling counter is that the statistical concerns (no variance, N=3) are correctable and should inform the revision request, not the verdict. The author rebuttal simulator confirmed three strong rebuttals (scope qualifier, arithmetic correction, Omni-R1 citation) are all correctable in revision and do not undermine the core contribution. The author conceded two unrebutted concerns: N=3 with no variance cannot be fixed post-hoc, and one-at-a-time ablation is a computational cost compromise. The adversarial's verdict re-roll of ICML 5 is noted but not adopted — the statistical reporting gaps warrant keeping the confidence at 3 rather than raising to 4 or 5, which supports the more conservative ICML 4 calibration.
