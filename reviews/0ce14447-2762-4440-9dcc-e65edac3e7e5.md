## One-Sentence Summary
Neural network weight signs are spectrally indistinguishable from i.i.d. Rademacher noise yet persist strongly from initialization; the paper formalizes this as "sign lock-in theory" via stopping-time analysis (geometric tail bound on effective sign flips) and proposes gap initialization plus outer-drift regularization to suppress flips, reducing the one-bit wall bottleneck in sub-bit model compression.

## Claimed Contributions
1. **Empirical discovery**: Learned weight signs resist low-rank approximation and are spectrally noise-like across Transformers, CNNs, and MLPs; sign patterns remain largely inherited from initialization (flip ratio well below 0.5 throughout training).
2. **Sign lock-in theory**: A stopping-time analysis of sign dynamics under SGD noise proving that effective outer-to-outer sign flip counts exhibit a geometric tail under two verifiable conditions (bounded-update and re-entry bound).
3. **Lock-in enhancement**: Gap-based initialization (rejection-sampling to keep weights away from zero) and a lightweight log-barrier outer-drift regularizer reduce effective flip rate to ~10⁻³ with ~1-point perplexity overhead.

### R3 Disagreement-Resolution Narrative

**Panel 1 — AdamW Optimizer Gap (adamw-optimizer-gap):**
The panel debated whether the theory-to-experiments bridge is undermined by using AdamW in billion-scale experiments when the sufficient conditions are proven only for SGD. All four panelists (novelty, technical-soundness, assumption-stress-tester, domain-expert-theory) converged by Turn C on the following factual basis: (1) Theorem 1 is a valid conditional result — the proof is sound under Assumptions 1-2; (2) the optimizer gap is an assumption-specification failure, not a proof bug — the sufficient conditions are proven for SGD, absent for AdamW; (3) the "optimizer-agnostic" remark conflates theorem syntax (optimizer-agnostic: true) with proven practical scope (theory applies to AdamW at scale: unverified); (4) the empirical ĝ→0 observation at billion scale under AdamW is real and valuable but is currently *observed but not formally explained* for the AdamW setting; (5) the gap is not fatal to acceptance — it is material, in principle closable, and the paper's core theoretical contribution stands independently. Severity remained mildly contested (High vs. Medium-High vs. Medium), tracking onto the useful distinction: High for the theory-to-experiments bridge (Claim B), Medium for the theoretical contribution standing alone (Claim A). The panel recommends: add an explicit Limitations entry acknowledging the AdamW sufficient condition gap, and either derive sufficient conditions for AdamW or qualify the "optimizer-agnostic" claim to specify proven optimizer scope.

**Panel 2 — g_T Brittleness / Vacuity (gt-brittleness-vacuity):**
The panel debated whether the theorem's assumptions permit vacuous parameter regimes (g_T→1⁻ producing P[K_eff_T ≥ 100] ≤ 0.60) that make the "sign flips are exponentially unlikely" conclusion misleading. All four panelists converged: (1) Theorem 1 is mathematically sound — the proof machinery is correct, no panelist disputed the arithmetic; (2) the assumption specification is underconstrained — g_T ∈ (0,1) with no lower bound on 1-g_T, and the stress case is mathematically unimpeachable and legally permitted; (3) "theorem brittleness" is the wrong label — the theorem does not break, it produces a valid but uninformative bound; (4) the appropriate remedy is a corollary, not a theorem revision — a corollary making the informative regime explicit would close the presentation gap; (5) the AdamW gap is the central unresolved issue. The panel characterizes this as a **completeness and presentation weakness**, not a mathematical correctness failure.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness 3/4:** Theorem 1 (Sign Lock-in Theorem) is a valid conditional result. The stopping-time analysis (geometric tail via tower property, Lemmas 1–2, induction) is rigorous and confirmed sound by technical-soundness, domain-expert-theory, and the assumption-stress-tester panel. The primary soundness weakness is assumption adequacy: the assumptions leave g_T unconstrained in ways that permit vacuous regimes (g_T=0.999 yields P[100+ flips] ≤ 0.60), and the optimizer mismatch (SGD sufficient condition proven, AdamW sufficient condition absent) severs the theory-to-experiments bridge for the paper's primary setting. The "optimizer-agnostic" remark is accurate as a theorem-syntax statement but practically misleading as a proven-scope statement. R4 adversarial raised this as a "misleading claim" disqualifying the paper; R4 rebuttal countered that the appendix explicitly flags the AdamW extension and the theorem syntax is genuinely optimizer-agnostic. The rebuttal's point is valid: the sufficient conditions are stated in optimizer-agnostic form and the extension to Adam-family is flagged in Appendix D. However, the formal AdamW sufficient condition derivation remains absent, and the Limitations section does not acknowledge this. The characterization falls between "misleading" and "incompletely qualified" — the paper would be strengthened by explicit acknowledgment.

**Presentation 3/4:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized with tight narrative coherence (10/10 layer pairs confirmed by narrative-coherence-auditor), excellent takeaway boxes, clear notation table, and well-designed figure captions. The primary presentation weakness is the non-standard Related Work placement after the Conclusion (§Related Work at line 811 vs. the conventional pre-Conclusion position), which violates ICML conventions and will actively confuse reviewers. The authors' rebuttal correctly acknowledges this as a convention violation and commit to the fix. Secondary weaknesses: non-standard `\mab{R}` notation; "largely inherited" (Claim 26) is over-interpreted without a quantitative threshold (the rebuttal points to Figure 2c data showing flip ratios 0.05–0.15, which is valid evidence, but an explicit threshold statement would eliminate ambiguity); Figure 5 baseline perplexity not explicitly labeled in caption.

**Originality 3/4:** The stopping-time / first-passage analysis of sign flip counts under SGD is genuinely novel. No prior work applies the Freidlin-Wentzell metastability framework to neural network sign dynamics. The "one-bit wall" empirical discovery (signs look noise-like yet are inherited) is non-obvious — the field expected training to create sign structure. The theory-derived interventions (gap initialization + log-barrier) are non-obvious in their specific parameterization (h_T, g_T as control targets). R4 adversarial raised that the practical value of the stopping-time novelty is "undemonstrated" — but mathematical novelty does not require immediate practical demonstration to score well on originality; the framework's portability to other discrete neural events (sparsity, attention heads) extends its value.

**Significance 3/4:** The paper identifies a genuine bottleneck (the one-bit wall) in sub-bit model compression and provides the first quantitative theory explaining why sign patterns resist compression. The theory-guided interventions are lightweight enough for community adoption (gap init + log-barrier require no special hardware). The stopping-time framework is portable to other discrete neural network events (sparsity patterns, attention head selection, routing), broadening impact beyond sub-bit compression. R4 adversarial raised the concern that the paper "never actually compresses a model below 1 bit per weight" — this is the most consequential point from the adversarial pass. The rebuttal's response is partial: the flip-rate reduction to ~10⁻³ with ~1-point perplexity overhead is a necessary but not sufficient demonstration of sub-bit compression viability. The paper establishes the *mechanism* and the *intervention*; the end-to-end compression demonstration is future work. This is a legitimate concern that should be reflected in the review's framing: the paper provides the theoretical and empirical foundation for the one-bit wall mitigation, but the applied claim "the one-bit wall can be overcome" is demonstrated only at the flip-rate level, not at the actual compression-ratio level. The significance is real but the applied contribution is partial.

**Overall 4/6 (Weak Accept):** The paper's core theoretical contribution (stopping-time theory of sign persistence) is genuine, mathematically sound, and non-obvious. The empirical one-bit wall discovery is real and well-documented. The theory-guided interventions show a flip-rate reduction Pareto improvement on CharLM. The primary weaknesses are: (1) the optimizer gap (theory proven for SGD, experiments use AdamW) is a material hidden assumption not disclosed in Limitations — R4 rebuttal narrows this but cannot close it; (2) the theorem's assumptions permit vacuous regimes that should be bounded by a corollary — R4 rebuttal correctly notes this is a presentation fix, not a correctness fix; (3) the absence of a code artifact limits reproducibility — authors commit to CharLM release; (4) the applied claim (one-bit wall mitigation) is demonstrated at the flip-rate level but not at the end-to-end compression level. These are revision-level weaknesses, not rejection-level. The single change that would most strengthen the paper: add an explicit Limitations paragraph acknowledging the AdamW sufficient condition gap and either derive it or qualify the "optimizer-agnostic" claim.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The submission's theoretical core (Theorem 1, stopping-time analysis, geometric tail) is unambiguously rooted in the Freidlin-Wentzell metastability tradition (Freidlin & Wentzell, 1998). The re-entry bound condition (Assumption 2) is the novel structural assumption that delineates stable training from "floating mode" collapse — this does not appear in prior metastability literature applied to neural networks. The most direct concurrent neighbor is OneBit (2024), which addresses the same sub-bit compression problem but treats signs as a storage object to compress, not as a dynamical object explained by boundary-excursion theory. The "first to X" claim (first to apply stopping-time analysis to sign dynamics) appears defensible against both the older anchor (Freidlin-Wentzell) and the concurrent neighbor (OneBit). The specific application of the excursion/stopping-time framework to sign dynamics is novel within the stochastic approximation literature applied to deep learning.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The sign lock-in theory's stopping-time framework is the paper's novel analytical tool and the most durable long-term contribution. Extending it to ReLU activation patterns is the natural and non-trivial follow-up: it requires re-deriving the assumptions for a different stochastic process (ReLU activation is a piecewise-linear threshold function, not a sign function), but the stopping-time formalism is portable. Concrete follow-up: characterize the boundary-excursion dynamics of ReLU activation patterns during training and derive the equivalent of Theorem 1 for activation sign/zero patterns. This would connect to the lottery ticket literature (Frankle & Carbin, 2019) by providing a dynamical-systems account of why certain initializations produce persistent activation patterns. A second follow-up: derive sufficient conditions for AdamW to satisfy the bounded-update assumption, which would close the paper's most consequential open problem.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

The paper identifies a real bottleneck (one-bit wall) in deploying sub-bit compressed models. The proposed interventions (gap initialization + log-barrier regularizer) are architecture-agnostic in principle and require no special hardware — a graduate student can implement them on a single A100. However, five concrete blockers limit current deployment: (1) no code artifact — reimplementation is required before production use (authors commit to CharLM release); (2) validation limited to character-level LM and MNIST — a practitioner deploying modern instruction-tuned LLMs cannot infer the perplexity overhead from available evidence; (3) the log-barrier regularizer requires architecture-specific tuning per model family, with no established protocol; (4) the theory-practice pipeline for adaptive optimizers (AdamW) is incomplete — a practitioner using AdamW (the standard optimizer for LLMs) cannot confidently predict flip suppression effectiveness; (5) the compressible sign template's independent contribution is unmeasured (always combined with gap initialization in experiments). The method is promising but not yet production-ready without additional validation and a released implementation.

## Integrated Verdict

Sign Lock-In is a theory-forward submission that makes a genuine, non-obvious contribution: the discovery that learned weight signs are spectrally noise-like yet trajectory-persistent, explained via a novel stopping-time analysis of sign flip dynamics under SGD. The theorem is mathematically sound (confirmed by multiple independent reviewers), the empirical one-bit wall phenomenon is real and well-documented, and the theory-guided interventions show a flip-rate reduction Pareto improvement on CharLM. The paper's primary weaknesses are: (1) the optimizer gap (theory proven for SGD, experiments use AdamW) is a material hidden assumption not disclosed in Limitations — R4 rebuttal narrows this concern (the theorem syntax is optimizer-agnostic and the AdamW extension is flagged in appendix) but cannot close the formal derivation gap; (2) the theorem's assumption brittleness (g_T unconstrained, permitting vacuous regimes that should be bounded by a corollary); (3) the absence of a code artifact limits reproducibility — authors commit to CharLM release; (4) the applied claim (one-bit wall mitigation) is demonstrated at the flip-rate level but not at the end-to-end compression-ratio level. These are revision-level concerns, not rejection-level. The stopping-time framework is the paper's most durable contribution — applicable beyond sign compression to any discrete neural network event — which gives it lasting value beyond its immediate empirical scope. Recommend **Weak Accept** (ICML 4).

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **§Remark after Theorem 1:** Qualify the "optimizer-agnostic" remark to specify that sufficient conditions are proven for SGD (Proposition 4.3) and that the AdamW sufficient condition derivation is left to future work. The current phrasing implies the theory is validated for AdamW at billion scale, which is unverified. Suggested revision: "While the argument extends to any optimizer satisfying Assumptions 1–2, we verify sufficient conditions only for SGD; the AdamW case is empirically observed but formally unverified."

2. **§Limitations:** Add explicit acknowledgment that the billion-scale experiments use AdamW while the theory's sufficient conditions are proven only for SGD. This is the paper's most consequential gap and its absence from Limitations misleads practitioners about the theory's proven scope.

3. **§Theorem 1 (post-statement):** Add a corollary bounding g_T away from 1 for the geometric tail to be informative. The stress case g_T=0.999, h_T=0.99, δ_upd=0.01 yielding P[K_eff_T ≥ 100] ≤ 0.60 is legally permitted by the current assumptions and makes the "exponentially unlikely" conclusion potentially misleading. A corollary stating "if g_T ≤ α for explicit α < 1, then the bound is informative at specified k" closes this presentation gap.

4. **§Experiments:** Ablate the compressible sign template independently of gap initialization. Currently the template is always co-deployed with gap init; their independent contributions are unmeasured, which prevents assessing whether the template's compression benefit is additive to gap init's lock-in benefit.

5. **§Related Work placement:** Move Related Work before the Conclusion. The current placement (§Related Work at line 811, after §Conclusion at line 802) violates ICML conventions.

6. **§Claim 26:** Quantify "largely inherited" — specify a flip ratio threshold (e.g., "flip ratios typically remain below 0.15, meaning >85% of signs are preserved") with explicit reference to the Figure 2c data that supports it. This eliminates ambiguity while using the evidence the paper already provides.

7. **Code release:** Release the implementation of gap initialization and log-barrier regularizer for the CharLM setting. This is the single highest-impact action for enabling community adoption and independent verification.

8. **§Compression demonstration:** Consider adding an end-to-end compression experiment (actual bits-per-weight vs. task quality) to directly demonstrate the one-bit wall being overcome, not just the flip-rate reduction. While the flip-rate reduction is the foundation, showing the actual compression outcome would complete the applied contribution.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you derive sufficient conditions for AdamW to satisfy the bounded-update assumption (Assumption 1), or provide empirical per-step update norm measurements during warmup from the billion-scale setting? This is the most consequential open question for the theory-to-experiments bridge.

2. What is the quantitative threshold for "largely inherited" — specifically, what flip ratio upper bound are you asserting, and what is the empirical distribution of flip ratios across architectures, learning rates, and model scales that supports it?

3. The compressible sign template is always evaluated with gap initialization. What is the independent contribution of the template (r=2 rank decomposition) to the perplexity maintenance property, absent gap init? Is the template's benefit additive to gap init, or is it only beneficial in the gap-init regime?
