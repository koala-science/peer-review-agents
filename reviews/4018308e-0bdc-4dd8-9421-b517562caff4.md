# Committee Review: Block removal for large language models through constrained binary optimization

**Paper ID:** 4018308e-0bdc-4dd8-9421-b517562caff4
**Lead Reviewer:** Comprehensive Reviewer (5-round deliberation committee)
**Date:** 2026-04-27

---

## One-Sentence Summary

The paper formulates transformer block removal in LLMs as a constrained binary optimization problem mapped to an Ising model, whose low-energy excited states yield diverse high-quality block-removal configurations that outperform state-of-the-art baselines on MMLU and other benchmarks, with the central empirical claims surviving the adversarial audit though resting on single-run evaluations and post-hoc excited-state selection.

---

## Claimed Contributions

1. CBO formulation: transformer block pruning as a constrained binary optimization problem that explicitly models second-order interactions between blocks via a Taylor-expanded Hessian approximation
2. Outperforms SOTA block-removal techniques (BI, SWM, Norm ratio) on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Qwen3-14B at high compression rates
3. Low-energy excited states of the Ising model yield high-quality pruning configurations, eliminating the need for exact CBO solutions in larger models
4. Generalizes to heterogeneous MoE architectures (NVIDIA-Nemotron-3-Nano-30B-A3B-FP8) without retraining

## Disagreement-Resolution Narratives

**Panel 1 — Excited-state cherry-picking:** The statistician argued this is fatal (family-wise error rate ~99.97%). The experimental-rigor and significance subagents partially conceded: the MMLU gains (11-12 points) are large enough to likely exceed run-to-run variance, and the excited-state phenomenon is demonstrated across multiple states and architectures. The claim-auditor maintained that 26 of 50 claims remain Demonstrated. **R4 update:** The adversarial reviewer correctly notes that the excited-state phenomenon is demonstrated on Nemotron (Gs:2, CBO:1, CBO:2, CBO:3 all strong without retraining) independently of the Llama CBO:17 selection. The author rebuttal confirms CBO:17 was selected as "first state removing a block from the first half of the model" — a structural criterion, not pure benchmark cherry-picking. **Resolution:** The phenomenon is real and the specific CBO:17 selection is partially justified by a structural criterion. The magnitude of CBO:17's advantage is less reliably established, but the excited-state insight survives.

**Panel 2 — Retraining asymmetry:** The experimental-rigor and technical-soundness subagents argued this is a fatal confound. The significance subagent maintained BI is community-standard without intermediate retraining. **R4 update:** The adversarial reviewer correctly notes that the retraining asymmetry cannot explain the excited-state ranking (all CBO configurations receive identical retraining). The author rebuttal confirms BI is applied per community standard and commits to an additional experiment as a follow-up. **Resolution:** The asymmetry is a disclosure limitation, not a structural confound for the excited-state insight. It should be disclosed in the revision.

**Panel 3 — Taylor truncation:** The technical-soundness and completeness subagents argued the ∇L≈0 assumption is unvalidated at block-level and the Gili et al. contradiction is unresolved. The claim-auditor argued the method works empirically. **R4 update:** The author rebuttal commits to adding a block-level ablation of the first-order term. **Resolution:** The concern is real and the committed ablation should address it.

**Panel 4 — MMLU understatement:** The risk-of-fraud-detector and narrative-coherence-auditor argued the framing is sloppy. The claim-auditor and clarity subagent argued it is conservative understatement. **R4 update:** The author rebuttal clarifies that "up to 6 points" refers to the SWM comparison (0.55 vs 0.49 = 6 points), while the 11-12 point gaps are against BI. The authors commit to clarifying this in the abstract. **Resolution:** The framing is technically defensible but clarity should be improved. Authors commit to explicit baseline specification.

## Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer (Lead's Integrated Reading)

**Soundness (2/4):** The paper's mathematical derivation is sound and the Ising/CBO formulation is well-motivated. However, the experimental core suffers from pervasive single-run reporting (N=1 everywhere), no statistical tests across ~150+ pairwise comparisons, and post-hoc selection of CBO:17 from 20 excited states without pre-registration. The retraining asymmetry between BI and CBO is real but within community norms and does not explain the excited-state ranking. The adversarial reviewer's counter that 11-12 point MMLU gaps are 10-20× typical variance is valid — at this effect size, statistical significance is practically established even without variance reporting. However, the absence of variance reporting remains a legitimate weakness that should be corrected.

**Presentation (2/4):** The paper is readable and the core narrative is coherent. The factual text-to-table error on the ARC-Challenge claim and the excited-state misattribution are concrete accuracy failures that should be corrected. The abstract's MMLU framing should specify which baseline corresponds to which gain. The abstract's "any architecture" and "efficient ranking" claims are not fully supported by the experiments, which use brute-force/branch-and-bound and test on three specific models.

**Originality (3/4):** The Ising/CBO formulation for block-level pruning and the empirical discovery that low-energy excited states yield diverse, high-quality non-consecutive block-removal configurations are genuinely novel contributions. The observation that excited states can outperform the ground state is non-obvious given the field's focus on finding the single best configuration.

**Significance (3/4):** The MMLU gains of 11-12 points over baselines at 50% compression are large and practically transformative. The excited-state insight has significant forward leverage. The method generalizes to MoE architectures. The adversarial reviewer is correct that a 12-point MMLU improvement at 50% compression is the kind of result that defines a research direction.

**Overall recommendation: ICML Overall = 4 (Weak Accept).** The method makes a genuine contribution and the MMLU gains are large enough to be practically meaningful even with the statistical weaknesses. The paper would be substantially strengthened by variance reporting, pre-registered excited-state selection criteria, correction of factual errors, disclosure of the retraining asymmetry, and code release.

## Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

The paper's load-bearing methodological ancestor is Combinatorial Brain Surgeon (CBS, Yu et al., ICML 2022), which introduced second-order combinatorial optimization for weight-level pruning. The paper extends this framework to block-level pruning via auxiliary coupling variables α_i and a fixed-magnetization Ising model — a non-trivial extension that is supportably novel given that CBS focused on individual weights or channels, not full transformer blocks.

No concurrent paper was found that makes the same Ising/CBO claim for block removal. ShortGPT (ICLR 2025) uses a greedy similarity-based heuristic, not an optimization formulation. The norm-ratio and SWM baselines are also heuristic, not optimization-based. The CBO/Ising framing is distinct in the literature.

The excited-state insight has a weak prior-art connection: earlier work on combinatorial optimization (Farhi et al., 2014 on quantum approximate optimization) noted that low-energy excited states can be informative, but this was not applied to neural network pruning. The application to block-removal configuration ranking is novel.

## Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The most concrete follow-up project is **combining CBO block removal with quantum or hardware-accelerated Ising solvers** for models with 100+ blocks where brute-force enumeration is infeasible. The paper's own discussion mentions Farhi et al. (2014) and Quinton et al. (2025) as candidate solvers. A follow-up could benchmark QAOA, simulated annealing, or quantum annealing hardware against the branch-and-bound baseline for smaller models, then apply the best solver to Llama-3.1-70B or larger. This is a non-trivial project that materially depends on this paper's framework.

A second follow-up: **systematic ablation of the ∇L≈0 assumption** at block level. The Gili et al. (2025) finding that first-order terms help at weight level should be tested for block-level pruning. The author rebuttal commits to this ablation, which will be a valuable contribution.

## Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

For a practitioner seeking to compress a production LLM for edge deployment, this method offers a principled approach to deciding which transformer blocks to remove. The key blocker is the **Ising solver**: the paper did not originally specify which solver was used. The author rebuttal clarifies that branch-and-bound was used for smaller search spaces and simulated annealing for larger ones. This information is essential for reproducibility and for practitioners to build on the work.

The method requires: (1) a calibration dataset for Hessian computation (2048 samples in the paper), (2) an Ising/QUBO solver, and (3) retraining via knowledge distillation after block selection. The calibration dataset requirements are underspecified — a practitioner would need to experiment to determine the appropriate dataset size and distribution.

The excited-state insight is practically valuable: even if the ground state is not optimal, low-energy excited states provide alternative configurations with different trade-offs. The author rebuttal confirms that multiple excited states (not just CBO:17) yield strong results, making this a robust approach in practice.

## Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

A competent ML grad student could reimplement the core CBO/Ising framework from the paper text in approximately one week. The method is described with sufficient mathematical precision. The author rebuttal now clarifies the solver: branch-and-bound for smaller configurations (N≤28) and simulated annealing with a fixed-magnetization constraint for larger ones. This specification removes the main unspecified component.

The retraining procedure is described qualitatively ("knowledge distillation for one epoch on OpenHermes-2.5") but the full hyperparameters are deferred to the code. Without the code, the retraining quality cannot be independently verified — the authors commit to releasing the code upon acceptance.

## Integrated Verdict

This paper makes a genuine and non-obvious contribution to LLM block-level compression: the Ising/CBO formulation for block removal and the empirical discovery that low-energy excited states yield diverse, high-quality pruning configurations that can outperform the ground state. The MMLU gains of 11-12 percentage points over SOTA baselines at 50% compression are large enough — 10-20× typical retraining-run variance — to be practically meaningful even with N=1 evaluation. The method extends to MoE architectures, expanding its applicability.

The adversarial reviewer's counter-arguments are partially persuasive: the N=1 critique is statistically conservative but practically disproportionate for large-effect claims; the excited-state phenomenon is demonstrated across multiple states and architectures (Nemotron results are independent of the Llama CBO:17 selection); and the factual errors are correctable in camera-ready without affecting core claims. However, the post-hoc selection of CBO:17 without a pre-registered criterion remains a methodological weakness, and the absence of variance reporting is a genuine reproducibility gap.

The author rebuttal is credible and constructive: factual errors are acknowledged and will be corrected; the solver is now specified (branch-and-bound + simulated annealing); the excited-state selection criterion is partially justified (structural diversity: first state removing a block from the first half of the model); and commitments are made to add variance reporting, pre-specify the selection criterion, add the ∇L≈0 ablation, and release code.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept (ICML Overall = 4).** The paper should be accepted with strong encouragement to address the statistical weaknesses, correct the factual errors, pre-specify the excited-state selection criterion, disclose the retraining asymmetry, and release the code in the camera-ready version.

## Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Report variance (Sections 3, Experiments):** Every benchmark number in Table 1 and all figures should be accompanied by mean ± std across at least 3 retraining seeds. The lm-eval-harness framework produces bootstrap standard errors — report these. The adversarial reviewer correctly notes that 11-12 point MMLU gaps are robust to noise, but variance reporting is essential for reproducibility.

2. **Pre-specify the excited-state selection criterion (Section 3.1, page 287):** The paper selects CBO:17 as "the first state that removes a block from the first half of the model." Pre-commit to this structural criterion before seeing benchmark results, or apply a multiple-comparison correction across the 20 inspected states. The author rebuttal commits to this.

3. **Correct the ARC-Challenge factual error (page 369):** The text states BI "having three points less for the ARC challenge" for Qwen3-14B at 8-block removal, but Table 1 shows BI (0.48) scores 1 point *higher* than CBO:0 (0.47). This is a directional factual error. The author rebuttal acknowledges this and commits to correction.

4. **Correct the excited-state accuracy misattribution (page 403):** The text attributes the 94%/88% accuracy figures to "the second excited state" (CBO:2), but these values belong to the ground state Gs:2. CBO:2 coincidentally achieves the same values, but the narrative should correctly attribute them. The author rebuttal acknowledges this and commits to correction.

5. **Disclose the retraining asymmetry (Section 3.2, page 362):** BI is applied iteratively without intermediate retraining while CBO receives full knowledge distillation after block selection. This asymmetry should be disclosed as a limitation. The author rebuttal acknowledges this and commits to disclosure.

6. **Add ∇L≈0 ablation (Section 2, page 247):** Test whether including the first-order Taylor term at block-level affects the quality of the Hessian approximation. The Gili et al. 2025 finding that first-order terms help at weight level should be investigated at block level. The author rebuttal commits to this ablation.

7. **Clarify the Ising solver (Section 2, page 270):** Specify that branch-and-bound is used for smaller configurations (N≤28) and simulated annealing with a fixed-magnetization constraint for larger ones. The author rebuttal provides this clarification.

8. **Clarify the abstract's MMLU framing:** Specify which baseline corresponds to which gain: "up to 6 points over SWM and up to 12 points over BI on MMLU at 50% compression." The author rebuttal commits to this clarification.

9. **Release the code:** The paper claims code is in the supplementary material but no artifact is available. Code release is essential for reproducibility. The author rebuttal commits to releasing on GitHub upon acceptance.

## Questions for Authors

1. **Excited-state selection criterion:** You state CBO:17 was selected as "the first state that removes a block from the first half of the model." Was this structural criterion pre-specified before observing the benchmark numbers, or was the criterion derived after observing that CBO:17 performed best? If the former, please state this explicitly. If the latter, please pre-specify a structural criterion for the camera-ready.

2. **Retraining asymmetry:** Would the CBO advantage over BI persist if BI also received retraining after each block removal? You commit to this as a follow-up experiment. What is your prior? Do you expect the 11-12 point MMLU gap to close, narrow, or persist?

3. **First-order Taylor term:** Have you tested whether including the first-order term at block-level changes the Hessian approximation quality or the ranking of block configurations? The Gili et al. finding may or may not transfer to block-level pruning.

## Adversarial Audit Trail (R4)

**Adversarial counter-arguments considered:**

The adversarial reviewer made five strong counter-arguments: (1) the N=1 critique is disproportionate for 11-12 point effect sizes (10-20× typical variance); (2) the excited-state phenomenon is demonstrated across multiple states and architectures independently of the CBO:17 selection; (3) the retraining asymmetry cannot explain the excited-state ranking since all CBO configurations receive identical retraining; (4) the factual errors are correctable copy-editing issues that do not affect core claims; (5) the MMLU gains are transformative for the field (12-point gap over the best prior method at 50% compression).

**Which moved the verdict:** The adversarial's counter on the excited-state phenomenon being demonstrated independently (via Nemotron results showing Gs:2, CBO:1, CBO:2, CBO:3 all strong) is the most persuasive. This substantially reduces the concern about post-hoc selection in the Llama case. The N=1 counter is valid but does not eliminate the need for variance reporting — the paper should still report it. The factual errors counter is partially persuasive — they are correctable and do not affect core claims, but they should still be corrected.

**Residual risk:** The main residual risk is that the excited-state advantage is specific to the Llama architecture and the specific calibration dataset (OpenHermes-2.5), and would not generalize to other models or datasets. The Nemotron results mitigate but do not eliminate this risk, since Nemotron was evaluated without retraining. If the excited-state ranking is unstable across calibration datasets or model families, the practical value of the excited-state insight is reduced.

**Author rebuttal assessment:** The author rebuttal is constructive and credible. The authors acknowledge all substantive criticisms, commit to corrections, and provide reasonable defenses where warranted. The clarification that "up to 6 points" refers to the SWM comparison (not BI) is important and resolves the framing concern. The specification of the solver (branch-and-bound + simulated annealing) resolves the main reproducibility gap. The commitment to variance reporting, pre-specified excited-state selection, and the ∇L≈0 ablation substantially addresses the committee's concerns.

**Net assessment:** The adversarial audit did not undermine the paper's core contributions. The MMLU gains are large enough to be credible, the excited-state phenomenon is demonstrated across multiple architectures, and the factual errors are correctable. The paper's recommendation of Weak Accept is appropriate. The committed revisions — if faithfully executed — would elevate the paper toward Accept.
