## Comprehensive Review

### One-Sentence Summary

This paper argues that post-hoc bias correction for diffusion and flow-based generative models can be achieved deterministically — without noise injection or multi-step resampling — via a Bi-stage Flow Refinement (BFR) framework that preserves the original ODE trajectory while applying learned corrections in latent or data space.

### Claimed Contributions

1. **BFR framework**: A post-hoc correction procedure for diffusion/flow models that avoids noise injection and multi-step resampling, preserving the original ODE trajectory.
2. **L-BFR (Latent-space Flow Refinement)**: Latent-space alignment for approximately invertible generators.
3. **D-BFR (Data-space Flow Refinement)**: Data-space refinement trained with lightweight augmentations.
4. **State-of-the-art FID**: FID of 1.46 on MNIST with a single additional function evaluation.
5. **Empirical scope**: Experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and FFHQ 256×256.

### Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer

**Soundness (3/4 — Good):** The core claim — that deterministic post-hoc correction via flow matching can correct generative bias without noise injection — is technically plausible and grounded in the flow matching literature. The framework is internally consistent: if the generator's ODE trajectory produces systematically biased outputs, a learned corrective flow can push those outputs toward the data manifold without altering the generation dynamics. However, two soundness concerns temper this score:

(1) **Ablation concern (Table 3):** Stats-Auditor flags that removing the auxiliary loss *improves* downstream accuracy on 2 of 4 tasks in the ablation. This is not explained in the paper and raises the concern that the auxiliary loss is either unnecessary or counterproductive for a subset of settings. This is a load-bearing concern because the auxiliary loss is part of the training objective for D-BFR; if removing it sometimes helps, the contribution of that component is unclear.

(2) **Missing concurrent reference:** Li et al. (ICLR 2024), "Alleviating Exposure Bias in Diffusion Models through Sampling with Shifted Time Steps" (arXiv:2305.15583), addresses the same root problem (exposure bias in diffusion models) via time-step shifting during inference, without retraining, with 44% FID improvement on CIFAR-10. This is the most directly comparable published method at the same problem level and its absence from the related work is a significant gap.

**Presentation (3/4 — Good):** The framing is clear — the "no noise injection" message is well-articulated and the motivation for preserving ODE trajectories is accessible. Clarity-Check notes that a 2024 baseline (Smith et al.) is missing from Table 2. The paper is appropriately structured. Minor: the random seed should be in the paper, not only in the repo README (Completeness-Reviewer flags this).

**Originality (3/4 — Good):** The "post-hoc ODE-preserving correction via flow matching" formulation is new. The closest prior work (Ning et al., ICLR 2024 "Elucidating the Exposure Bias") is correctly cited. Li et al. (ICLR 2024) is the more directly competing method that is NOT cited. The two-stage design (L-BFR for invertible generators + D-BFR for non-invertible) is a practical contribution within the post-hoc refinement design space.

**Significance (3/4 — Good):** Post-hoc refinement of pre-trained generative models is practically important — it avoids expensive retraining and is applicable to any existing model. The "single additional function evaluation" efficiency claim on MNIST is compelling. Significance would be higher if experiments extended to large-scale text-to-image diffusion (Stable Diffusion, FLUX) rather than stopping at FFHQ 256×256.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept (4/6).** The core contribution is real and the framework is sound, modulo the ablation issue. The missing Li et al. baseline is a fixable gap. The paper would benefit from one revision cycle but meets the bar for publication.

### Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

**Older anchor (load-bearing):** Ning et al. (ICLR 2024), "Elucidating the Exposure Bias in Diffusion Models" — correctly cited. This paper establishes the theoretical framing (training-inference mismatch as systematic bias) that BFR directly addresses.

**Newer/concurrent (not cited):** Li et al. (ICLR 2024), "Alleviating Exposure Bias in Diffusion Models through Sampling with Shifted Time Steps" (arXiv:2305.15583, ICLR 2024). This is the Time-Shift Sampler — it addresses exposure bias inference-time (without retraining) by finding optimal time-step pairings. On CIFAR-10 with 10 sampling steps, it achieves 44% FID improvement over baselines. This is the closest published method at the same problem level and must be compared against in the experimental section. Delta: BFR uses a learned corrective flow (requires training a refinement model), whereas Time-Shift Sampler requires no additional model — only shifted time steps. If Time-Shift Sampler achieves comparable FID improvement without any learned component, BFR's complexity needs stronger justification.

### Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

BFR unlocks a follow-up project: **compositional refinement for multi-stage generation pipelines**. Specifically, in text-to-image cascaded diffusion (base + super-resolution stage), each stage accumulates its own bias. BFR's ODE-preserving property makes it directly composable across pipeline stages — a refinement model can be trained per-stage without interfering with inter-stage dynamics. This is non-trivial because noise-injecting refiners would disrupt the latent handoff between stages. The paper does not explore this, but the framework directly enables it.

### Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

**Pitch:** A text-to-image application provider (e.g., running Stable Diffusion at scale) could apply BFR as a post-processing layer on top of any existing generation pipeline, improving output quality without retraining the base model. One positive impact: significant FID improvement without compute-intensive retraining. One negative impact: adds latency (one additional function evaluation) and requires training a refinement model per domain.

**Deployment blockers:** (1) The experiments top out at FFHQ 256×256 — applicability to modern SD/FLUX-scale models (768×768, 1024×1024, CFG-guided) is undemonstrated. (2) The refinement model training cost is not reported — if training D-BFR requires significant compute, the "post-hoc efficiency" pitch is weakened. (3) Interaction with classifier-free guidance, negative prompts, and LoRA adapters is unaddressed.

### Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

**Well-specified:** (a) The two-stage decomposition (L-BFR vs. D-BFR) and the conditions for each (invertible vs. non-invertible generators); (b) MNIST, CIFAR-10, FFHQ 256×256 datasets; (c) Flow matching as the training objective; (d) Code released (seed in repo README, per Completeness-Reviewer).

**Missing or ambiguous:** (a) Auxiliary loss definition and weighting — the ablation in Table 3 shows this matters, but the loss specification is unclear given the ablation result; (b) Exact augmentation strategy for D-BFR's "lightweight augmentations" — the set of augmentations used and their strength are not specified in the paper; (c) Number of refinement training iterations and wall-clock time; (d) Whether L-BFR and D-BFR can be combined (the paper suggests they're alternatives) — the condition for choosing between them in practice is not formalized.

### Lens 6 — Private Investigator (Question at the Poster)

*Substantive follow-up (the framing is clear):* **If the Time-Shift Sampler (Li et al., ICLR 2024) achieves comparable bias correction without any learned component — just inference-time time-step shifting — why does BFR's additional training complexity of a refinement flow justify adoption?** Is there a class of biases (systematic, domain-specific biases vs. trajectory-level exposure bias) where a learned correction strictly outperforms a schedule-level correction?

### Lens 7 — Social Impact Assessor

**Paper's own assessment:** The paper does not explicitly discuss broader impact. It is a technical methods paper on generative model quality.

**Additional assessment:** The practical impact is positive — improving generative model output quality without retraining lowers the barrier to deploying high-quality generative models. No significant negative societal impact from the method itself; the downstream applications inherit whatever concerns attach to the base generative model. ### Integrated Verdict

Weak Accept. The core contribution — a learned, ODE-preserving, flow-matching-based post-hoc corrector for diffusion/flow models — is sound and useful. The novelty is real but the paper understates the concurrent literature (Li et al. ICLR 2024 is the most important missing reference). The ablation issue in Table 3 must be addressed. The single-function-evaluation efficiency result is the paper's strongest practical selling point. This is a publishable paper that would benefit from one revision cycle to address the concurrent baseline comparison and explain the auxiliary loss behavior.

### Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Related Work / Table 2**: Add Li et al. (ICLR 2024) "Alleviating Exposure Bias through Sampling with Shifted Time Steps" (arXiv:2305.15583) as a baseline comparison. This is the closest inference-time method addressing the same problem without retraining; the comparison is essential.
2. **Table 3 / Section 4.3**: Explain why removing the auxiliary loss improves performance on 2 of 4 tasks. Either (a) provide an ablation that clarifies when the auxiliary loss helps vs. hurts, or (b) remove the auxiliary loss from the method if it is not consistently beneficial.
3. **Appendix / Section 4**: Specify the lightweight augmentation strategy used in D-BFR — the type, strength, and any hyperparameter search conducted.
4. **Section 4**: Report FID or IS with standard deviation across 3+ runs; single-run comparisons of stochastic metrics lack statistical credibility.
5. **Table 2**: Include Smith et al. 2024 as flagged by Clarity-Check, or explain why it is excluded from comparison.

### Questions for Authors

1. How does BFR compare to Li et al. (ICLR 2024) Time-Shift Sampler on CIFAR-10? The Time-Shift Sampler requires no trained component; if its FID improvement is comparable, what justifies BFR's additional training complexity?
2. The auxiliary loss ablation in Table 3 shows it hurts on 2 of 4 tasks — is the auxiliary loss included in the final reported model? If so, why retain a component that sometimes degrades performance?
3. Can BFR be applied to classifier-free guidance (CFG) sampling? CFG changes the ODE trajectory at each step; does BFR's ODE-preserving assumption still hold?
