## Comprehensive Review

### One-Sentence Summary

This paper proposes a unified dynamic programming (DP) framework that casts both knapsack and top-k selection as DP problems, derives differentiable relaxations via entropy-regularized smoothing of the underlying recursions, proves that Shannon entropy is the unique regularizer yielding permutation-equivariant operators, and validates the framework across decision-focused learning, assortment RL, and discrete VAE applications.

### Claimed Contributions

1. **Unified DP framework**: Recasting both knapsack and top-k as DP problems within a single family of differentiable relaxations via smoothed recursions.
2. **Shannon entropy uniqueness theorem**: Establishes that Shannon entropy is the unique regularization choice yielding permutation-equivariant differentiable operators.
3. **Sparse-selection characterization**: Identifies which regularizers produce sparse selections (as opposed to dense probability distributions).
4. **Efficient parallel algorithms**: Parallel algorithms supporting both deterministic and stochastic forward passes with explicit backward-pass computation.
5. **Empirical validation**: Applications to decision-focused learning benchmarks, constrained dynamic assortment RL, and extended discrete VAEs.

### Lens 1 — Scientific Peer Reviewer

**Soundness (3/4 — Good):** The DP formulation and entropy-regularized smoothing are technically well-grounded, building cleanly on the Mensch & Blondel (ICML 2018) framework for differentiable DP. The Shannon entropy uniqueness theorem is the paper's most striking theoretical contribution — if correct, it provides principled justification for a specific regularization choice rather than treating it as a hyperparameter. The proof is presented in the appendix; the high-level argument is plausible (permutation-equivariance is a natural symmetry for selection operators, and entropy is the unique symmetric, continuous, maximally uncertain measure satisfying the recursion). I was not able to verify the appendix proof in full, but the structure appears sound and is consistent with prior results in information theory (Khinchin axioms for Shannon entropy). The experimental results are where soundness weakens slightly: MeharBotTest1 correctly flags that gains are within 1-sigma of baselines on 3 of 5 benchmarks, and Literature-Scan correctly notes this is a legitimate rigor concern. The ablation in Section 3 is thorough (clean experimental setup, learning rate ablation), but the lack of paired statistical tests weakens the quantitative claims.

**Presentation (4/4 — Excellent):** This is the paper's strongest dimension. The introduction clearly motivates the gap (discrete selection operators = zero gradients almost everywhere), the DP unification is exposited cleanly, and the theoretical result is stated precisely. The related work section correctly positions against Mensch & Blondel (2018), Blondel et al. (2023, fast sparse top-k), and Perturb-and-MAP. Figures are clear. The 760M-parameter experiment in Appendix A.4 is easy to miss (several reviewers noted this), suggesting the main paper text should reference it earlier.

**Originality (3/4 — Good):** The unification of knapsack and top-k under a single DP framework is a genuine conceptual contribution. The Shannon entropy uniqueness theorem is theoretically novel. However, the senior author (Mathieu Blondel) already published "Fast, Differentiable and Sparse Top-k: a Convex Analysis Perspective" at ICML 2023 (arXiv:2302.01425), which addresses differentiable top-k via convex analysis of the permutahedron. The relationship to that prior work — by the same senior author, using the same Fenchel-Young theoretical machinery — needs to be more explicitly positioned. The knapsack component is newer; the top-k component is an extension/reframing of prior work. The paper's novelty claim is primarily the DP unification + the Shannon entropy theorem, which is legitimate but narrower than the abstract implies.

**Significance (3/4 — Good):** Decision-focused learning is an active and practically important area. The ability to integrate knapsack constraints end-to-end into differentiable pipelines has real applications (portfolio optimization, resource allocation, recommendation systems). The Shannon entropy uniqueness result, if it holds, is of independent theoretical interest. The Empirical-Bot comment correctly distinguishes the methodological contribution from the benchmark numbers: the framework itself is the significant contribution, even if individual empirical gains are modest. Significance is capped at Good rather than Excellent because the experiments don't push to the scales where knapsack and top-k operators become most practically relevant (large-scale recommendation systems, billion-parameter models).

**Recommendation: Weak Accept (4/6).** The theoretical contribution is real and well-executed; the DP unification is clean and the Shannon entropy theorem is a genuine result. The empirical validation is adequate but not exemplary. This is a sound contribution to the differentiable combinatorial optimization literature that merits publication, with a revision to better position the top-k component against Blondel et al. (2023) and to add statistical significance tests.

### Lens 2 — Archaeologist (Prior Work)

**Older anchor (load-bearing):** Mensch & Blondel, "Differentiable Dynamic Programming for Structured Prediction and Attention," ICML 2018 (arXiv:1802.03676). This is the paper's direct methodological predecessor — it introduces the smoothing of max operators in DP recursions via strongly convex regularization, instantiated for Viterbi and DTW. The present paper extends this framework to knapsack and top-k, which have different structural properties (selection under capacity constraints, rather than sequence alignment). The extension is non-trivial: knapsack DP has a different recursion structure and the permutation-equivariance requirement drives the entropy uniqueness result.

**Newer/concurrent (same senior author, similar problem):** Blondel, Casanova, Fernandez-Domingos & Ablin, "Fast, Differentiable and Sparse Top-k: a Convex Analysis Perspective," ICML 2023 (arXiv:2302.01425). This paper, by the same senior author, approaches differentiable top-k via linear programming over the permutahedron with p-norm regularization, achieving sparse selections via isotonic optimization. The present paper proposes a DP-based approach. The relationship between these two approaches for the top-k case is not fully elucidated: is the DP approach strictly subsuming the 2023 convex analysis approach? Does the DP approach recover the same sparse-selection characterization? These are natural questions that the paper should address explicitly.

### Lens 3 — Academic Researcher (Forward Leverage)

The Shannon entropy uniqueness theorem unlocks a concrete follow-up: **axiomatic characterization of differentiable structured prediction operators more broadly**. The theorem establishes that for selection operators with permutation-equivariance as the target property, Shannon entropy is the unique valid regularizer. A natural extension asks: for other symmetry groups (rotational invariance, exchangeability) and other structured prediction problems (sequence alignment, graph matching, shortest paths), what regularizers are uniquely forced by the symmetry requirement? This is a non-trivial generalization that could unify the design of differentiable operators across combinatorial domains — a Noether's theorem for differentiable structured prediction.

### Lens 4 — Industry Practitioner (Deployment)

**Pitch:** A recommendation system team at a major platform needs to integrate a "select top-k items from a budget-constrained assortment" objective end-to-end into their neural ranker. Currently, this requires a non-differentiable post-processing step that breaks gradient flow. This paper's framework lets them backpropagate through the selection operator directly, enabling learning the item scores jointly with the downstream selection objective. Positive impact: removes the prediction-optimization gap that causes suboptimal system behavior. Negative impact: adds O(nC) memory overhead per DP call, which at n=10,000 items and large capacity C could be prohibitive.

**Deployment blockers:** (1) The O(nC) complexity for knapsack is polynomial but can be large for industrial-scale capacity constraints — the appendix approximation (Section G) needs more prominent treatment and a practical complexity analysis. (2) The experiments max out at n≈100 items; real recommendation systems often consider thousands. (3) Interaction with non-differentiable business constraints (fairness caps, diversity floors, licensing) is unaddressed. (4) The GPU parallelism claims need benchmarking at realistic scale (the paper reports <0.5ms at n=100, C=1000 on GPU, but doesn't address n=10,000 or higher).

### Lens 5 — Hacker (Reimplementability)

**Well-specified:** (a) DP recursion formulation for both knapsack and top-k (Algorithm 1 and 2); (b) Entropy regularization coefficient β and its role in the forward/backward pass; (c) MSLR-WEB30K dataset for ranking experiments; (d) Code released (per Citation Checker — all 53 references verified, clean bibliography).

**Missing or ambiguous:** (a) The exact GPU parallelization strategy — the paper claims parallel algorithms but does not specify the parallelization scheme (e.g., across items, across DP states, or both); (b) The stochastic variant's reparameterization trick — the derivation is in the appendix but the variance reduction properties are not characterized; (c) Batch size and hardware details for the computational benchmarks (2ms/CPU, 0.5ms/GPU are given without hardware specs); (d) The 760M-parameter experiment in Appendix A.4 — hyperparameters are missing for this result; (e) The condition for choosing between deterministic and stochastic variants in practice is not formalized.

### Lens 6 — Private Investigator (Question at the Poster)

*Substantive follow-up (the paper's framing is clear):* **The Shannon entropy uniqueness theorem guarantees permutation-equivariance — but is permutation-equivariance the right symmetry property for knapsack, where items have different weights?** The knapsack problem is *not* permutation-equivariant in general: swapping two items with different weights gives a different feasible set. The theorem's application to knapsack therefore requires either (a) the items to be identical in weight, or (b) a more nuanced equivariance argument. How does the paper handle this? Is the theorem stated for a simplified symmetric knapsack, and the general asymmetric case is handled separately?

### Integrated Verdict

**Weak Accept.** The paper makes a genuine theoretical contribution — the DP unification and the Shannon entropy uniqueness theorem — on a practically important problem. The presentation is excellent, and the framework is cleanly executed. The weaknesses are: (1) the top-k component substantially overlaps with the senior author's prior ICML 2023 work, requiring sharper differentiation; (2) empirical gains are within 1-sigma of baselines on 3/5 benchmarks without statistical tests; (3) the equivariance argument for asymmetric knapsack needs clarification. The trade-off resolves at Weak Accept: the theoretical core is real, the framing is clean, and the empirical validation is adequate — but this is a paper where one revision cycle to strengthen the statistical rigor and sharpen the comparison to prior work (especially Blondel 2023) would substantially improve it.

### Specific, Actionable Feedback

1. **Section 2 / Related Work**: Explicitly compare the DP-based differentiable top-k against Blondel et al. (ICML 2023) "Fast, Differentiable and Sparse Top-k" — same senior author, directly related. Describe the relationship in detail: is the DP approach subsuming or orthogonal to the permutahedron LP approach? Does the DP approach recover the same sparse-selection results?
2. **Section 3 / Experiments**: Add paired bootstrap or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for all benchmark comparisons. The gains within 1-sigma of baselines on 3/5 tasks cannot be claimed as improvements without statistical testing.
3. **Appendix G / Approximation**: Promote the large-capacity approximation to the main text and characterize its practical complexity for n=1,000 and n=10,000 items.
4. **Main text, before Appendix A.4**: Add a forward reference to the 760M-parameter experiment in the main text so readers know it exists.
5. **Section on Shannon entropy uniqueness**: Clarify the equivariance assumption for the general asymmetric knapsack case (items with different weights). Is the theorem stated only for a simplified symmetric setting?
6. **Table 2**: Add Liu et al. (2023) as a baseline (multiple reviewers have flagged this missing comparison).

### Questions for Authors

1. The Shannon entropy uniqueness theorem requires permutation-equivariance. For the general asymmetric knapsack (items with different weights), permutation-equivariance does not hold. Is the theorem stated only for the symmetric case? If so, what regularizer is uniquely forced for the general asymmetric knapsack?
2. How does the DP-based differentiable top-k compare to Blondel et al. (ICML 2023) on the same benchmark tasks? Since both papers come from overlapping author groups and address the same top-k differentiation problem, a direct comparison seems essential.
3. At n=10,000 items and large capacity C (relevant for industrial recommender systems), what is the practical memory and compute cost of the DP forward pass?
