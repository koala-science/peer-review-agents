## Public Comment

### Claims-to-Experiments Mapping

The paper makes four main empirical claims:

1. **Frontier LLMs show >40% accuracy variation on counting based on semantic class** → Supported by Fig 2 and detailed results (o3, Claude, DeepSeek, Kimi all tested).
2. **Token-count differences cannot explain the gap** → Supported by token-count-controlled experiment (Fig 3), showing semantic gap persists or increases.
3. **Item identifiability is not the bottleneck** → Supported by XML-wrapping experiment (Fig 4).
4. **Fine-tuning on unrelated data reshapes semantic gap unpredictably** → Supported by DPO/PPO experiments on Tulu-2-13B variants (Fig 7).

All main claims have corresponding experiments. The agentic example is illustrative rather than systematic — described more as a demonstration. The claim that "LLMs do not implement algorithms; they approximate them" is well-supported by the ablation chain.

### Baseline Assessment

This is a benchmarking/evaluation paper, so "baselines" are existing LLMs. The model selection (o3, Claude, DeepSeek, Kimi) covers frontier closed and open models. The Tulu-2-13B fine-tuning variants are a sensible controlled experiment.

One gap: the paper does not compare against chain-of-thought prompting or structured reasoning explicitly (beyond the o3 reasoning-effort study). The reasoning-effort experiment with o3 is valuable but covers only one model. Whether explicit scratchpad prompts uniformly change the picture is not tested.

### Dataset Assessment

WhatCounts is a purpose-built minimal benchmark — 6 semantic classes, no duplicates, no distractors. The design is motivated by isolating semantic sensitivity. The benchmark covers: addresses, chemicals, cities, names, phones, symbols.

Potential concern: the benchmark is author-constructed, and the specific classes may interact with training-data frequency in ways that are not controlled. The paper does not explicitly check whether class frequency in pretraining correlates with the semantic gap magnitude (this would be an informative correlation).

No contamination concern in the standard sense — the benchmark is novel and controlled.

### Metric Assessment

Accuracy on counting (exact-match) and semantic gap (max − min accuracy across classes) are appropriate and well-defined. No ambiguity. The gap metric captures the paper's central claim directly.

The paper does not report confidence intervals or bootstrapped standard deviations on the accuracy figures. For benchmarks with small sample sizes per class, this is a meaningful omission.

### Statistical Rigor

**Main weakness**: Statistical significance of the semantic gap is not formally tested. The paper reports raw accuracy differences (e.g., "over 40% variation") but does not provide confidence intervals, bootstrapped error ranges, or significance tests across the 20-questions-per-range design. Given that semantic gaps are the central finding, this is an important gap.

Number of questions per cell: 20 per range (with multiple ranges). At these sample sizes, a 40% accuracy difference is large enough to be unlikely noise, but a 10–15% gap (as seen in some conditions) is less clear-cut without error bars.

The fine-tuning experiment uses a small number of model variants (DPO/PPO × 3 datasets = 6 models), and the differences are described qualitatively rather than with significance tests.

### Ablation Assessment

The paper runs a clean chain of ablation hypotheses (H1–H6 roughly), each eliminating a potential confound:
- H1: Token count → ruled out by token-equalized experiment
- H2: Separator recognition → ruled out by explicit-separator experiment
- H3: Item identifiability → ruled out by XML-wrapping experiment
- H4–H5: Semantic disruption → tested by token-shuffling
- H6: Reasoning effort → tested with o3 effort levels

This is a well-structured ablation design for an evaluation paper. The key components (the claim-supporting experiments) are all individually tested.

The one missing ablation: the paper could have asked whether the gap persists when models are given explicit few-shot examples of different semantic classes (within-context calibration), to separate training-induced bias from in-context adaptability.

### Missing Experiments

**Expected:** Confidence intervals / error bars on the main accuracy figures. At 20 items per range, the variance around each accuracy estimate should be reported to validate that the 40% gap is robust to sampling noise.

**Expected:** A frequency-in-pretraining analysis correlating semantic class gap magnitude with estimated pretraining frequency of each semantic type. This would strengthen or complicate the paper's core mechanistic claim.

**Helpful (not essential):** Few-shot in-context calibration experiment to test whether the gap is in-context-correctable.

### Error Analysis Assessment

The paper does provide class-level breakdowns (Figures 2, 3, 7), which constitute a form of stratified error analysis. Symbols and chemicals tend to show larger gaps; names and cities show more variable behavior.

What is missing: failure-mode analysis within a class. Are there specific list lengths or item values where performance collapses? The paper focuses on the gap between classes but does not analyze structure within each class's failure distribution.

### Scientific-Peer-Reviewer / Hacker Pair

**Scientific Peer Reviewer:** The experimental design is genuinely careful for an evaluation paper — the ablation chain systematically eliminates alternative explanations. The main rigor gap is the lack of confidence intervals on the accuracy figures. Given 20 questions/range and multiple ranges, the paper should report bootstrapped accuracy distributions to validate that the claimed semantic gaps are not sampling artifacts. For an ICML-level evaluation paper, this is a revision-worthy gap but not a fatal one. The core finding — >40% variation under token-count-controlled conditions — is convincing if error bars confirm it.

**Hacker:** To reimplement: the exact prompt template is critical (pipe separator, no explicit separator mention by default). The exact six semantic classes and their members are needed. The paper indicates the benchmark is released, which is helpful. For the fine-tuning experiments, the exact Tulu-2-13B checkpoint and dataset versions are in a reference (Ivison et al., 2024) but not stated in the paper itself. The reduced context-window experiment at 4098 tokens changes the counting range — this detail is mentioned but the specific ranges used are not stated explicitly.

### Sanity Check on the Numbers

The reported >40% semantic gap for o3 (Fig 6 shows Δsem=0.38, 0.41, 0.48 for low/medium/high reasoning effort) is large but not implausible for semantic sensitivity in LLMs. The pattern that higher reasoning effort increases the gap (while not improving average accuracy) is counterintuitive but internally consistent with the paper's thesis. The numbers do not show suspicious rounding or precision patterns.

### Overall Experimental Rigor Verdict

**Mostly rigorous with gaps**

### Justification

The WhatCounts evaluation design is well-controlled and the ablation chain is rigorous for an evaluation paper. The central finding (semantic-class-dependent counting gap) is robust across multiple frontier models and across four control experiments ruling out token count, separator, identifiability, and semantic disruption as confounds. This is a solid empirical design.

The main rigor gaps are: (1) absence of statistical confidence intervals on the accuracy figures, which matters for smaller gaps; (2) no pretraining-frequency correlation analysis; and (3) thin systematic treatment of the agentic setting (which appears as a motivating example rather than a fully evaluated experiment).

The fine-tuning experiments are suggestive but descriptive — the variation pattern across DPO/PPO × datasets is presented as a qualitative finding without significance testing, which limits their evidentiary weight.

None of these gaps undermine the core claim, which rests on large-magnitude, multi-model, multi-ablation evidence. But the absence of error bars on a counting-accuracy benchmark is a meaningful omission at the ICML standard.
