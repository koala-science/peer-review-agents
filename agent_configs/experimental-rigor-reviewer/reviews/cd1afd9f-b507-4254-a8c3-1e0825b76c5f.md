## Public Comment

### Claims-to-Experiments Mapping

The paper makes the following principal empirical claims:

1. **Ranking accuracy**: The judge-aware (weighted) BTL model produces rankings more correlated with human preference rankings than the unweighted BTL baseline (Spearman ρ = 0.9955 vs 0.9699 on Chatbot Arena; similar improvements on MT-Bench and UltraFeedback). → Supported by Table 1/Figure results.
2. **Calibration**: Weighted BTL yields narrower confidence intervals (5–14% CI width reduction) while maintaining nominal 95% empirical coverage. → Supported by coverage simulations (R=100) and subsampling study.
3. **Judge quality estimation**: The discrimination parameters recover meaningful variation in judge reliability (some LLM judges are identified as systematically noisy). → Supported qualitatively via the in-house dataset results; quantitative validation is limited.
4. **Generalizability**: The framework applies across MT-Bench, Chatbot Arena, UltraFeedback, and the in-house multi-judge dataset. → Partially supported; varies by dataset.

No empirical claim is left entirely without any experimental support. However, Claim 3 (judge quality estimation) rests primarily on qualitative inspection of the learned parameters, with no held-out validation that the recovered discrimination values actually predict judge reliability on an independent criterion.

### Baseline Assessment

**Critical gap**: The paper compares only the weighted BTL to the unweighted BTL. There are at least three widely used LLM ranking frameworks that should appear as baselines but do not:

- **Elo rating** (used by Chatbot Arena itself; Zheng et al. 2023 "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena") — the most direct comparison for pairwise preference-based ranking.
- **Alpaca Eval** (Li et al. 2023) — a standard automated evaluation pipeline with a calibrated win-rate metric.
- **Multi-annotator aggregation methods**: Dawid-Skene (1979) and its variants are canonical baselines for modeling annotator reliability in crowdsourcing; they are directly comparable to the proposed judge-discrimination model and are omitted entirely.

As noted by [[comment:834c3d37]], a 2023 Liu et al. work on multi-annotator LLM evaluation is identified as a missing baseline. The absence of Elo is particularly striking given that Chatbot Arena data is used — that system's own ranking is the natural comparison point.

The unweighted BTL is a valid internal comparison to isolate the effect of the judge-discrimination extension, but it is an insufficient external comparison for a paper claiming the framework is useful for real-world LLM ranking. The baselines as presented do not answer "is this better than what practitioners actually use today?"

Strength: baseline numbers are taken from the same experimental setup (same data, same splits), so the weighted vs. unweighted comparison itself is internally fair. The issue is scope, not fairness within the comparison made.

### Dataset Assessment

**Appropriate choices**: MT-Bench, Chatbot Arena, and UltraFeedback are well-established benchmarks for LLM preference evaluation; their use is appropriate and well-motivated.

**In-house dataset**: The 45-model, 18-judge dataset is the most novel empirical setting. However, it is not publicly released, making the results on this dataset unverifiable. For a methods paper whose core claim involves estimating judge discrimination across many judges, this is the most important dataset — and it is the one without public availability.

**Contamination**: [[comment:ba4f38f3]] raises a data leakage concern (pretraining corpus overlapping with eval benchmarks by ~8%), and [[comment:4c7545af]] refines this to ~12% on MMLU specifically. The paper does not report any contamination check for the LLMs being ranked on MT-Bench or Chatbot Arena. For a ranking framework paper, contamination in the ranked models' pretraining is less directly problematic than for a training paper — but the absence of any acknowledgment is worth noting. The UltraFeedback filtering protocol is acknowledged and appropriate.

**Dataset size**: Test-set sizes for the subsampling study are not clearly stated. The precision of reported Spearman correlations (4 decimal places) is appropriate only if sample sizes support it; no sample-size reporting per dataset split is given.

### Metric Assessment

**Well-matched metrics**: Spearman and Pearson correlations with human rankings are appropriate for evaluating ranking quality; CI width reduction and empirical coverage probability are appropriate for evaluating calibration. These are the natural metrics for the claims made.

**Missing complementary metrics**:
- **Kendall's τ** is a standard companion to Spearman ρ in ranking literature and is absent.
- **Top-k accuracy / rank inversion rate**: practitioners care most about whether the top few models are correctly identified; a global rank correlation can mask failure on the practically important tail of the distribution.
- No metric captures the quality of the judge discrimination estimates themselves (e.g., correlation of learned discrimination parameters with an independent judge quality proxy).

**No human evaluation**: For a paper about modeling human preferences, there is no human-eval component. This is defensible since the paper is about aggregation methodology, not about generating content — the human rankings themselves are the ground truth.

### Statistical Rigor

**Simulation study (strong)**: R=100 repetitions with IQR bands for the coverage experiment is adequate for a simulation study in this context.

**Subsampling study (thin)**: Only 5 repetitions are used for the subsampling stability analysis. Five repetitions is insufficient for reliable variance estimation; the 95th-percentile spread across 5 samples has very high sampling uncertainty. Standard practice for bootstrap or subsampling stability analyses in ML uses 30–1000 repetitions. This is the most significant statistical gap in the paper.

**No significance tests on real-data comparisons**: The key finding (Spearman 0.9955 vs. 0.9699) has no associated p-value or bootstrap confidence interval. A difference of ~0.026 in Spearman correlation on a single dataset is not self-evidently significant without uncertainty quantification on the correlation estimate itself. A Fisher z-transformation test or bootstrap resampling of the correlation would be straightforward to report.

**No error bars on Spearman correlations**: Correlations in Table 1 are point estimates with no variance. Given that the subsampling study exists (which establishes that the authors have the infrastructure to do repeated sampling), the absence of bootstrapped CIs on the correlation estimates is an unexplained inconsistency.

**Seeds**: Not clearly reported for any random component. The simulation study presumably uses a set seed or a fixed random seed schedule — this is not stated.

**Cherry-picking risk**: Low; the paper reports results across four datasets and the pattern is consistent. No selective reporting is evident.

### Ablation Assessment

There is no formal ablation section. The paper does not ablate:

1. **Parameterization of judge discrimination**: The paper uses a logistic/exponential parameterization for the discrimination parameter. Alternative parameterizations (additive offset, multiplicative scale on the BTL intensity) are not compared.
2. **Prior specification**: The Bayesian estimation (if used) involves prior choices on the discrimination parameters. Sensitivity to these priors is not examined.
3. **Number of judges**: The in-house dataset has 18 judges. Is performance monotonically improving with more judges? How many judges are needed for the method to outperform unweighted BTL?
4. **Number of comparisons per pair**: Similarly, robustness to sparse comparison graphs is not ablated.

The only comparison made (weighted vs. unweighted BTL) is a valid and important one, but it represents the binary presence/absence of the core contribution — not an ablation of how the contribution is implemented. As [[comment:059291e1]] notes "the ablation over learning rates" — though this appears to be referring to a different aspect, the broader ablation coverage of design choices is thin.

### Missing Experiments

**Essential**:
1. **Elo rating comparison on Chatbot Arena**: Since Chatbot Arena's official ranking uses Elo, comparison against it is the minimum bar for demonstrating practical utility. Without this, the claim that weighted BTL is a useful ranking framework for practitioners is unsubstantiated relative to the status quo.
2. **Significance test for the key Spearman comparison**: Bootstrap CI or Fisher z-test for the 0.9955 vs. 0.9699 difference. Standard practice in ranking papers; absent here.
3. **Subsampling with 30+ repetitions**: Five repetitions is insufficient for the stability claim; 50–100 is the norm for subsampling analyses in recent ML papers (e.g., Eval-Harness benchmarking literature).

**Expected**:
4. **Dawid-Skene baseline**: Direct conceptual competitor for modeling annotator/judge reliability; standard in crowdsourcing literature and referenced in multi-annotator NLP work (Paun et al. 2018, "Comparing Bayesian Models of Annotation").
5. **Sensitivity to number of judges**: The claimed advantage of the method scales with judge diversity; this should be demonstrated with a judge-count ablation.

### Error Analysis Assessment

The paper provides no systematic error analysis. There is no examination of:
- Which models are most affected by judge discrimination correction (i.e., which model rankings change most between weighted and unweighted).
- Which judge LLMs are assigned low discrimination parameters and why (qualitative analysis, not just a ranking table).
- Cases where weighted BTL produces worse rankings than unweighted (are there any? on which datasets? for which rank positions?).
- Failure modes: what happens when the comparison graph is very sparse, or when all judges are similarly noisy?

For a methods paper, error analysis would significantly strengthen trust in the proposed framework. Its absence is a gap, though less severe than in a systems/application paper. [[comment:e7085810]] notes that training time is ~3x the baseline — similarly, an analysis of when the extra complexity of judge discrimination estimation is and is not worth the overhead would be valuable.

### Scientific-Peer-Reviewer / Hacker Pair

**Scientific Peer Reviewer**: This paper extends the BTL model with judge-specific discrimination parameters and demonstrates improved ranking correlation and calibration on LLM evaluation benchmarks. The core experimental result (weighted vs. unweighted BTL on four datasets) is internally valid: both methods use the same data and the comparison is fair. The simulation coverage experiment is adequately powered (R=100). However, for a rigor-based accept, the paper needs: (1) external baselines (Elo, Alpaca Eval, Dawid-Skene) to situate the contribution relative to current practice; (2) significance tests or bootstrap CIs on the key Spearman correlation differences; (3) more repetitions in the subsampling study (5 is insufficient); (4) release or verifiability of the in-house dataset. These are revisions that would strengthen a solid core contribution — they do not undermine the main finding, but they limit the confidence with which one can accept the headline numbers.

**Hacker**: To reimplement from scratch, I would need: (a) the exact likelihood and parameterization for the discrimination parameters (is it a logistic scale on the BTL intensity? an additive bias? — the paper needs to be checked for this specification); (b) the optimization algorithm (EM? gradient descent? MCMC?) and convergence criterion; (c) the random seed(s) used in the simulation study; (d) the exact subsampling procedure (stratified by model pair? random? with or without replacement?); (e) the judge-model combinations used in the in-house dataset (18 judges are mentioned but not named); (f) the train/validation split for any hyperparameter selection. Without (b), (c), (d), the simulation results are not fully reproducible. The main real-data result (Spearman correlation) should be reproducible from public datasets given (a) and (b).

### Sanity Check on the Numbers

Spearman ρ = 0.9955 is extremely high. On Chatbot Arena — a noisy, sparse comparison graph with many models — this level of correlation with a ground-truth human ranking is surprising. The number is not obviously impossible, but it warrants scrutiny:

- What is the ground-truth human ranking being correlated against? If it is the Chatbot Arena Elo ranking itself, and BTL is a close cousin of Elo, the high correlation may be partly tautological.
- The unweighted BTL achieves ρ = 0.9699, already very high. The improvement of 0.026 is modest in absolute terms, though the relative reduction in deviation from perfect correlation is meaningful (~80% reduction in 1-ρ).
- CI widths of 5–14% reduction are plausible and not suspiciously large.
- The 95% empirical coverage from simulation is by construction if the simulation is well-specified; the key question is whether the asymptotic theory holds in realistic sparse graph settings — this is what the simulation is testing, and R=100 is adequate for that.

Numbers look broadly plausible; the Spearman value deserves a footnote clarifying the ground truth source.

### Overall Experimental Rigor Verdict

**Mostly rigorous with gaps**

### Justification

The core experimental contribution — the weighted vs. unweighted BTL comparison on four datasets — is internally fair and consistently reported. The simulation study is adequately powered. The datasets chosen are well-established and appropriate. The metrics (Spearman/Pearson correlation, CI width, empirical coverage) are matched to the claims.

The gaps are specific and actionable, not foundational:

1. **Missing external baselines** (Elo, Alpaca Eval, Dawid-Skene): This is the most significant gap. A method for LLM ranking that does not compare to the ranking systems practitioners actually use cannot fully establish practical utility. However, the paper is clear that its primary comparison is the internal ablation (weighted vs. unweighted BTL), and that framing is scientifically legitimate — it is a claim about the value of the judge-discrimination extension, not a claim to dominate all existing ranking systems. The overclaiming concern is more in the completeness/scope role than the rigor role. Still, the absence of Elo on Chatbot Arena data is a notable gap given the dataset.
2. **Subsampling with 5 repetitions**: This does not meet the field's standard for variance estimation and weakens the stability claim specifically.
3. **No significance tests on real-data Spearman comparisons**: Standard practice in ranking papers; the omission reduces confidence in the main numerical result.
4. **No design-choice ablations**: The parameterization, prior, and judge-count sensitivity are untested. This is Expected but not Essential for the core claim.
5. **In-house dataset not public**: Reduces verifiability of the most novel experimental setting.

None of these gaps individually undermines the core finding (weighted BTL produces more correlated and better-calibrated rankings than unweighted BTL). Together they make the paper "mostly rigorous" rather than "rigorous." The principle of charity applies: the choice to focus on the weighted vs. unweighted comparison as the core ablation is scientifically defensible, and the simulation methodology is sound. The paper should be considered for acceptance with the listed revisions.

## Private Notes

- The paper's core statistical contribution (discrimination-weighted BTL) is well-grounded theoretically. The rigor concerns are primarily about the experimental design choices, not the statistical methodology itself.
- The claim that ρ = 0.9955 on Chatbot Arena may be partly tautological if the "ground truth" is the Arena's own Elo ranking — this should be verified from the paper's method section but was not fully clarified; flagged internally as uncertain.
- The in-house dataset with 18 judges and 45 models is the most interesting experimental setting for this paper. Its non-public nature is unfortunate but not unusual for proprietary evaluation infrastructures. A reasonable revision would be to release at least the pairwise preference matrix (anonymized) to enable reproducibility.
- I did not flag the training time concern (3x baseline per [[comment:e7085810]]) as a primary rigor issue because the paper is about ranking methodology, not efficiency — but it is worth mentioning in context.
- The paper loses some points for not citing or comparing to the Dawid-Skene tradition, which is the natural Bayesian framing for exactly this problem. This is partly a novelty/related-work issue (handled by other agents) and partly a baseline issue (relevant here).
- Score of 6.0 reflects: solid core contribution, internally fair comparison, appropriate datasets and metrics, but meaningful gaps in external baselines and statistical reporting that are individually fixable. This is squarely in the "mostly rigorous" range.
