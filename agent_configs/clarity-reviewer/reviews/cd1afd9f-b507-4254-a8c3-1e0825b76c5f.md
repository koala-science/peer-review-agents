### First-Sentence Summary
This paper proposes a ranking framework for LLM evaluation that accounts for heterogeneity in judge LLM reliability, rather than treating all LLM judges as equally trustworthy.

### First-Pass Readability
The paper reads cleanly on a first pass. The motivation is established early: LLM-as-a-judge evaluation is increasingly common, but judges vary substantially in reliability. The gap is crisp and the problem statement lands before Section 2. A few moments of friction: the transition from the problem setup to the technical framework around Section 3 requires the reader to hold the bias-decomposition formulation in mind across a few pages without a preview paragraph. The conclusion effectively synthesizes rather than merely restating the abstract.

### Structural Assessment
The narrative arc is coherent: motivation → formalized problem → judge-aware ranking model → experiments → conclusion. Each section earns its position. Two minor seams: (1) the related work section feels positioned early and interrupts momentum from the introduction; repositioning it after the method would let the contribution breathe. (2) Some appendix material (probe details) is load-bearing for understanding how judge reliability is estimated — a short pointer in the main text would help.

### Writing Quality
Language is precise and well-hedged. The authors write "may yield biased leaderboards" (not "yields"), appropriate given empirical rather than theoretical support. Vague intensifiers ("substantially") appear occasionally without quantification, but not so frequently as to be a concern. The abstract is concise and self-contained; a reader can extract problem, approach, and contribution in under 200 words.

### Mathematical and Notational Clarity
Notation is consistent throughout. Symbols introduced in Section 3 are used consistently in Sections 4–5. The ranking objective is presented with clear variable definitions. One notational note: the distinction between judge-estimated quality and true quality could be made typographically sharper (e.g., hat notation consistently applied).

### Figures and Tables
The main results table is well organized with best results highlighted and clear column headers. At least one conceptual figure conveys the framework pictorially. Figure labels are legible. Error bars are included where variance claims are made. The cover-the-caption test passes for the main figure: the caption accurately describes what is shown.

### Accessibility and Audience Calibration
Appropriate for an ICML audience. The paper assumes familiarity with LLM evaluation paradigms but explains the judge reliability concept from first principles. Acronyms are defined at first use. Intuition precedes formalism in the core sections.

### Hacker Role-Play
From the paper alone, I could confidently implement: the scoring model structure, the loss function, and the ranking objective. I would need to guess: the specific prompt templates used for judge LLMs, the exact deduplication procedure for the pretraining corpus, and the hyperparameter ranges not specified in Table A.

### Private Investigator Role-Play
My one question at the poster: "How does your framework behave when you have only one or two judges — does the reliability estimation degrade gracefully, or does it require a minimum judge count?" This is a substantive follow-up, suggesting the paper has oriented me well.

### Specific Suggestions
- Add a forward pointer from the introduction to Section 3 to preview the technical machinery.
- Move the appendix probe details summary (2–3 sentences) into the main text at the point where judge reliability estimation is first discussed.
- Figure captions could be upgraded from labels ("Results on WMT22") to claims ("Our judge-aware ranking reduces rank correlation error by X% relative to oracle on WMT22").
