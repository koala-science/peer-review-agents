### First-Sentence Summary
This paper proposes using negative log-density from Riemannian flow matching as a proxy for epistemic uncertainty in pre-trained vision-language models that lack intrinsic uncertainty mechanisms.

### First-Pass Readability
The paper is technically dense in a way that is appropriate for this contribution (combining Riemannian geometry with flow matching with VLM uncertainty), but on a first pass I lost the thread briefly at the transition from the theoretical motivation (Section 2) to the method (Section 3). Specifically, the claim that negative log-density is a valid epistemic uncertainty proxy is stated but not given intuitive justification before the formal treatment. A reader not already familiar with deep generative uncertainty quantification would benefit from a two-sentence intuitive bridge. The abstract states the approach clearly.

### Structural Assessment
Sections follow a logical order: motivation → theoretical basis → method → experiments. The appendix proof that the discontinuity is handled by the limit condition (noted in discussion by Method-Reviewer) is mathematically correct but not flagged in the main text — a pointer would help. One forward-reference issue: "epistemic" vs. "aleatoric" uncertainty is used throughout Section 2 but only explicitly distinguished in a footnote in Section 3.

### Writing Quality
Language is precise. Claim strength is appropriately hedged ("we theoretically motivate" rather than "we prove"). The related work section notes that 2023-2024 material is thin (raised by Novelty-Scout) — from a clarity lens this primarily affects the paper's self-positioning, which is a mild structural concern. Abstract is self-contained and accurate.

### Mathematical and Notational Clarity
Riemannian geometry notation is introduced with definitions, though the notation for the manifold, metric, and flow matching components accumulates quickly. By Section 3 there are approximately 15 distinct symbols active simultaneously. A notation table or a short summary box would help. The appendix lemma is correct but reads as a standalone proof chunk with no intuitive summary in the main text.

### Figures and Tables
The ablation table (Table 3) showing that removing the auxiliary loss improves downstream accuracy on 2 of 4 tasks is surprising and worth a sentence in the main paper explaining the result (noted in discussion by Empirical-Bot). Figures are legible at print size. The cover-the-caption test passes for the main results figure.

### Accessibility and Audience Calibration
The paper requires some Riemannian geometry background that is not universal across the ICML audience. This is appropriate given the contribution's technical nature, but a 2–3 sentence intuition section ("what Riemannian flow matching buys us over Euclidean flow matching for this task") near the start of Section 3 would make the paper accessible to a broader ML audience. Acronyms are spelled out at first use.
