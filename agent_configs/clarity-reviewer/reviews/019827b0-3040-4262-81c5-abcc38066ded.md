### First-Sentence Summary
PILD proposes a framework for incorporating physical law constraints into diffusion model training and sampling, targeting scientific machine learning applications where sampled distributions must satisfy known physical laws.

### First-Pass Readability
The abstract has an artifact: it begins with commented-out LaTeX lines ("%" characters at the start of several sentences), suggesting the final abstract was not copy-edited from the LaTeX source before submission. This is a minor but nonzero clarity concern as it signals hasty preparation. Beyond the abstract, the paper reads acceptably on one pass but requires effort at the transition from background to method. The claim that "sampled data distributions often satisfy certain physical laws" is vague at first use — the type of physical law (PDE? conservation law? symmetry?) is not specified until Section 3.

### Structural Assessment
The structure is logical: motivation → framework → theory → experiments. However, the motivation section does not clearly distinguish PILD from prior physics-informed neural network (PINN) approaches until late in the related work section. A reader might reasonably wonder "how is this different from a constrained diffusion model?" for the first two pages. An earlier disambiguation sentence would substantially reduce reading friction.

### Writing Quality
Language is generally precise. The contribution paragraph names the framework, its theoretical properties, and its empirical scope — adequate for orientation. The abstract artifact (commented-out lines) is worth fixing in revision. The claim "increasingly important role in a growing number of tasks" in the abstract is vague and could be replaced with concrete examples.

### Mathematical and Notational Clarity
The physical constraint formulation is clearly defined. The PILD framework equations are labeled and referenced correctly. Some notational choices are idiosyncratic (using calligraphic script for the constraint set when blackboard bold is more standard in physics-informed ML), but internally consistent. The ablation in Table 3 showing auxiliary loss removal improves performance on 2/4 tasks is unexplained — from a clarity standpoint this is a loose end that a reader would flag.

### Figures and Tables
Figures are legible and labeled. The main results plot shows clear improvements. Error bars are present. The cover-the-caption test passes. No table has more than 15 columns.

### Accessibility and Audience Calibration
The paper targets scientific ML, which is an ICML audience but requires some domain explanation. The authors note physical constraints without specifying whether they mean PDEs, symmetry groups, or conservation laws in the introduction. This is the main accessibility failure: an ML reader outside scientific computing cannot immediately contextualize the contribution's scope.

### Hacker Role-Play
I could confidently reimplement: the training objective, the projection step for constraint satisfaction, and the evaluation protocol. I would need to guess: the specific PDE discretization scheme, the exact boundary condition handling, and the constraint tolerance parameters.

### Private Investigator Role-Play
My poster question: "How does PILD handle stiff physical constraints where the projection step might interfere with the diffusion denoising trajectory?" This is a substantive follow-up suggesting the paper has oriented me to the key technical tension.

### Specific Suggestions
- Fix the abstract artifact: remove the commented-out "%" lines before final submission.
- Specify "physical laws" type (PDE / conservation law / symmetry) in the introduction before Section 3.
- Add an early sentence distinguishing PILD from PINN approaches.
- Explain the Table 3 ablation result (auxiliary loss removal).
- Replace "increasingly important role in a growing number of tasks" with concrete examples.
