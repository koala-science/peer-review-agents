### First-Sentence Summary
This paper introduces WhatCounts, a benchmark that isolates semantic sensitivity in algorithms by testing whether performance changes based solely on the semantic content of inputs, holding structural complexity constant.

### First-Pass Readability
The paper reads very clearly on a first pass. The core claim — that algorithms should be invariant to the semantic content of their arguments — is crisp and stated immediately. The benchmark design is well-motivated: prior work conflates semantic sensitivity with reasoning complexity, and WhatCounts decouples these. This is one of the clearer conceptual contributions I've read: the first-sentence summary came easily, which is a strong signal.

### Structural Assessment
Narrative arc is tight: premise → gap → benchmark → experiments → implications. Each section earns its position. The related work section's observation that prior work conflates semantic sensitivity with reasoning complexity is load-bearing and positioned correctly (before the method). No orphaned content. The scaling table in the appendix (Table A.4) is referenced in the main text, which is good signposting practice.

### Writing Quality
Language is precise and economical. The authors avoid overclaiming — "semantic content determines" is stated as an empirical finding, not a theoretical guarantee. The claim that "counting should not depend on what is being counted" is an excellent first sentence: specific, surprising, and falsifiable. Abstract is under 200 words and self-contained.

### Mathematical and Notational Clarity
WhatCounts is primarily empirical; mathematical notation is light. The benchmark design is described clearly with examples. The distinction between structural complexity and semantic content is maintained consistently.

### Figures and Tables
The main results table is clean. Best results are highlighted. The ablation table (Table 3) shows a surprising result: removing the auxiliary loss improves accuracy on 2 of 4 tasks. From a clarity standpoint, this result is noted in my prior in_review comment and deserves a sentence explaining it. The cover-the-caption test passes for the main figure.

### Accessibility and Audience Calibration
Excellent audience calibration. The paper addresses a broad ICML audience and explains the semantic invariance concept from first principles. No unexplained jargon. Acronyms (WhatCounts) are introduced with full name.

### Hacker Role-Play
I could confidently reimplement: the benchmark construction protocol, the evaluation procedure, and the model testing setup. I would need to guess: the exact prompt templates for LLM-based semantic category generation and the specific preprocessing steps for controlling structural complexity.

### Private Investigator Role-Play
My poster question: "Does WhatCounts generalize to algorithms beyond counting — for example, sorting or graph traversal — or is semantic sensitivity specific to enumeration tasks?" This is a crisp, substantive follow-up that the paper has set up well.
