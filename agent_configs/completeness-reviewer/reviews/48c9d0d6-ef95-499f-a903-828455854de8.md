### Claim Ledger

**Method claims:**
- A controlled pretraining study isolates contamination effects on *generative* evaluations, distinct from discriminative ones.
- A scaling law ℒ(C,R) = E(R) + C₀(R)·C^(−α(R)) captures how loss changes with compute C and contamination replica count R.
- Three distinct memorization regimes are formally characterized: Exponentially Fast Decoherence (E>0), Brittle Memorization (E≈0, α≤1), and Deterministic Lock-In (E≈0, α>1).
- High sampling temperature acts as a "truth serum" that decouples contamination gains from robust generalization.
- The study discovers and documents a significant bug in EleutherAI's Language Model Evaluation Harness (gold reference solutions receiving only ~70% Math Verify score instead of 100%).

**Empirical claims:**
- Performance improves systematically with contamination level and model size, mirroring prior discriminative findings.
- Even one test set replica enables models to achieve lower loss than the irreducible error of uncontaminated training.
- Contamination gains stem from verbatim memorization, not acquisition of mathematical reasoning (evidenced by performance collapse when problems are rephrased or numerically perturbed).
- Supervised fine-tuning counterintuitively helps low-contamination but hurts high-contamination models.
- Overtraining on fresh data dilutes contamination effects.
- Increasing temperature from 0 to 1 reduces performance ~2x at low contamination vs. ~40x at high contamination.
- Longer solutions are exponentially harder to memorize than shorter ones.

**Theoretical claims:**
- The irreducible error E(R) shrinks from 3.594 (uncontaminated) to 0.0347 (316 replicas), representing a fundamental departure from standard scaling law assumptions.
- The three-regime theory provides a principled explanation of why generative contamination behaves differently from discriminative contamination.

### Claim-Evidence Scope Analysis

- **"Performance improves systematically with contamination and model size"** — **Fully supported.** Tested across 5 model sizes (34M–344M parameters) and 8 contamination levels (0–3162 replicas) with systematic scaling law fitting.

- **"Gains stem from verbatim memorization, not reasoning"** — **Fully supported.** Evidenced by performance collapse under rephrasing and numerical perturbation (Table 1). This is a strong and clean experimental test.

- **"Even one replica enables lower loss than irreducible error of uncontaminated corpus"** — **Fully supported** for the tested models and corpus. The scaling law analysis is rigorous and the finding is striking.

- **"High temperature acts as truth serum"** — **Partially supported.** Demonstrated for MATH at 6 temperature settings. The claim is stated broadly but only tested in this one context; the mechanism (disproportionate amplification of memorization uncertainty) is plausible but the claim's generalizability to other benchmarks/domains is not evidenced.

- **"Longer solutions are exponentially harder to memorize"** — **Partially supported.** The theoretical regime characterization explains this, but empirical validation directly varying solution length is not explicitly described in the results. The relationship emerges from the model structure but isn't verified as a standalone ablation.

- **"Supervised fine-tuning counterintuitively hurts high-contamination models"** — **Fully supported** within the experimental setup. The mechanism (SFT amplifies memorized solutions for high contamination, then attempts decontamination) is consistent with theory.

- **"Framework generalizes to generative evaluations broadly"** — **Overclaimed.** The abstract implies broad applicability ("generative evaluations"), but the entire study uses MATH only. The conclusion discusses coding and creative writing as unexplored territory, but the title and abstract suggest broader scope than one benchmark.

### Missing Experiments and Analyses

**Essential:**
- *Fine-tuning contamination (instruction tuning stage)*: Real-world contamination often happens at the instruction fine-tuning stage (e.g., benchmark problems appearing in SFT datasets), not just pretraining. This study only examines pretraining contamination. The mechanisms and relative severity likely differ substantially — this is not mentioned as a limitation, yet it is a critical gap for practitioners.

**Expected:**
- *Paraphrase/near-duplicate contamination*: The study uses exact-copy contamination (verbatim test set replicas). In practice, contamination often occurs as paraphrased or slightly modified versions of benchmark problems. The rephrasing test (Table 1) goes in this direction but only probes the *test-time* side; it would be valuable to also pretrain on paraphrased contamination and measure effects.
- *Additional benchmarks*: The paper limits to MATH. GSM8K (simpler math), HumanEval (code generation), and MBPP are standard generative benchmarks with known contamination risks. At least one additional benchmark would substantially strengthen claims about "generative evaluations" as a class.
- *Larger model sizes*: 344M parameters is very small relative to operational frontier models (7B–405B). The authors have a resource constraint here (acknowledged), but a brief discussion of whether the scaling laws extrapolate — or why they might break — would be valuable.

**Not essential (not raised in public comment):**
- Cross-architecture comparison (MoE, Mamba, encoder-decoder)
- Few-shot vs. zero-shot evaluation under contamination
- Multi-epoch training effects

### Hidden Assumptions

1. **Exact-copy contamination** — The contamination model assumes verbatim test set replicas inserted into the pretraining corpus. This is a clean experimental control but may not represent the most common real-world contamination mode. *Stated?* No. *Reasonable?* Yes, for controlled study design. *Testable?* Yes (paraphrase contamination variant). *If violated?* The three-regime theory would need modification; brittle memorization may behave differently under fuzzy contamination.

2. **Compute-optimal (Chinchilla) pretraining regime** — All models are trained at 20 tokens-per-parameter. This constrains the generalization of findings to models trained in different regimes (over-trained small models, which are increasingly common in practice). *Stated?* Partially (mentioned in methodology, not called out as a scope limitation). *Reasonable?* Yes for a controlled study. *If violated?* E(R) values and regime boundaries would shift.

3. **MATH as representative of generative evaluations** — The theoretical framing (three regimes, scaling laws) is derived from MATH properties. Results may not transfer to generation tasks with different solution length distributions, structural constraints, or scoring mechanisms (e.g., creative writing scored by LLM judges). *Stated?* Partially (listed in limitations). *Impact?* Moderate — limits the actionability of the "truth serum" temperature finding for non-math tasks.

4. **Random uniform distribution of test replicas in pretraining corpus** — Contamination is simulated by inserting replicas uniformly. Real contamination from web crawls clusters around specific documents and has non-uniform repetition patterns. *Stated?* No.

5. **Math Verify scoring is correct** — The paper discovers a bug in EleutherAI's harness. The corrected evaluator is used throughout. *Stated?* Yes, the bug is documented. Appropriate.

### Deployment Pair

**Industry Practitioner pitch:** This paper provides the first quantitative study of test set contamination on generative (free-form) math evaluations, offering precise scaling laws and a diagnostic framework (temperature probing, solution-length analysis) that practitioners can apply today. The core finding — that even one replica can break standard scaling law assumptions — directly informs how labs should structure data deduplication pipelines and benchmark hygiene. The three memorization regime characterization gives evaluators a principled framework to distinguish robust generalization from contamination artifacts.

**Industry Practitioner deployment concerns:** (a) All results are at ≤344M parameters; whether the regime boundaries and temperature "truth serum" effect hold at 7B–70B is completely unvalidated. A practitioner deploying this diagnostic on a 70B model is extrapolating many orders of magnitude beyond the study. (b) The study only addresses pretraining contamination; if your contamination risk is in the instruction fine-tuning stage (e.g., ChatML-formatted benchmark problems in an SFT dataset), the authors provide no guidance. (c) The temperature diagnostic requires the ability to run the model at multiple temperatures and access to known-clean holdout problems — in practice, this is feasible for open models but not for API-only models where temperature behavior is underspecified.

**Hacker (reimplementation) paragraph:** The paper is admirably transparent about its setup: Qwen 3 architecture, 20 tokens-per-parameter compute budget, logarithmically-uniform contamination levels (0–3162), 6 temperature settings, 5 model sizes. The LM Eval Harness bug fix is documented. However, the exact contamination insertion code (how replicas are interleaved with training documents, deduplication policy for non-contamination data) is not described at sufficient detail to replicate. The Math Verify evaluation correction needs the exact implementation of the fix to reproduce. The scaling law fitting procedure (choice of functional form, fitting method, error bars) is described but the appendix details would need verification. Overall, implementation-complete enough for replication with moderate effort, but not trivial.

### Limitations Section Audit

The paper's stated limitations are:
1. Single benchmark focus (MATH only) ✓ *Specific, honest, and appropriately prominent*
2. Limited to decoder-only dense transformers up to 344M ✓ *Specific and honest*
3. Specific pretraining corpus mixture may not generalize ✓ *Specific*
4. Results unclear for other domains (coding, creative writing) ✓ *Specific, follows from #1*

**Assessment:**
- *Specificity*: Good — the limitations are specific to this work, not generic placeholders.
- *Severity honesty*: The limitations section understates one important omission: **fine-tuning contamination** is never mentioned. This is a separate and practically important contamination scenario that the pretraining-centric framing implicitly excludes. Practitioners reading this paper might reasonably assume the findings apply to SFT-contamination, but there is no evidence or caution about this.
- *Constructiveness*: The limitations suggest future directions implicitly (more benchmarks, larger models) but don't explain what specific mechanisms might change or why the findings might not transfer.
- *Completeness*: Missing the assumption of exact-copy contamination, the fine-tuning contamination gap, and the implications of the LM Eval Harness bug for the broader community.

**Credit where due:** The authors are honest about the single-benchmark limitation and explicitly say "results unclear for other domains" — this is scientific maturity. They also discovered and disclosed an existing tool bug, which adds value.

### Negative Results and Failure Modes

The paper reports several constructive negative results:
- Supervised fine-tuning *hurts* heavily contaminated models (counterintuitive but fully discussed and explained).
- Overtraining on fresh data only *partially* mitigates contamination at high replica counts.
- Performance collapses under rephrasing/perturbation (negative result for generalization claims — handled honestly).

Conspicuously absent: The paper does not report cases where the three-regime theoretical framework *fails* to predict observed behavior, or conditions where the temperature "truth serum" diagnostic doesn't cleanly separate contaminated from clean models. The theoretical framework is presented without falsifying tests.

### Scope Verdict

The claims broadly match the evidence for the specific conditions tested (MATH benchmark, exact-copy pretraining contamination, decoder-only models 34M–344M), but the title and abstract framing ("generative evaluations" broadly) overstates the generality of findings that are firmly grounded in a single benchmark and contamination mode.

### Justification

This is a carefully designed controlled study with honest scoping and good experimental coverage *within its chosen setting*. The five model sizes × eight contamination levels × six temperature settings × multiple SFT conditions provide a thorough empirical grid. The three-regime theoretical framework is elegant and grounded in the data.

The honest scoping: the authors explicitly acknowledge the single-benchmark limitation, the model size ceiling, and the corpus specificity. They discovered and reported an important tool bug. These are marks of scientific integrity.

The minor gaps that prevent a "Complete" verdict:

1. **Fine-tuning contamination is unaddressed and undiscussed** as a limitation. This is not appropriate scoping — it is a practical gap that practitioners will hit immediately when trying to apply the paper's insights.

2. **The "generative evaluations" framing in the title/abstract** claims scope the experiments don't deliver. One benchmark is one benchmark. This is a classic scope mismatch between claim and evidence.

3. **The exact-copy contamination assumption** is not flagged as a scope boundary, even though paraphrase contamination is a common real-world mode.

4. **The temperature "truth serum" claim** is presented as broadly applicable but only tested in one context.

I apply the principle of charity: these gaps do not fundamentally undermine the paper's main contribution, which is well-executed and important. The core finding (even one replica breaks scaling law assumptions) is fully supported and valuable. The deployment concerns are not fatal; they are reasonable research scope decisions given compute constraints. The right verdict is "mostly complete with minor gaps" at the higher end of the scale.
