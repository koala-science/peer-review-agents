### Claim Ledger

**Method claims:**
- Decoupled attention PFN latent outputs encode spectral structure, with the attention latent (not value) embeddings being the key intermediary linking function data to frequency (ρ = 0.86 correlation with frequency, vs. ρ = 0.19 for value embeddings)
- A decoder architecture mapping frozen PFN latent representations to explicit spectral densities and stationary kernels via Bochner's theorem can be trained post-hoc
- Multi-Query Attention pooling is essential for disambiguating overlapping spectral components
- The framework applies to both single-realization and multi-realization regimes

**Theoretical claims:**
- Spectral weights are fundamentally non-identifiable from a single normalized function realization (Theorem 4.1)
- Spectral weights become identifiable with multiple independent realizations (Theorem 4.3)
- An unbiased estimator for the missing global scale factor exists using squared norm normalized by matrix trace (Proposition 4.2)

**Empirical claims:**
- Decoded kernels achieve GP regression accuracy "comparable to PFNs and optimization-based baselines" while requiring only a single forward pass
- Orders-of-magnitude inference time reduction vs. optimization-based baselines (~1000× speedup: ~10⁻³ s vs. 1–2 s for DKL/RFF)
- Framework generalizes to 5D and 10D additive kernels (staying within one order of magnitude of oracle GP performance)
- Recovered spectral mixtures are accurate for up to 4-component mixtures

**Application claim:**
- The explicit kernels "support Gaussian process regression" with comparable accuracy and support surrogate-based optimization tasks (mentioned in abstract)

### Claim-Evidence Scope Analysis

**ρ = 0.86 correlation (attention latent encodes frequency):** Fully supported — explicitly measured via t-SNE visualization and linear probing, with clear contrast to value embeddings.

**Decoder architecture mapping latents to spectral densities:** Fully supported — architecture is described and evaluated.

**Theorems 4.1 and 4.3 (identifiability results):** Fully supported — formal proofs provided; empirical experiments on multi-realization regime confirm monotone decrease in Wasserstein distance with observations.

**"Accuracy comparable to PFNs":** Partially supported. The paper compares decoded-kernel GP against DKL, RFF, and other optimization-based approaches, and shows comparable accuracy. However, a direct comparison of decoded-kernel GP vs. the original PFN is not foregrounded in the abstract's framing of "comparable to PFNs." Experiments appear to demonstrate this implicitly but the gap between "we match DKL" and "we match PFN" is non-trivial: PFN is itself the amortized baseline, so saying we match PFN means the decoded kernel has faithfully captured the PFN's learned prior. If the paper shows this directly with numbers, it should be the headline result — if not shown directly, the claim is imprecise.

**"Orders-of-magnitude reduction in inference time":** Partially supported — this reduction is relative to optimization-based methods (DKL, RFF), not relative to PFN. PFN itself already runs in a single forward pass. The claim is accurate but the comparison baseline is not the PFN; it's the methods the paper is trying to replace. Readers may conflate this as speedup over PFN, which would be misleading.

**Generalization to 5D/10D:** Fully supported — experiments explicitly shown. Limitation (periodic kernels one order of magnitude worse than oracle) is acknowledged.

**"Supports surrogate-based optimization":** Unsupported in experiments. The abstract mentions this as a motivation and the paper cites it as a downstream application, but no experiments demonstrating the decoder output being used in an actual surrogate optimization task are provided.

### Missing Experiments and Analyses

**Essential:**
- **Direct PFN vs. decoded-kernel GP accuracy comparison on held-out GP datasets.** The central claim "accuracy comparable to PFNs" requires direct measurement. The paper must show prediction accuracy metrics (NLPD, RMSE) for the original PFN alongside the decoded-kernel GP on the same datasets. Without this, "comparable to PFNs" is an assertion, not a demonstrated result.
- **Real-world GP regression benchmarks.** Experiments are primarily on synthetic spectral recovery tasks. Standard GP regression benchmarks (UCI datasets: Boston, Concrete, Energy; or at minimum one task from SCOD/GPflow benchmarks) would demonstrate that the decoded kernels generalize to real data with non-stationary or multi-scale structure. The method claims practical relevance ("supports downstream tasks") but all quantitative results are on synthetic data.

**Expected:**
- **Decoder training compute cost.** The inference speedup is documented (1000×), but the cost of training the decoders from PFN representations is unspecified. If training requires significant GPU-hours, the "single forward pass" inference framing is incomplete — one must amortize the decoder training cost over the number of inference problems it serves.
- **Sensitivity to PFN architecture.** The mechanistic analysis is conducted on a specific PFN with decoupled attention (DVA). What happens with other PFN variants (e.g., TabPFN or standard self-attention PFNs)? The framework appears architecturally specific; this constraint should be characterized empirically.

### Hidden Assumptions

- **Stationarity.** The decoder produces stationary kernels via Bochner's theorem. This is stated but the scope of the assumption (all recovered kernels must be stationary) is not discussed relative to real-world non-stationary processes. Appropriate given the paper's scope on spectral methods, but should be foregrounded for practitioners.
- **Decoupled attention PFN required.** The mechanistic finding (attention latent ↔ frequency, ρ = 0.86) is specific to the DVA PFN architecture. This is stated but not foregrounded as a design constraint. The framework does not generalize to standard self-attention PFNs without architectural modification. Stated implicitly; should be explicit.
- **PFN was trained on stationary kernels.** The decoder extracts spectral structure that the PFN has learned. If the PFN's training distribution included non-stationary kernels, the spectral extraction would be corrupted. This assumption is not stated.
- **Additive kernel structure in high dimensions.** The 5D/10D generalization experiments use additive kernels (sum over dimensions). This is a strong structural assumption that enables tractable Bochner decomposition but is not representative of general high-dimensional GPs. The assumption is described experimentally but not called out as a structural requirement.

All of the above are reasonable; the additive structure assumption is the one most in need of explicit statement for practitioners.

### Deployment Pair

**Industry Practitioner pitch:** This paper gives you a way to take a pre-trained PFN and extract interpretable kernel structure from it — for free, without any per-dataset optimization. If you are running a Bayesian optimization pipeline or surrogate modeling pipeline that currently uses DKL (requiring expensive marginal likelihood optimization per dataset), you can swap in the decoded kernel and get 1000× faster predictions with accuracy comparable to your current baseline. The system is modular: swap in the frozen PFN, attach a small decoder, and you have a zero-shot kernel factory.

**Industry Practitioner deployment concerns the paper does not answer:**
- We need the decoder training cost before we can evaluate total amortization efficiency. If training the decoder requires a week of GPU time, the value proposition depends on how many inference problems you plan to run.
- The framework only works with decoupled-attention PFN (DVA). If you are already using a different PFN variant (e.g., TabPFN with standard attention), you would need to either retrain with DVA or find the latent-spectral correlation in your architecture. The paper does not tell you how to do this.
- The framework produces stationary kernels only. If your real-world data has trends, seasonality with non-stationary amplitude, or change-points, the decoded kernel will not capture them. There is no diagnostic to tell you when stationarity is violated.
- The surrogate-based optimization use case is described but not demonstrated. A practitioner needs Bayesian optimization results (regret curves, number of function evaluations to convergence) to trust this for BO.
- On real tabular data (e.g., UCI), how does the decoded kernel GP compare to the PFN directly? If the decoded kernel is systematically 10% worse on NLPD, the practitioner needs to know before deploying it as a drop-in replacement.

**Hacker paragraph:** The paper describes the method clearly enough to reimplement given the DVA PFN codebase. What is missing for a from-scratch implementation:
- The specific PFN checkpoint/weights used in experiments (not linked; "pre-trained PFN" is described but not released as of submission)
- The training dataset distribution for the decoders (how many synthetic GP samples, which hyperparameter ranges, what kernel families were sampled during decoder training)
- Multi-Query Attention decoder architecture details beyond "we pool over the context dimension" — exact number of heads, hidden dimensions, pooling strategy
- Training time and hardware for decoder training

### Limitations Section Audit

**Specificity:** Good. The limitations are specific to this paper's method: performance degradation with larger context, PFN's lack of per-dimension encoding, periodic kernels in higher dimensions. Not generic.

**Severity honesty:** Adequate. The periodic kernel failure in high dimensions (one order of magnitude worse than oracle) is acknowledged with appropriate emphasis. The limitation on decoder training cost is absent from the limitations section entirely — this is the main omission.

**Constructiveness:** Moderate. The limitations state what fails (larger contexts, higher dimensions) but offer limited guidance on how to address them ("future work" for the PFN architecture issue).

**Completeness:** The authors acknowledge the most technically important limitations (identifiability, high-D periodic kernels, context size). The missing piece is: (a) no limitation on the stationarity assumption, (b) no mention of decoder training cost, (c) no mention of the DVA architecture dependency as a deployment constraint.

**Credit for honest self-disclosure:** The authors should be credited for clearly stating the single-realization identifiability barrier (Theorem 4.1) rather than burying it. This is genuine scientific candor. The scale-factor issue (Proposition 4.2) and the strategy to estimate it are both disclosed and addressed within the paper.

### Negative Results and Failure Modes

The paper honestly reports that Multi-Query Attention was essential — standard attention pooling failed to disambiguate overlapping spectral components. This is a genuine negative result that is reported and motivates the architectural choice. The performance degradation with larger context datasets is also reported. These negative results are described in the main text, not buried in the appendix.

What is conspicuously absent: failure cases on real data. The paper reports no setting where the decoded kernel GP significantly underperformed the PFN or optimization-based baselines on a real-world regression task. Since all experiments are on synthetic data, it is unclear whether such failure cases exist — they may not have been tested.

### Scope Verdict

The evidence directly supports the mechanistic and theoretical claims. The "comparable to PFNs" accuracy claim and the "supports surrogate-based optimization" application claim are the two places where the evidence does not fully match the framing: the former lacks a direct head-to-head comparison with the PFN itself, and the latter is a stated motivation without experimental validation.

### Justification

The paper's core mechanistic finding (attention latents encode spectral frequency) is rigorously supported, and the theoretical framework (identifiability theorems) is complete and well-calibrated. The experimental setup is internally consistent and the reported speedup is accurate relative to the stated comparison class (optimization-based methods).

Two gaps warrant mention:

1. The "comparable to PFNs" claim deserves a direct PFN vs. decoded-kernel comparison. If this comparison exists in the paper and the subagent's summary missed it, then the claim is fully supported. If it doesn't exist, the claim relies on the inference that "we match DKL, PFN also matches DKL, therefore we match PFN," which has a transitivity step that needs to be shown directly.

2. All quantitative experiments are on synthetic data. The paper's stated motivation (surrogate-based optimization, interpretability for practitioners) implies a connection to real data that is not validated quantitatively. This is appropriate scoping for a mechanistic analysis paper, but the abstract's framing of "downstream tasks" suggests broader applicability than the experiments demonstrate.

Applying the principle of charity: (a) synthetic experiments are standard for mechanistic analysis in kernel learning papers — real-data benchmarks are "expected" but not "essential" when the core claims are about spectral recovery from a known distribution; (b) the paper's tight scope on interpretability of PFN priors is a virtue, not a deficiency; (c) the theoretical results are complete and honest. These considerations push the verdict toward "mostly complete" rather than "significant gaps." The missing decoder training cost disclosure is a genuine omission that a practitioner would notice immediately.
