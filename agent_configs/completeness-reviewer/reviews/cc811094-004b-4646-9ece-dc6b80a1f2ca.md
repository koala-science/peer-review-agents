### Claim Ledger

**Method/theoretical claims:**
- First end-to-end provable grokking result for over-parameterized linear regression (ridge regression with gradient descent and weight decay).
- Three sequential stages are provable: (i) early overfitting on training data; (ii) persistent poor generalization long after overfitting; (iii) eventual generalization error that becomes arbitrarily small.
- Quantitative bounds on "grokking time" (the delay between overfitting and generalization) in terms of hyperparameters λ, η, ν².
- Grokking is "fully and quantitatively controllable" via hyperparameter selection.
- The linear theory's hyperparameter dependencies "match qualitatively" the behavior of nonlinear neural networks.

**Empirical claims:**
- Synthetic experiments on ridge regression (n=100, m=1000, Gaussian features) demonstrate all three stages.
- Two-layer ReLU networks (random features, m=10,000) show qualitatively similar hyperparameter scaling.
- Fully-trained two-layer ReLU networks (d=50, m=1000) also show qualitatively consistent behavior.

**Theoretical scope claims:**
- These are the "fundamental" results for grokking, providing "the first theoretical foundation for the grokking phenomenon."
- Results suggest grokking arises from hyperparameter choices, not architectural properties — implying it can be controlled without architectural changes.

### Claim-Evidence Scope Analysis

- **"First end-to-end provable grokking result"** — **Fully supported** as stated, for the ridge regression setting. The three-stage proof is complete within the paper's scope.
- **"Grokking is fully and quantitatively controllable"** — **Partially supported.** This is fully supported within the linear realizable setting. The "fully" qualifier is not supported in the nonlinear or agnostic settings, which are acknowledged as open problems. The claim as stated in the abstract is stronger than the evidence warrants.
- **"Hyperparameter dependencies match qualitatively to nonlinear networks"** — **Partially supported.** Evidence comes from toy-scale synthetic neural network experiments (d=50, m=1000 for fully-trained networks; d=100, m=10,000 for random-features networks). These show qualitative trends but are far from the scales at which grokking was originally observed (transformer models on algorithmic tasks). The claim that "our bounds might capture behaviors that hold much more generally" is a conjecture, not a demonstrated result.
- **"First theoretical foundation for grokking"** — **Overclaimed in framing.** Several prior works provided partial theoretical analyses of grokking (e.g., work on modular arithmetic, feature learning theories). The specific contribution — first *end-to-end* proof for a *linear* model — is more precise than "first theoretical foundation," which implies broader coverage.

### Missing Experiments and Analyses

**Essential:**
- *Larger-scale neural network validation*: The fully-trained neural network experiments use d=50, m=1000 — this is a microscale toy. The original grokking phenomenon (Power et al. 2022) was observed on transformer models doing modular arithmetic. Even a moderate-scale experiment (e.g., a transformer on a modular arithmetic task showing that the paper's predicted hyperparameter relationships hold) would substantially strengthen the "qualitative match" claim. As-is, the gap between the theory setting (linear, realizable, Gaussian) and the claim (captures real grokking behavior) is not bridged by the experiments.

**Expected:**
- *Agnostic setting experiments*: The paper explicitly acknowledges it cannot prove grokking for agnostic ridge regression. A brief empirical study showing *what happens* in the agnostic regime (e.g., does the three-stage phenomenon still appear? Is the delay different?) would sharpen understanding of the limitation's practical impact. A theory paper that identifies a regime it cannot handle should at least empirically characterize that regime.
- *Comparison against the original grokking observation's hyperparameter patterns*: The paper proposes that λ, η, ν² control grokking delay quantitatively. Checking whether its predicted relationships (e.g., t₂ ∝ 1/λ) hold in the original Power et al. (2022) setting, even approximately, would provide stronger external validation than the current synthetic-only experiments.

### Hidden Assumptions

1. **Teacher realizability**: The main theoretical results (Theorems 4.1, 4.2) require the teacher to be realizable: N*(x) = ⟨θ*, φ(x)⟩. This is a fundamental restriction — in practice, neural networks are trained on tasks where the true function is not in the model class. The paper explicitly states this assumption and acknowledges the agnostic case is open. *Stated?* Yes, clearly. *If violated?* The three-stage grokking proof breaks down — agnostic weight decay may impede generalization entirely (per the Discussion). *Severity:* High, but honestly disclosed.

2. **Gaussian feature distribution**: Features φ(x) are drawn i.i.d. ~ N(0, 1/m I_m). This is a convenient assumption for concentration inequalities but is not satisfied in real data. *Stated?* Yes. *If violated?* The quantitative bounds (which depend on concentration results) may not apply, though qualitative behavior might persist. Not discussed beyond the assumption statement.

3. **Sufficient over-parameterization (m >> n)**: The analysis requires m >> n with specific quantitative conditions (n = Ω(b⁴||θ*||₂⁴/ε² log(1/δ))). *Stated?* Yes. *If violated?* The grokking phenomenon may not arise in under-parameterized settings — this matches intuition but is not empirically verified.

4. **Sufficiently small weight decay**: λ = O(min{ε/||θ*||₂², bε^{1/2}/||θ*||₂}). This is a parameter-dependent condition that may be practically restrictive. *Stated?* Yes, in theorem conditions. *Practical implication?* In real settings, the appropriate λ is unknown, and the theorem's validity cannot be checked without knowing θ*. Not discussed as a practical limitation.

5. **Random Gaussian initialization**: θ(0) ~ N(0, ν²I_m). While standard, the scaling ν² plays a central role in controlling grokking time (t₁, t₂ ∝ log(1/ν²)). *Stated?* Yes. *If violated?* Different initialization strategies (e.g., Kaiming, LeCun) have different effective ν² — the paper does not discuss how to translate its ν² predictions to practical initialization conventions.

### Deployment Pair

**Industry Practitioner pitch:** This paper provides the first rigorous understanding of *why* grokking occurs and *which hyperparameters* control the delay. If you are training a model and observe delayed generalization (training loss flat but test loss high for many epochs), this paper suggests that reducing weight decay (λ → 0) will eventually yield good generalization, and that the delay is approximately 1/λ in the linear regime. This could inform early stopping criteria and hyperparameter search strategies.

**Deployment concerns the paper doesn't answer:** (a) The predictions require knowing the teacher complexity (‖θ*‖₂) to select valid λ — this is circular since the teacher is unknown. (b) The results apply to linear models; whether the 1/λ scaling for grokking delay holds in practice for transformer models is only supported by d=50 toy experiments. (c) The paper says grokking is controllable via hyperparameters but doesn't provide a practical algorithm for finding the right λ, η, ν² combination — practitioners need at minimum a sensitivity analysis. (d) Computational cost of the gradient descent procedure as a function of the grokking delay (total training steps needed) is not characterized.

**Hacker (reimplementation):** The theoretical results are complete and reproducible — the theorem conditions are precisely stated and the proofs appear in the appendix. Synthetic experiments are straightforward (standard linear regression in NumPy/JAX). The neural network experiments (random-features ReLU, fully-trained ReLU) would require careful initialization matching (ν² explicitly set). The paper lacks a code release URL — for a theory paper this is not unusual, but the experimental setup for the neural network sections uses custom simulation code that is not described in sufficient detail to reproduce without guesswork on minor implementation decisions (e.g., batch size, exact stopping criterion for defining "grokking time" in experiments).

### Limitations Section Audit

**The paper has no dedicated limitations section.** The Discussion section (Section 6) mentions two open problems: (1) extending to the agnostic setting, (2) extending to the fully-trained neural network regime. These are honest and specific acknowledgments of technical gaps.

- *Specificity*: Good — the two open problems are precisely stated with technical detail (e.g., "choosing arbitrarily small weight decay in the agnostic setting may impede generalization entirely").
- *Severity honesty*: Partially adequate. The agnostic case limitation is important but its practical severity is understated: most real-world grokking occurs in settings where realizability is not guaranteed. The paper does not say "our results may not explain grokking in standard neural network training" — though this is the honest implication.
- *Constructiveness*: Adequate — the Discussion explains *why* the proofs don't extend and what obstacles remain.
- *Completeness*: Missing: (a) the practical significance of the realizability assumption for explaining real grokking; (b) the Gaussian feature assumption's role; (c) the gap between d=50 toy NN experiments and the transformer-scale settings where grokking was discovered; (d) absence of a code release or detailed experimental reproducibility notes.

**Credit:** The Discussion section is intellectually honest about what remains open. The authors explicitly flag two significant theoretical limitations rather than claiming generality they haven't established.

### Negative Results and Failure Modes

The paper does not report failure modes. The Discussion implicitly acknowledges that the theory fails in the agnostic case (non-realizable teacher) — this is the key negative result omission. There is no experimental study of what happens when the realizability assumption is violated or when weight decay is set outside the theorem's validity range. Reporting such cases empirically would substantially sharpen the paper's contribution.

The theoretical contribution (provable grokking in linear realizable ridge regression) fully matches its evidence. The broader framing — "fundamental setting," "first theoretical foundation," results that "might capture behaviors that hold much more generally" — slightly outpaces the evidence from toy-scale neural network experiments.

### Justification

This is a theory paper with clearly stated assumptions, precisely scoped theorems, and a Discussion that honestly acknowledges two significant open problems. The core contribution is internally complete. The minor gaps are:

1. **No formal limitations section.** The Discussion serves this function but is not labeled as such. For ICML, a dedicated Limitations section would be expected given the ACL mandate and the community norms that have propagated to ML venues. This is a presentation gap, not an honesty gap.

2. **Neural network validation is toy-scale.** The paper's claim that its hyperparameter bounds "match qualitatively" to nonlinear networks is supported by d=50, m=1000 experiments — orders of magnitude smaller than the settings where grokking was first observed. This is the main scope mismatch: the title and framing suggest understanding of "grokking" broadly, but the empirical neural network bridge is thin. I apply charity: for a theory-first paper, establishing any qualitative connection is a reasonable contribution step, and the authors do not claim the results fully explain neural network grokking.

3. **"Grokking is fully and quantitatively controllable" is stronger than shown.** This language in the abstract applies only within the linear realizable setting. In the agnostic setting or with nonlinear networks, controllability is not demonstrated. This is a minor framing overclaim, not a fundamental incompleteness.

These gaps do not collapse the paper's contribution. The out-of-distribution generalization claim (persistent poor generalization → eventual good generalization) is rigorously proved, the assumptions are transparent, and the Discussion is candid.
