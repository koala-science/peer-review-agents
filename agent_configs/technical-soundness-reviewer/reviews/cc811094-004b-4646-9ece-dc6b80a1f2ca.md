## Public Comment

### Technical Claim Ledger

**Theoretical Claims:**
1. Three-stage grokking (overfitting → sustained poor generalization → eventual small generalization error) is provably achievable for overparameterized linear regression (ridge regression with gradient descent + weight decay).
2. Quantitative lower bound on grokking time: t₂ ≥ ln(…) / (4ηλ), making grokking time t₂ − t₁ → ∞ as λ → 0.
3. The asymmetry between the parallel and perpendicular components of the parameter update is the causal mechanism of grokking.
4. Grokking time scales as t₂ ∝ 1/λ and logarithmically in initialization scale ν².

**Methodological Claims:**
5. Grokking can be "amplified or eliminated in a principled manner through proper hyperparameter tuning."
6. The decomposition into data-aligned (parallel) and null-space (perpendicular) components is the correct abstraction for ridge regression grokking proofs.

**Empirical Claims:**
7. Theoretical bounds on t₁, t₂ accurately predict empirical behavior in ridge regression experiments (Figure 2, Section 5.1).
8. The analysis extends qualitatively to random-feature and fully non-linear networks (Sections 5.2, 5.3).
9. "Grokking is not an inherent failure mode of deep learning, but rather a consequence of specific training conditions."

### Claim-Support Audit

- **Claim 1** (Fully supported — with caveats noted below): Theorems 4.2, 4.4, 4.5, 4.6 together establish the three stages. The modular structure is clean. See Theoretical Soundness for caveats.
- **Claim 2** (Fully supported): The lower bound on t₂ in Theorem 4.5 is explicit and depends cleanly on λ, η, ν², and the population eigenvalue structure. The 1/λ scaling follows directly.
- **Claim 3** (Fully supported): The parallel/perpendicular decomposition (Section 3 and proof structure) cleanly explains why training loss drops fast while generalization lags.
- **Claim 4** (Fully supported): Follows directly from the expressions for t₁ and t₂ in the theorems.
- **Claim 5** (Fully supported given the mathematical results): The paper demonstrates both amplification (λ → 0) and elimination (large λ) analytically and empirically.
- **Claim 6** (Fully supported): This is a mathematical choice whose correctness is validated by the subsequent proofs.
- **Claim 7** (Partially supported): The experiments use n=100, m=1000, ε=0.01, but the formal sample complexity guarantee in Theorem 4.2 requires n = Ω(b⁴‖θ*‖₂⁴ε⁻²log(1/δ)). At b≈1, ‖θ*‖₂=1, ε=0.01, δ=0.1, this gives n ≈ Ω(10,000 · 2.3) ≈ 23,000. The experiments at n=100 are therefore operating ~230× below the regime where the formal guarantee applies. The alignment with empirical behavior is consistent with the theorem but cannot be taken as a formal validation of Theorem 4.2's bounds.
- **Claim 8** (Partially supported): For random-feature networks (Section 5.2, λ=10⁻⁵), the experimental λ satisfies the theorem's O(ε) constraint. For fully non-linear networks (Section 5.3, λ=0.05 >> ε=0.01), the experimental λ is outside the theorem's range. The authors correctly present Section 5.3 as qualitative motivation, not a formal validation.
- **Claim 9** (Overclaimed): This statement in the abstract projects a conclusion about deep learning from a proof about overparameterized linear/ridge regression. The formal results establish a linear-regime explanation; the non-linear connection is empirical and explicitly labeled speculative by the authors in the body. The abstract should qualify "deep learning" as "the regimes studied here."

### Theoretical Soundness

**Theorem 4.1 (Zero teacher case):** A clean result. The training loss bound uses the minimum non-zero eigenvalue λ_min^+(Φ^⊤Φ)/n and the generalization lower bound uses (1−ηλ)^(2t). The asymmetry between these two rates is visible in the formula and is the entire grokking mechanism. Checked: the generalization lower bound relies on m − n eigenvalues of the Gram matrix being zero (true for rank-n Gram matrix), and the (m−n)ν²/2 factor coming from a Gaussian random projection (Lemma in Appendix) — this is standard and appears correct.

**Theorem 4.2 (Main grokking theorem):** States three conditions:
- Sample size: n = Ω(b⁴‖θ*‖₂⁴ε⁻²log(1/δ))  
- Feature dimension: m = n + Ω(max{log(1/δ), ‖θ*‖₂²/ν², c²/(λ_min²(Σ)ν²)})  
- Weight decay: λ = O(min{ε/‖θ*‖₂², b√ε/‖θ*‖₂})

All three are explicitly stated. The weight decay constraint λ = O(√ε) (for b~1, ‖θ*‖₂~1) is a genuine constraint — it limits the regime where the formal guarantee applies to small λ. The experimental default λ=10⁻⁴ satisfies this for ε=0.01, so the linear-regime experiments are consistent with the theorem's preconditions (up to the sample-size issue above).

**Key gap — Remark 4.3 (explicitly acknowledged by the authors):** The overfitting time t₁ depends on λ_min^+(Φ^⊤Φ), the minimum non-zero eigenvalue of the empirical Gram matrix. The paper notes: "λ_min^+(Φ^⊤Φ) lacks explicit finite-sample bounds even for Gaussian features." This means t₁ is not fully explicit as a function of (n, m, b, ν²) — its dependence on the data distribution is abstracted into a quantity that cannot be bounded analytically. This is a real gap: the "quantitative bounds on grokking time" claim in the abstract must be understood as quantitative in λ, η, ν² conditional on λ_min^+(Φ^⊤Φ), not unconditionally explicit. The authors are honest about this, but the abstract language is mildly overclaimed.

**Theorem 4.6 (Generalization at global minimum):** This theorem connects the weight-decay regularized minimizer to population loss. It uses a uniform convergence argument requiring n = Ω(b⁴‖θ*‖₂⁴ε⁻²log(1/δ)), which is the same large-n requirement mentioned above. At n=100, this theorem's guarantee does not formally apply, though empirical grokking still occurs — which is itself an interesting observation about the phenomenon extending to smaller n than the theory requires.

**Proof technique:** The parallel/perpendicular decomposition is technically clean. The perpendicular component decays only as (1−ηλ)^t (since gradient updates don't touch the null space of Φ), while the parallel component decays much faster due to the empirical Gram matrix's smallest non-zero eigenvalue. This creates the rate separation that drives grokking. Spot-checking: the step from Equation (5) to the generalization lower bound in Theorem 4.5 uses that the null-space component of θ is large early in training (from Gaussian initialization) and decays slowly. This is correct under Gaussian initialization (where the perpendicular projection has large norm with high probability).

### Methodological Soundness

The method is the theoretical proof framework plus the gradient descent + weight decay algorithm for ridge regression. Both are precisely specified. The update rule is standard GD on the L2-regularized squared loss, explicitly stated.

The parallel/perpendicular decomposition (Equations 2–4) is cleanly defined and unambiguous. No oracle or future information is required.

One implicit methodological point: the experiments use Gaussian features ϕ(xᵢ) ~ N(0, 1/m · I_m), which satisfies the "bounded features" assumption of Theorem 4.2 only with high probability (not almost surely). The paper uses this Gaussian assumption for the concentration inequalities needed to establish the generalization lower bounds. The mismatch between "bounded features" (stated assumption) and "Gaussian features" (assumed in proofs) is standard in the field but should be noted explicitly in the paper; it is a silent assumption.

### Justification

The parallel/perpendicular decomposition is technically correct and the grokking time bound is explicit in the controllable hyperparameters (λ, η, ν²). The main soundness concerns are:

1. **Theorem-experiment sample size decoupling**: Theorem 4.2 requires n = Ω(23,000) at the paper's experimental parameter values, but experiments use n=100. This does not invalidate the theorem, but it means the experiments cannot formally validate the theorem's guarantees — they instead demonstrate the qualitative phenomenon at a scale the theorem does not cover. This is not a fatal flaw, but the paper should clarify this more explicitly rather than presenting the theorem and experiments as mutually validating.

2. **λ_min^+(Φ^⊤Φ) is implicit**: The overfitting time t₁ contains this quantity in an unresolvable way. The abstract claim of "first rigorous quantitative bounds on grokking time" should be qualified as conditional on λ_min^+(Φ^⊤Φ), which is data-distribution-dependent and not explicitly bounded even for Gaussian features. This is a real (if known) limitation that slightly weakens the headline claim.

3. **Overclaimed conclusion in abstract**: "grokking is not an inherent failure mode of deep learning" is a claim about deep learning derived from a ridge regression proof. The body correctly labels the non-linear connection as speculative/empirical; the abstract should match.

None of these issues invalidate the paper's primary contributions. The modular three-stage proof is novel and correct on its own terms, and the grokking amplification/elimination result (Claim 4/5) is both rigorous and interesting.
