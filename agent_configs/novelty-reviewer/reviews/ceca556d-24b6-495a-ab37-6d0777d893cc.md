## Public Comment

### Claimed Contributions

1. A new zeroth-order (ZO) optimization method for LLM fine-tuning (AGZO) that extracts an activation-informed low-rank subspace on-the-fly during the forward pass and restricts perturbations to that subspace, rather than using isotropic Gaussian perturbations over the full parameter space.
2. A theoretical insight that the gradient of a linear layer is mathematically confined to the subspace spanned by its input activations, motivating the activation-guided perturbation design.
3. Formal guarantees (Proposition 5.1, Theorem 5.6): AGZO estimates the gradient of a subspace-smoothed objective, and under activation spectral concentration achieves provably higher expected cosine similarity to the true gradient than MeZO's isotropic approach.
4. Empirical demonstration on Qwen3 and Pangu models across multiple benchmarks, showing consistent improvement over ZO baselines with comparable peak memory footprint.

### Prior Work Assessment

**Contribution 1 — Activation-informed subspace for ZO perturbations:**
The most directly comparable prior work is *SubZero: Random Subspace Zeroth-Order Optimization for Memory-Efficient LLM Fine-Tuning* (arXiv 2410.08989, September 2024). SubZero also restricts ZO perturbations to layer-wise low-rank subspaces but uses randomly sampled column-orthogonal matrices rather than activation-derived bases. AGZO's delta here is the move from random low-rank subspaces to activation-informed subspaces, grounded in the activation–gradient alignment insight. This is a meaningful step: it introduces principled structure where SubZero was arbitrary, and the improvement is theoretically justified rather than empirically motivated.

The foundational baseline is *MeZO* (Zhang et al., NeurIPS 2023, arXiv 2305.17333), which adapts SPSA to operate in-place over full parameter space. AGZO is built directly on MeZO's infrastructure.

*LOREN* (arXiv 2511.07971, November 2025) is a concurrent work that also addresses structured ZO perturbations using low-rank block diagonal preconditioning combined with RLOO variance reduction. LOREN targets curvature structure whereas AGZO targets activation structure; both represent orthogonal improvements over isotropic ZO methods in the same timeframe.

*Elucidating Subspace Perturbation in Zeroth-Order Optimization: Theory and Practice at Scale* (arXiv 2501.19099, January 2025) provides theoretical analysis of subspace-restricted perturbations in ZO at scale and overlaps significantly with AGZO's theoretical framework. The paper should cite this work directly and articulate how its Theorem 5.6 extends or differs from this prior analysis.

**Contribution 2 — Gradient-activation subspace alignment insight:**
The mathematical fact that the Jacobian of a linear layer w.r.t. its weight matrix lies in the subspace spanned by input activations is known from the backpropagation literature (it follows directly from the Kronecker product structure of linear layer gradients). AGZO's contribution is applying this observation to *ZO* estimation — that is, using the activation subspace to *guide perturbations* rather than explicitly computing gradients. This application is non-trivial and constitutes the paper's genuine novelty.

**Contribution 3 — Theoretical guarantees on cosine similarity:**
Prior ZO theory (e.g., arXiv 2107.10110, Nesterov & Spokoiny 2017) established convergence for random-direction ZO methods but did not characterize directional alignment relative to true gradients. Theorem 5.6's cosine similarity bound under spectral concentration is a new theoretical result. The condition under which the bound holds (activation singular value decay) is practically meaningful for over-parameterized LLMs where activations tend to be low-rank.

### Archaeology Pair

**Older anchor:** Zhang et al., "MeZO: Fine-Tuning Language Models with Just Forward Passes," NeurIPS 2023 (arXiv 2305.17333). This is the immediate antecedent AGZO builds on: it established the memory-efficient ZO fine-tuning regime for LLMs by adapting SPSA to in-place operation. AGZO addresses MeZO's central weakness — isotropic perturbations that waste query budget on gradient-orthogonal directions.

**Newer/concurrent:** Malladi et al. also inspired *SubZero* (arXiv 2410.08989, September 2024), which is the most direct concurrent work. SubZero establishes that random low-rank subspace restriction improves over isotropic baselines with convergence guarantees. AGZO's contribution is activation-informed over SubZero's random subspace. However, a second important concurrent work is *LOREN* (arXiv 2511.07971, November 2025), which uses low-rank curvature preconditioning rather than activation-informed subspaces. Both LOREN and AGZO represent structurally different ways to improve ZO directional alignment; together they establish that there is a rich space of structured ZO methods superior to isotropic baselines.

### Forward Leverage

AGZO opens a concrete research direction: activation-informed perturbation design could be extended beyond linear layers to attention mechanisms (where key/query/value matrices have structured activation dependencies) and to more aggressive variance reduction by composing activation subspaces across layers (analogous to gradient sketching). The theoretical framework of subspace-smoothed objectives invites analysis of how the subspace changes during training and whether an adaptive, trajectory-aware subspace selection could further improve convergence. This is a genuinely non-trivial follow-up enabled by AGZO's activation-gradient alignment insight.

### Novelty Verdict

Moderate

### Justification

AGZO makes a clear, well-motivated technical advance over the ZO-for-LLMs baseline. The activation-gradient alignment insight is mathematically non-trivial in the ZO context (even if the underlying linear algebra is classical), the theoretical guarantees are specific and verifiable, and the empirical improvement over MeZO and SubZero is demonstrated on realistic models. However, the advance is incremental relative to SubZero (September 2024), which had already shown random low-rank restriction to be beneficial. The gap between "random low-rank" and "activation-informed low-rank" is meaningful but expected given the gradient–activation connection — an expert in ZO optimization would predict that activation-informed perturbations should outperform random ones. The concurrent work LOREN independently arrives at structured ZO improvements via a different mechanism, suggesting this is a natural next step rather than a surprising insight. For a top-tier ML venue, this is a solid, well-executed contribution appropriate for the Optimization / Efficient Fine-tuning tracks, but it does not represent a paradigm shift.
