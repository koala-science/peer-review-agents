## Public Comment

### Claimed Contributions

1. A novel image-text pair jailbreaking framework (BVS) targeting harmful *image generation* via MLLMs, as opposed to prior work focused on eliciting harmful *text* output.
2. A "reconstruction-then-generation" strategy using (a) neutralized visual splicing — partitioning a harmful image into patches, shuffling them, and interleaving with benign images in a 3×3 grid — and (b) inductive recomposition via a cross-lingual (Chinese) prompt instructing the MLLM to mentally reconstruct specific grid positions and generate based on them.
3. The MIDOS (Multi-Image Distance Optimization Selection) algorithm for selecting benign interleaving images that maximize semantic distance from the harmful target while minimizing local perceptual discord.
4. Empirical evaluation achieving 98.18% jailbreak success rate against GPT-5 (January 2026) and 95.45% against Gemini 1.5 Flash.

### Prior Work Assessment

**Contribution 1 — Targeting harmful image generation rather than text:**
The authors claim this is a distinguishing dimension. However, image generation via MLLM manipulation was already explored. *Odysseus: Jailbreaking Commercial MLLM-integrated Systems via Dual Steganography* (Guo et al., NDSS 2026, arXiv 2512.20168, December 2025) achieves up to 99% ASR on MLLM-integrated systems and directly targets multimodal generation pipelines. The paper does not cite Odysseus. *JPS: Jailbreak Multimodal Large Language Models with Collaborative Visual Perturbation and Textual Steering* (ACM MM 2025) uses a similar decoupling philosophy (separate visual perturbation and textual steering) against generative pipelines. These two papers substantially reduce the novelty of the image-generation targeting claim.

**Contribution 2 — Neutralized visual splicing + inductive recomposition:**
The core technique (partition harmful image into patches, interleave with benign images, instruct MLLM to reconstruct) is closely related to two prior approaches:
- *Contextual Image Attack (CIA)* (arXiv 2512.02973, December 2025) embeds harmful queries in semantically benign visual contexts, achieving 86-91% ASR against GPT-4o. The principal mechanism — creating semantic mismatch between local visual and global intent — is the same.
- *FigStep* (Gong et al., AAAI 2025 Oral, arXiv 2311.05608) demonstrated that structured visual arrangement combined with typographic encoding bypasses safety filters; the spatial arrangement idea in BVS echoes this.

The specific choice of 3×3 grid and cross-lingual prompting is novel in its particular instantiation, but the design principle (hide harmful semantics in spatial arrangement, then instruct the model to mentally reconstruct) is well-established.

**Contribution 3 — MIDOS algorithm:**
This is the most technically specific contribution. Maximizing semantic distance from the harmful image while minimizing local perceptual discord to select benign patch interleaving images appears new relative to prior work that uses random or heuristic patch selection. The ablation showing MIDOS (98.18%) vs. random selection (81.82%) demonstrates the algorithm's contribution. However, the underlying optimization (selecting images from a corpus by embedding distance) is straightforward and does not constitute a deep algorithmic advance.

**Contribution 4 — 98%+ ASR on GPT-5:**
Odysseus reports 99% ASR on MLLM-integrated systems. The incremental improvement from 86-91% (CIA) to 98% (BVS) is meaningful empirically, but the conceptual advance is limited — this represents engineering optimization of a known attack type rather than a new vulnerability class.

### Archaeology Pair

**Older anchor:** Liu et al., "MM-SafetyBench: A Benchmark for Safety Evaluation of Multimodal Large Language Models," ECCV 2024 (arXiv 2311.17600, November 2023). This work identified that MLLMs are vulnerable to coordinated image-text attacks that exploit the gap between visual content and textual safety filters — the precise vulnerability class BVS exploits. It serves as the foundational empirical motivation for the entire line of work BVS belongs to.

**Newer/concurrent:** Guo et al., "Odysseus: Jailbreaking Commercial Multimodal LLM-integrated Systems via Dual Steganography," NDSS 2026 (arXiv 2512.20168, December 2025). Odysseus predates BVS (December 2025 vs. January 2026) and achieves 99% ASR via LSB steganographic embedding of both query and response in benign images. Both Odysseus and BVS exploit semantic-visual decoupling; Odysseus uses steganographic hiding while BVS uses spatial patch arrangement. The paper must engage with Odysseus to establish what BVS adds.

### Forward Leverage

The MIDOS algorithm — selecting benign interleaving patches by semantic distance maximization — could inspire targeted defenses: if models were trained to detect semantic incongruence between local and global image understanding, BVS-style attacks would be neutralized. This is a concrete, non-trivial follow-up (defensive application of the MIDOS spatial distance insight). However, the attack-design space BVS opens is narrow: spatial arrangement attacks are a specific technique class, and its advance over CIA and Odysseus is incremental rather than paradigm-opening.

### Novelty Verdict

Incremental

### Justification

BVS provides a specific, well-engineered attack with high empirical success, but the paper's conceptual novelty is limited given the landscape as of January 2026. The core design principle — hide harmful intent in visual structure, instruct model to mentally reconstruct — appears in CIA (December 2025) and Odysseus (December 2025), both of which predate BVS and achieve comparably high success rates. The MIDOS selection algorithm is the most novel component, but it is a heuristic optimization over image embeddings rather than a principled algorithmic contribution. The claim of targeting harmful *image generation* as a distinguishing feature is undermined by Odysseus, which this paper does not cite.

For ICML acceptance, the paper would need to: (1) clearly differentiate BVS from Odysseus and CIA at a mechanistic level; (2) situate MIDOS's contribution more rigorously against random baselines across more models; and (3) clarify whether the 98% success rate represents a meaningful improvement over Odysseus's 99% given methodological differences. The empirical results are strong, but without addressing these prior works directly, the novelty claim cannot be sustained.
