## Public Comment

### Claimed Contributions

1. **R-GTD algorithm**: A regularized variant of GTD2 that introduces a regularized convex–concave saddle-point formulation. The regularization ensures the modified problem has a unique saddle point without requiring the feature interaction matrix (FIM) to be nonsingular.

2. **Convergence guarantees without nonsingularity assumption**: R-GTD is proved to converge to its unique saddle point even when the FIM is singular, using primal–dual gradient dynamics and the ODE method (Borkar-Meyn framework).

3. **Explicit error bounds**: The paper establishes bounds between the R-GTD solution and the true projected solution (the solution of the original MSPBE) in both singular and nonsingular settings, characterizing how regularization bias scales with the regularization parameter.

4. **Empirical validation**: Experiments on Baird's counterexample and other environments demonstrate that R-GTD is stable where GTD2 diverges.

### Prior Work Assessment

**GTD2 (Sutton et al., ICML 2009)**: The direct ancestor. GTD2 minimizes MSPBE via a saddle-point formulation, but requires the FIM to be nonsingular for its convergence proofs to hold. R-GTD replaces the unregularized saddle-point problem with a regularized version that is always uniquely solvable. Delta: R-GTD specifically lifts the nonsingularity assumption, whereas GTD2 leaves singular cases unaddressed theoretically.

**TDRC — Gradient Temporal-Difference Learning with Regularized Corrections (Ghiassian et al., ICML 2020)**: TDRC introduces regularization in GTD-style algorithms, but its motivation is empirical: it adds a regularization term to the secondary weight update to improve practical robustness and balance between TD and TDC behavior. TDRC does not analyze or specifically target the singular FIM setting, and does not provide error bounds relating the regularized solution to the true projected solution. Delta: R-GTD's regularization is motivated by and analyzed through the lens of FIM singularity, with formal guarantees for that regime.

**New Versions of Gradient TD Learning (Lee et al., IEEE TAC 2022)**: Proposes new saddle-point formulations and GTD2 variants exploiting structural insights. These still assume nonsingular FIM for their convergence analyses. Delta: R-GTD specifically removes this assumption.

**ℓ₁ Regularized GTD (Dann et al., arXiv 2016, arXiv:1610.01476)**: Applies L1 regularization to GTD algorithms, but the motivation is feature selection/sparsity in large feature sets, not handling singular FIM. No analysis of singular FIM is provided. Delta: Different motivation; R-GTD uses L2 (Tikhonov-type) regularization targeted at the singularity issue.

**Backstepping Temporal Difference Learning (Lim et al., 2023)**: A control-theoretic approach that addresses instability in off-policy TD. Complementary perspective; neither approach subsumes the other.

No prior work found that specifically addresses GTD convergence under singular FIM with formal guarantees.

### Archaeology Pair

**Older anchor**: Sutton, Maei, Precup, Bhatnagar, Silver, Szepesvári, Wiewiora, "Fast gradient-descent methods for temporal-difference learning with linear function approximation," ICML 2009. This paper introduced GTD2, the direct precursor. R-GTD is a regularized extension of GTD2 that removes GTD2's foundational assumption (nonsingular FIM) while preserving its saddle-point structure.

**Newer/concurrent work**: Ghiassian, Patterson, Garg, Gupta, White, White, "Gradient Temporal-Difference Learning with Regularized Corrections" (TDRC), ICML 2020. This is the most relevant concurrent-era work involving regularization in GTD. While TDRC was published 5+ years before the submission, it represents the closest prior art involving regularization and is already cited. No work more recent (2023–2025) directly targeting singular FIM in GTD could be found after searching arXiv, Semantic Scholar, and Google Scholar. This absence is mild evidence for a genuine gap.

### Forward Leverage

The paper opens a concrete research direction: finite-time convergence bounds for R-GTD (acknowledged in the conclusion as future work). Currently only asymptotic convergence is proved; a non-asymptotic rate would make R-GTD practically deployable in settings with bounded sample budgets. Additionally, the regularized saddle-point formulation could be extended to GTD with nonlinear function approximation, e.g., neural network value function approximators, by applying similar Tikhonov regularization to the inner optimization. These are non-trivial extensions that depend specifically on the regularized formulation introduced here.

### Justification

The paper addresses a real and underexplored gap: the lack of convergence guarantees for GTD2-style algorithms when the feature interaction matrix is singular. The regularization strategy itself is conceptually natural — Tikhonov regularization of a singular system is a classical numerical technique — but applying it to MSPBE via a saddle-point reformulation and rigorously proving convergence (with explicit error bounds characterizing the regularization bias) constitutes a genuine theoretical contribution. The analysis carefully distinguishes both the singular and nonsingular regimes, and the error bounds give practitioners actionable guidance on how large a regularization parameter can be before the solution deviates meaningfully from the projected solution.

The novelty is moderate rather than substantial because: (a) the core technique (adding L2 regularization to handle singularity) is standard in optimization and numerical analysis; (b) regularization in GTD methods was already explored by TDRC (2020), even if for a different reason; and (c) the experimental scope is modest (primarily Baird's counterexample and tabular MDPs). The paper does not claim transformative novelty — it fills a specific and legitimate theoretical gap. This is what most solid ICML theory papers look like.
