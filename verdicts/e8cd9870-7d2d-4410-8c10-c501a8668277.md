# Verdict: Quality-Diversity Optimization as Multi-Objective Optimization

## Paper Claim Summary

The paper reformulates Quality-Diversity (QD) optimization — which seeks diverse high-performing solutions covering a behavior space — as a massive set-based Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO) problem with M objectives (one per densely-sampled target behavior), enabling direct adoption of set-based scalarization techniques (SoM, TCH-Set and smooth variants SSoM, STCH-Set) to solve QD through collaborative search.

## What the Committee Verified

The 36-subagent deliberation committee ran five full rounds (R1 independent assessment, R2 cross-reading, R3 three-turn disagreement panels on four contested findings, R4 adversarial pass, R5 calibration audit) before issuing this review. The following findings survived the full deliberation process:

**The reformulation is genuinely novel.** No prior work connects QD to many-objective MOO via set-based scalarization. The paper correctly attributes the underlying machinery (SoM: Ding et al. 2024; TCH-Set/STCH-Set: Lin et al. ICML 2024). The SSoM and STCH-Set variants are principled extensions enabling gradient-based optimization.

**The theoretical framework has three material defects.** (1) The objective function ṽ_m(x) = −f(x) · exp(−||b_m − b(x)||²/γ²) requires f(x) > 0 as an unstated load-bearing assumption; when violated, all five theorems become vacuous or undefined. The LSI benchmark produces negative mean objectives, and SoM/TCH-Set fail catastrophically (QVS = 0.0, Table 2) — yet the paper never connects this failure to the hidden assumption. (2) Theorem 1's TCH-Set monotonicity claim exceeds its proof: the appendix proof covers only equal reference points (z₁* = ⋯ = z_M*), not the general case stated in the theorem. (3) Theorem 2's "supermodularity" label is wrong under the paper's minimization convention — the inequality direction corresponds to submodularity (diminishing returns).

**The bibliography has integrity failures.** Three references (liu2024many, liu2025few, maus2025multi) cannot be verified in standard academic databases. TCH-Set is misattributed to "Lin et al., ICLR 2025" but should be "Lin et al., ICML 2024" (lin2024smooth). These are not peripheral — they undermine the paper's scholarly infrastructure.

**The abstract's "competitive with SoTA" claim is overstated.** SQUAD beats SSoM/STCH-Set on the primary QVS metric in 2 of 3 benchmarks (IC: 457 vs 319; LSI base: 177 vs 99). "Competitive" implies rough parity the data does not show.

## What Survived Adversarial and Rebuttal Passes

The `adversarial` subagent argued the negative-quality failure mode is overstated — SoM/TCH-Set are baselines/ablations, not proposed methods, and SSoM/STCH-Set succeed on all benchmarks. This is valid and the verdict score has been adjusted accordingly. The `author-rebuttal-simulator` raised a credible counter on gradient inversion: the exponential decay term ensures the gradient with respect to b(x) always points toward target behaviors regardless of f(x) sign. This rebuttal is valid for the gradient direction but does not rescue the theorems — the scalarization value guarantees still fail under f(x) < 0. The R3 panel on the f(x) > 0 assumption unanimously concluded the concern is real but its severity is mitigated by the fact that the proposed methods (SSoM, STCH-Set) do not exhibit the failure in practice.

## Final Verdict

**ICML 3 (Weak Reject).** The reformulation concept is a genuine contribution worthy of publication at a venue with a more forgiving bibliography standard, and conditional acceptance with mandatory revisions would be appropriate. However, the cumulative weight of the unstated theoretical assumption, the two material theorem defects, the three likely hallucinated references and one misattribution, and the systematic overstatement of empirical claims collectively justify rejection at ICML 2026. The reformulation itself is real and valuable; what fails is the scholarly reporting, not the core idea.
